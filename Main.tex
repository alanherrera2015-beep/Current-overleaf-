\textbf{Module Learning Objectives} (connected to the course learning objective: \emph{Interactions in communities}).
After completing this module you are expected to answer the following questions so that someone with an
undergraduate Biology knowledge can fully understand your answers.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Describe all types of interspecific interactions.
  \item Giving examples, describe how a niche of a species forms with biotic and abiotic factors of its environment.  Compare fundamental and realized niche.
  \item Compare intraspecific and interspecific interactions.
  \item Describe different types of competition avoidance in nature.
  \item Explain how the Lotka--Volterra model helps understand if two competing species would co-exist in a habitat.
  \item Describe major symbiotic interactions with examples.
  \item Describe different types of ecological/community successions.
  \item Demonstrate the use of the Shannon--Weiner index to measure biodiversity.
\end{itemize}

% ── Section 3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q2 --- Niche: Biotic, Abiotic and Evolutionary Factors]

\textit{Biotic factors} (any three): interspecific interactions including coevolution; diet and foraging behaviour; trophic position; life history timing; morphological specialisations; habitat specialisations.

\textit{Abiotic factors} (any three): temperature range tolerance; oxygen/CO$_2$ levels; pH; salinity; diurnal and seasonal light cycles; substrate type; nutrient availability.

\textit{Multidimensional niche:} Each environmental variable is one axis; the organism's tolerable range on every axis together defines an $n$-dimensional hypervolume representing all conditions under which the species can persist.

\textbf{Ecological niche:} The persistence/role of an individual, population, or species based on the \emph{totality of environmental factors} at a given locale.

\textbf{Evolutionary niche:} The fitness/phenotype-changing \emph{selection pressures} affecting a population in a given environment (i.e., how the environment shapes the evolution of traits).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 4 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q3 --- Fundamental vs.\ Realized Niche]

\textbf{Fundamental niche:} The full range of conditions and resources under which a species \emph{could} survive and reproduce if there were no biotic limiting factors; primarily determined by \textbf{abiotic} factors.

\textbf{Realized niche:} The actual subset of conditions the species occupies in the wild, after accounting for biotic interactions (predators, competitors, parasites, disease); primarily determined by \textbf{biotic} factors.

\textit{Example biotic factor:} A superior competitor excludes the focal species from part of its temperature range, reducing its realized niche.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 5 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q4 --- Intraspecific vs.\ Interspecific Competition]

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
\begin{tabular}{|p{4cm}|p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|}
\hline
& \textbf{Intraspecific} & \textbf{Interspecific} \\
\hline
Individuals involved & Same species & Different species \\
\hline
Typical intensity & Often higher (identical resource needs) & Varies; depends on niche overlap \\
\hline
Main evolutionary consequence & Natural selection; can drive niche expansion & Character displacement; competitive exclusion or coexistence \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Scenario answers: (a) IA \quad (b) IE \quad (c) IE

\textbf{By mechanism:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Interference competition (d):} Two species directly interact (fight, display, produce toxins) for the resource.  Example: two male deer fighting for territory.
  \item \textbf{Exploitative competition (e):} One species uses the resource, making it unavailable for the other species without direct interaction.  Example: two plant species both absorbing the same soil nutrients.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{By outcome:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Contest competition (f):} Only one or a few individuals acquire the resource; clear winner/loser scenario (e.g., territorial defence).
  \item \textbf{Scramble competition (g):} All competitors attempt to acquire the resource; everyone obtains a share, but possibly insufficient amounts (e.g., many seedlings competing for limited light).
\end{itemize}
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 6 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q5 --- Competition Avoidance]

\textbf{Mechanism 1 --- Resource partitioning:} Species divide up a resource in time, space, or type so that direct competition is reduced.
\textit{Example:} Five warbler species partition the vertical zones of a spruce tree (Bay-breasted uses lower branches; Cape May uses the crown, etc.).
Does it affect morphology? Yes --- can lead to character displacement.

\textbf{Mechanism 2 --- Character displacement:} When two species co-occur, natural selection drives their morphologies (e.g., beak depth) further apart, reducing niche overlap and competition.
\textit{Example:} Darwin's finch species on the same Gal\'{a}pagos island have more different bill sizes than populations of the same species on islands where they occur alone.

\textbf{Competitive exclusion principle:} Two species occupying exactly the same ecological niche cannot coexist indefinitely; one will competitively exclude the other.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 7 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q6 --- Lotka--Volterra Competition Model]

(a) $K_1$, $K_2$ = carrying capacities; $\alpha$, $\beta$ = interspecific competition coefficients.

(b) Species 1 wins when $K_1 > K_2/\beta$ \emph{and} $K_1/\alpha > K_2$ (Species 1's isocline lies entirely outside Species 2's isocline).

(c) Stable coexistence occurs when each species inhibits itself more than it inhibits the other species: $K_1 < K_2/\beta$ \emph{and} $K_2 < K_1/\alpha$ (isoclines cross with a stable equilibrium point).

(d) $N_2$-intercept $= K_1/\alpha = 200/0.5 = \mathbf{400}$.

(e) When the two ZNGIs cross, there is an equilibrium point at which both species can potentially coexist; stability depends on the slopes (i.e., the relative magnitudes of intra- vs.\ interspecific competition).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

(a) Pioneer communities: \textbf{high} GCP; \textbf{low} biodiversity.\\
(b) Climax community: $P \approx R$ (production $\approx$ respiration); biomass accumulates \textbf{very slowly}.\\
(c) \textit{Microsuccession (serule):} A decomposing log undergoes a predictable series of microbial and invertebrate communities; each community alters the substrate (temperature, moisture, chemistry), enabling the next community.  The log is eventually decomposed, eliminating the microhabitat without reaching a climax.

% ── Section 10 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q9 --- Shannon--Weiner Diversity Index]

(a) Both communities: $S = 4$ species.

(b) \textbf{Community A:} $p_1=p_2=0.40$, $p_3=p_4=0.10$
\begin{align*}
H_A &= -[2(0.40\ln 0.40) + 2(0.10\ln 0.10)]\\
    &= -[2(-0.3665) + 2(-0.2303)]\\
    &= -[-0.7330 - 0.4606]\\
    &\approx \mathbf{1.19}
\end{align*}

(c) \textbf{Community B:} $p_1=p_2=0.10$, $p_3=0.70$, $p_4=0.10$
\begin{align*}
H_B &= -[3(0.10\ln 0.10) + 0.70\ln 0.70]\\
    &= -[3(-0.2303) + (-0.2497)]\\
    &= -[-0.6909 - 0.2497]\\
    &\approx \mathbf{0.94}
\end{align*}

(d) \textbf{Community A} has higher evenness; its species are distributed more equally
    (40:40:10:10) compared to Community B (10:10:70:10), where one species dominates.

(e) \textit{Species evenness} is the relative homogeneity (equitability) of individuals
    among species --- how similar the proportions of different species are in a community.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 11 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q10 --- Biodiversity in Communities]

(a) \textbf{Dominant species:} The species that is most abundant or makes up the greatest biomass in a community.
Example: Beech--maple forest (named for the dominant trees); mussel zone in an intertidal community.

(b) \textbf{Keystone species:} Has effects on community structure and species diversity far larger than its abundance would predict; often controls populations of other species or maintains habitat structure.
Example: Sea otter (controls sea urchin populations, thereby maintaining kelp forests).

(c) \textbf{Ecological engineer:} Beaver --- builds dams that create wetland habitats, alter water flow, and increase habitat diversity for many species.

(d) Ranked highest to lowest: Tropical rainforest (1) $\approx$ Coral reef (2) $>$ Estuary (3) $>$ Arctic tundra (4) $>$ Agricultural land (5).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 12 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q11 --- Predator--Prey Relationships]

(a) Predator and prey populations \textbf{oscillate out of phase with} each other.
When hare numbers are high, lynx numbers subsequently \textbf{increase} because abundant prey supports higher predator survival and reproduction.

(b) Defense matching:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Mechanical defense: \textbf{C} (honey locust thorns)
  \item Chemical defense: \textbf{D} (foxglove cardiac glycosides) [also acceptable: B]
  \item Cryptic coloration: \textbf{A} (stick insect)
  \item Aposematic coloration: \textbf{B} (monarch butterfly --- also chemical; the bright orange coloration warns predators)
\end{itemize}

(c) \textbf{Batesian mimicry:} A harmless species (mimic) resembles a dangerous or unpalatable species (model) to deter predators; the mimic gains protection but the model does not benefit.
\textbf{M\"{u}llerian mimicry:} Two or more genuinely unpalatable/dangerous species resemble each other; predators learn to avoid the shared warning pattern faster, benefiting all co-models.
\textbf{Mertensian mimicry:} A deadly/highly dangerous species (the mimic) resembles a less dangerous but aversive species (the model), because predators must survive a non-lethal encounter with the model to \emph{learn} to avoid the warning pattern; if the mimic were the only species encountered, predators could not survive to learn.  Example: a highly venomous coral snake species (mimic) converges in coloration on a moderately venomous coral snake species (model) that predators can survive and learn to avoid.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 13 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q12 --- Community Equilibrium and Trophic Cascades]

(a) A \textbf{trophic cascade} is a powerful indirect interaction that propagates through multiple trophic levels when one component of a community changes.  The Yellowstone example is a \textbf{top-down} cascade (apex predator suppresses herbivore, which releases vegetation).

(b) Cause-and-effect chain:
Wolf reintroduced $\rightarrow$ elk/bison populations \textbf{decrease} $\rightarrow$ overgrazing \textbf{reduced} $\rightarrow$ riparian vegetation \textbf{increases} $\rightarrow$ river banks stabilise $\rightarrow$ fish \& bird diversity \textbf{increases}.

(c) \textbf{Community stability} (or \emph{ecological resilience}).

(d) Example: \textit{Agricultural land clearing} removes native vegetation and biodiversity, simplifies food chains, disrupts nutrient cycling, and increases community instability (e.g., pest outbreaks, soil erosion).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

(b) Conventional monocultures: \textbf{low} biodiversity; \textbf{unstable} ecologically.  Eco-agriculture increases biodiversity through \textbf{crop rotation}, \textbf{intercropping/multiple cropping}, and \textbf{agroforestry}.

(c) N-fixing cover crops add biologically fixed nitrogen to the soil (via root nodules with \textit{Rhizobium} or free-living bacteria), reducing the need for synthetic fertilisers and improving soil fertility for subsequent crops.

\textbf{Concept 23 (Four L-V outcomes):}
(1)~Species~1 always wins ($K_1 > K_2/\beta$ and $K_1/\alpha > K_2$);
(2)~Species~2 always wins ($K_2 > K_1/\alpha$ and $K_2/\beta > K_1$);
(3)~Stable coexistence (intraspecific $>$ interspecific for both);
(4)~Unstable equilibrium / priority effects (each species wins when it starts at a higher density).

\textbf{Concept 56 (River geomorphology):}
Wolf predation keeps elk from lingering near riverbanks $\Rightarrow$ willows and aspens regenerate on banks $\Rightarrow$ roots stabilize soil $\Rightarrow$ rivers straighten and deepen. This is called the \textbf{geomorphic trophic cascade} (``how wolves change rivers'').
