\section{Chromosome Organization}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Introduction to Chromosomes and the Genome}

\begin{diagbox}{1. Introduction to Chromosomes and the Genome}

\textbf{Chromosomes} are structures that contain the genetic material.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item They are complexes of \textbf{DNA and proteins}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
The \textbf{genome} comprises \emph{all} the genetic material that an organism possesses.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Bacterial genome:} a single circular chromosome.
  \item \textbf{Eukaryotic genome:} a complete set of nuclear chromosomes.
  \item Eukaryotes also possess a \textbf{mitochondrial genome}.
  \item Plants additionally possess a \textbf{chloroplast genome}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Main function} of genetic material: store the information required to produce an organism.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Organization of Eukaryotic Chromosomes}

\begin{diagbox}{2. Organization of Eukaryotic Chromosomes}

Eukaryotic species contain \textbf{one or more sets of chromosomes} (several different linear chromosomes).
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Humans have \textbf{2 sets of 23 chromosomes} (46 total in the diploid genome: 6{,}200~Mbp).
  \item Each chromosome contains a \textbf{single, linear molecule of DNA} (tens to hundreds of millions of bp; a few hundred to several thousand genes).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Lower eukaryotes} (e.g.\ yeast, \textit{Saccharomyces cerevisiae}):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Genes are relatively small; few, short introns.
  \item Genome $\approx$ 12~Mbp, organised in 16 chromosomes.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Higher eukaryotes} (e.g.\ mammals):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Genes are long and tend to have many introns (100 to $>$10{,}000~bp).
  \item Diploid human genome: 6{,}200~Mbp in 46 chromosomes.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Four essential chromosomal elements:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Origins of replication} --- interspersed every $\sim$100{,}000~bp.
  \item \textbf{Centromere} --- recognition site for kinetochore proteins.
  \item \textbf{Telomeres} --- specialised sequences at both ends of the linear chromosome.
  \item \textbf{Repetitive sequences} --- near centromeric/telomeric regions and throughout.
\end{enumerate}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Genome Sizes and Repetitive Sequences}

\begin{diagbox}{3. Genome Sizes and Repetitive Sequences}

\textbf{Genome size} is \emph{not} simply related to organismal complexity (C-value paradox).
Eukaryotic species generally have more DNA than bacteria but vary substantially among themselves.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Difference in size is due to repetitive DNA, not extra genes.}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Three categories of repetitive sequences:}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5cm}|p{5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Category} & \textbf{Copy Number} & \textbf{Examples} \\
\hline
Unique (non-repetitive) & Once or a few times & Structural genes, intergenic regions; 41\% of human genome \\
\hline
Moderately repetitive & Hundreds to thousands & rRNA genes, histones, regulatory sequences, transposable elements \\
\hline
Highly repetitive & Tens of thousands to millions & Short sequences (a few to several hundred nt each) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Chromosome Territories}

\begin{diagbox}{4. Chromosome Territories}

During interphase, chromosomes occupy \textbf{distinct regions (territories)} within the nucleus.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Particular chromosomes preferentially occupy specific nuclear regions.
  \item This spatial organisation is non-random and influences gene regulation.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

\begin{diagbox}{5. Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

The DNA--protein complex is called \textbf{chromatin}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Nucleosome} --- the repeating structural unit of eukaryotic chromatin:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Histone octamer:} two copies each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4 (core histones).
  \item \textbf{146~bp} of DNA wrapped $\sim$1.65 times around the octamer.
  \item \textbf{Linker histone H1} binds DNA in the linker region between nucleosomes.
  \item Non-histone proteins also associate with chromatin.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Five types of histones:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item H2A, H2B, H3, H4 --- \emph{core histones} (two of each per octamer).
  \item H1 --- \emph{linker histone} (binds between nucleosomes).
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Heterochromatin versus Euchromatin}

\begin{diagbox}{6. Heterochromatin versus Euchromatin}

The compaction level of interphase chromosomes is \emph{not} completely uniform.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5.5cm}|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Heterochromatin} & \textbf{Euchromatin} \\
\hline
Compaction & Tightly compacted & Less condensed \\
\hline
Transcription & Inactive (in general) & Transcriptionally active \\
\hline
Protein access & Little to no access & DNA accessible to proteins \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Levels of Chromatin Compaction}

\begin{diagbox}{7. Levels of Chromatin Compaction Leading to a Metaphase Chromosome}

Eukaryotic chromosomal DNA undergoes successive levels of compaction:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{2~nm} --- DNA double helix.
  \item \textbf{11~nm} --- DNA wrapping around nucleosomes (``beads on a string'').
  \item \textbf{30~nm} --- Zig-zag (solenoid) structure via histone H1 and non-histone proteins.
  \item \textbf{300~nm} --- Radial loop domains: MARs (matrix attachment regions) bind the nuclear matrix.
  \item \textbf{700~nm} --- Radial loops more compacted.
  \item \textbf{1{,}400~nm} --- Metaphase chromosome; protein filaments (scaffold) give the chromosome its shape.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{DNase~I} cuts chromatin into repeating units containing $\sim$200~bp of DNA, demonstrating the nucleosomal repeat unit.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Condensin and Cohesin}

\begin{diagbox}{8. Condensin and Cohesin}

Two multiprotein complexes organise metaphase chromosomes, both containing \textbf{SMC proteins} (Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes) that use ATP energy to alter chromosome structure.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5.5cm}|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Complex} & \textbf{Timing} & \textbf{Function} \\
\hline
Condensin & Cytoplasmic in interphase; enters nucleus at mitosis & Chromosome condensation; compacts radial loops \\
\hline
Cohesin & Present during S phase onward & Sister chromatid cohesion; also regulates transcription \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Cornelia de Lange Syndrome (CdLS; MIM~122470):} caused by mutations in cohesin complex genes.
Hallmarks: craniofacial dysmorphisms (synophrys, arched eyebrows, thin downturned upper lip, long philtrum), hirsutism,
intellectual disability, microcephaly, growth retardation, and limb anomalies (micromelia, oligodactyly, phocomelia).

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Bacterial Chromosome Organization}

\begin{diagbox}{9. Bacterial Chromosome Organization}

Bacterial chromosomal DNA is typically a \textbf{circular molecule}.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textit{Escherichia coli}: $\sim$4.6~million bp.
  \item \textit{Haemophilus influenzae}: $\sim$1.8~million bp.
  \item Contains thousands of genes, mostly protein-encoding (\textbf{structural genes}).
  \item Non-transcribed DNA between genes = \textbf{intergenic regions}.
  \item \textbf{One origin of replication.}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{The nucleoid:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Region of the cell where the bacterial chromosome resides.
  \item \emph{Not} bounded by a membrane (no nucleus).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Compaction via loop domains:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item DNA must be compacted $\sim$1{,}000-fold to fit inside the cell.
  \item \textit{E.\ coli} forms 50--100 loop domains, each containing 40{,}000--80{,}000~bp.
  \item Micro-domain number varies with chromosome size and species.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Introduction to Biotechnology}

\begin{diagbox}{10. Introduction to Biotechnology}

\textbf{Biotechnology:} technologies that involve the use of living organisms,
or their products, to benefit humans.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Common uses of microorganisms:}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{8cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Application} & \textbf{Examples} \\
\hline
Production of medicines & Antibiotics, vitamins; human insulin in recombinant \textit{E.\ coli} \\
\hline
Food fermentation & Cheese, yogurt, vinegar, wine, beer \\
\hline
Biological control & Plant disease, insect pest, weed control \\
\hline
Symbiotic nitrogen fixation & Prevention of frost formation \\
\hline
Bioremediation & Cleanup of petroleum hydrocarbons and synthetic pollutants \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Human insulin:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A hormone consisting of two polypeptide chains (A and B).
  \item Regulates glucose uptake into fat and muscle cells; produced by $\beta$~cells of the pancreas.
  \item Persons with insulin-dependent diabetes cannot synthesise enough insulin.
  \item Synthesised in recombinant \textit{E.\ coli} for therapeutic use.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference}

\begin{diagbox}{11. Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference (RNAi)}

\textbf{Gene knockouts (KO):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Used to study how loss of gene function affects an organism.
  \item The genomic DNA is perturbed to \emph{permanently prevent} expression of a specific gene.
  \item No mRNA or protein product from the knocked-out gene.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{RNA interference (RNAi) --- gene knockdown:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) results in \textbf{sequence-specific gene silencing}.
  \item The target mRNA is degraded post-transcriptionally.
  \item The gene is still present; mRNA is made but degraded.
  \item Useful for transient, post-transcriptional knockdown.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Knockout} & \textbf{Knockdown (RNAi)} \\
\hline
Gene present? & No & Yes \\
mRNA produced? & No & Yes (then degraded) \\
Protein produced? & No & No (or reduced) \\
Permanence & Permanent & Transient \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gene Cloning}

\begin{diagbox}{12. Gene Cloning}

\textbf{Gene cloning:} isolating and producing many copies of a gene.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Two DNA molecules required:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Chromosomal (genomic) DNA} --- source of the gene of interest.
  \item \textbf{Vector (plasmid) DNA} --- carrier for the insert; replicates independently of host chromosome.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Vector features (example: pMSH6-MYC):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Bacterial origin of replication} (\textit{ori}) for replication in bacteria.
  \item \textbf{Antibiotic resistance gene} (e.g.\ ampicillin resistance --- \textit{amp}$^R$) for selection.
  \item \textbf{ARSH4 + CEN6} for replication and maintenance in yeast.
  \item \textbf{Leucine marker} ($LEU2$) for yeast selection in leucine-deficient medium.
  \item \textbf{Shuttle vector:} can replicate in both bacteria and yeast.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Steps in gene cloning:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Extract and purify genomic DNA from the organism of interest.
  \item Digest both chromosomal DNA and vector with the same restriction enzyme.
  \item Ligate the insert into the vector $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{recombinant vector}.
  \item Transform \textbf{competent cells} (cells capable of taking up DNA).
  \item Select transformants on antibiotic plates.
  \item Identify recombinant clones by \textbf{blue-white screening} (lacZ).
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Blue-white screening (LacZ):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Blue colonies:} vector re-circularised without insert; lacZ intact; $\beta$-galactosidase cleaves X-gal $\Rightarrow$ blue pigment.
  \item \textbf{White colonies:} insert disrupts lacZ $\Rightarrow$ no $\beta$-galactosidase activity $\Rightarrow$ no blue pigment.
  \item Select \emph{white} colonies to find recombinant plasmids.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Amplification:} a single bacterium harbouring a plasmid gives rise to millions of cells overnight ($\sim$25--50 plasmid copies per cell; division every 20~min).

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Restriction Enzymes}

\begin{diagbox}{13. Restriction Enzymes}

\textbf{Restriction endonucleases (restriction enzymes):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Naturally produced by many bacteria to protect against foreign DNA (bacteriophages).
  \item Cleave DNA at specific \textbf{palindromic recognition sequences}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Palindromic sequences:} the sequence on one strand (read 5$'\!\to\!$3$'$) is identical to the complementary strand read in the opposite direction.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{EcoRI example:}
\[
  5'\text{-}\underbrace{\texttt{G\,A\,A\,T\,T\,C}}_{\text{EcoRI site}}\text{-}3'
  \qquad
  3'\text{-}\underbrace{\texttt{C\,T\,T\,A\,A\,G}}_{}  \text{-}5'
\]
Cuts between G and A on each strand, generating \textbf{sticky (cohesive) ends}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Generating compatible ends:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Both insert and vector digested with the same enzyme $\Rightarrow$ complementary overhangs.
  \item DNA ligase seals the nick to form a \textbf{recombinant molecule}.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\begin{diagbox}{14. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\textbf{PCR:} a method to amplify a specific DNA sequence \emph{without} vectors or host cells.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Required starting materials:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Template DNA} --- contains the region to be amplified.
  \item \textbf{Two oligonucleotide primers} ($\sim$15--20~nt) complementary to each end of the target.
  \item \textbf{Deoxynucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs)} --- precursors for DNA synthesis.
  \item \textbf{Taq polymerase} --- thermostable DNA polymerase from \textit{Thermus aquaticus}; survives high-temperature denaturation steps.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{The three steps of each PCR cycle:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Denaturation} ($\sim$94--96$^\circ$C) --- strands separate.
  \item \textbf{Annealing} ($\sim$50--65$^\circ$C) --- primers bind to complementary template.
  \item \textbf{Extension/Synthesis} ($\sim$72$^\circ$C) --- Taq extends from each primer.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Amplification:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item 20 cycles $\Rightarrow$ $2^{20} \approx 1{,}000{,}000$-fold increase.
  \item 30 cycles $\Rightarrow$ $2^{30} \approx 1{,}000{,}000{,}000$-fold increase.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Site-Directed Mutagenesis}

\begin{diagbox}{15. Site-Directed Mutagenesis}

\textbf{Site-directed mutagenesis:} alters the sequence of a cloned gene at a \emph{specific, defined position}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Denature the vector + insert to single-stranded DNA.
  \item Anneal a synthetic primer containing the desired mutation (creates a mismatch).
  \item Extend to produce a heteroduplex containing one mutant strand.
  \item Transform into bacteria; identify mutant by DNA sequencing.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Uses:} study how specific mutations affect:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Gene expression.
  \item Protein function.
  \item Organismal phenotype.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting Techniques}

\begin{diagbox}{16. Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting Techniques}

\textbf{Gel electrophoresis} separates DNA (or RNA/protein) by size in an electric field.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Negatively charged DNA migrates from the negative electrode (cathode) toward the positive electrode (anode) (``run to red!'').
  \item Smaller fragments migrate faster through the gel matrix.
  \item \textbf{Ethidium bromide} (or safer alternatives) intercalates into DNA and fluoresces under UV light.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Topology and migration rate (slowest to fastest):}
\[
  \text{Nicked circle} > \text{Linear} > \text{Supercoiled}
\]

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Detects protein--DNA interactions.
  \item A protein bound to a labelled DNA fragment slows its migration (``shift'').
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Western blot:} detects a specific protein.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Proteins separated by SDS-PAGE.
  \item Transferred to nylon membrane.
  \item Primary antibody binds the target protein.
  \item Secondary antibody (linked to alkaline phosphatase) binds the primary antibody.
  \item Addition of XP (5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl phosphate) $\Rightarrow$ dark purple band.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Northern blot:} detects a specific RNA (mRNA).
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item mRNA isolated and separated by gel electrophoresis.
  \item Blotted to nylon membrane.
  \item Hybridised with a labelled DNA probe complementary to the target mRNA.
  \item Labelled bands appear where complementary mRNA is present.
\end{enumerate}

\end{diagbox}

\section{Gene Regulation in Eukaryotes}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Why Gene Regulation Is Necessary}

\begin{diagbox}{17. Why Gene Regulation Is Necessary}

Gene regulation is necessary to ensure:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Expression of genes in an accurate pattern} during the various developmental stages of the life cycle.
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Some genes are only expressed during \textbf{embryonic stages}; others only in the adult.
  \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Differences among distinct cell types.}
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item Nerve and muscle cells look different because of \textbf{gene regulation}, not differences in DNA content.
    \item All somatic cells carry the same genome; cell identity arises from which genes are expressed.
  \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Levels at which eukaryotic gene expression can be regulated:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Transcriptional regulation (most common and best studied)
  \item RNA processing (splicing, capping, polyadenylation)
  \item RNA stability / degradation
  \item Translational regulation
  \item Post-translational modification
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Transcription Factors}

\begin{diagbox}{18. Transcription Factors}

\textbf{Transcription factors} are proteins that influence the ability of RNA polymerase to transcribe a given gene.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Two main types:}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{4.5cm}|p{5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Type} & \textbf{Examples} & \textbf{Function} \\
\hline
General (basal) transcription factors & TFIIB, TFIID, TFIIE, TFIIF, TFIIH & Required for binding of RNA pol~II to the core promoter and progression to elongation; necessary for \textbf{basal transcription} \\
\hline
Regulatory transcription factors & Activators, repressors & Regulate the \textbf{rate} of transcription of target genes; influence ability of RNA pol to begin transcription of a specific gene; 2--3\% of human genes encode transcription factors \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{TFIID} recognises the \textbf{TATA box} (core promoter element $\sim$25~bp upstream of TSS) via its TBP (TATA-box binding protein) subunit.
The pre-initiation complex (PIC) assembled from general TFs plus RNA pol~II is required for basal transcription.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Regulatory Transcription Factors: Activators and Repressors}

\begin{diagbox}{19. Regulatory Transcription Factors}

\textbf{Gene activation:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A regulatory protein that \textbf{increases} the rate of transcription is an \textbf{activator}.
  \item The DNA sequence an activator binds is called an \textbf{enhancer}.
  \item Enhancers can be located thousands of bp upstream, downstream, or within introns.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Gene repression:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A regulatory protein that \textbf{decreases} the rate of transcription is a \textbf{repressor}.
  \item The DNA sequence a repressor binds is called a \textbf{silencer}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Domain structure of regulatory transcription factors:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{DNA-binding domain} --- recognises and binds specific DNA sequences (motifs: helix-turn-helix, zinc finger, leucine zipper, helix-loop-helix).
  \item \textbf{Activation (or repression) domain} --- interacts with co-activators/co-repressors or the basal transcription machinery.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Combinatorial Control of Gene Expression}

\begin{diagbox}{20. Combinatorial Control}

\textbf{Combinatorial control} refers to the cooperative action of multiple regulatory proteins in determining whether a gene is transcribed.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Mechanisms that contribute to combinatorial control:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Multiple activators binding at a single gene's enhancer region} --- additive or synergistic effects on transcription.
  \item \textbf{Regulatory proteins altering nucleosomes} near the promoter --- loosening or tightening chromatin to permit or block RNA pol access.
  \item \textbf{DNA methylation} at CpG islands --- can prevent binding of an activator protein or recruit proteins that compact chromatin.
  \item \textbf{Chromatin compaction} --- recruitment of histone deacetylases or methyltransferases by repressors condenses chromatin.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Key concept:} The same gene can be regulated differently in different cell types depending on which combination of transcription factors is present.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Mediator Complex}

\begin{diagbox}{21. The Mediator Complex}

The \textbf{Mediator} is a large multi-subunit coactivator complex that serves as a \textbf{bridge} between regulatory transcription factors and the basal transcription machinery (RNA pol~II~+ general TFs).

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Activators bound to enhancers contact Mediator subunits.
  \item Mediator in turn contacts the PIC assembled at the core promoter.
  \item \textbf{DNA looping} brings distant enhancers into proximity with the promoter (cohesin facilitates stable loops).
  \item Mediator stimulates phosphorylation of the RNA pol~II C-terminal domain (CTD), triggering promoter clearance and elongation.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Chromatin Remodeling and Histone Modifications}

\begin{diagbox}{22. Chromatin Remodeling and Histone Modifications}

\textbf{ATP-dependent chromatin remodeling complexes} (e.g.\ SWI/SNF) use ATP hydrolysis to:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Slide} nucleosomes along DNA.
  \item \textbf{Eject} nucleosomes, creating nucleosome-free regions (NFRs) at promoters.
  \item \textbf{Exchange} histone variants (e.g.\ H2A.Z).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Covalent histone modifications (histone code):}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Modification} & \textbf{Residue(s)} & \textbf{Effect} \\
\hline
Acetylation (HAT) & H3K9ac, H3K27ac & Neutralises $+$ charge $\Rightarrow$ loosens DNA–histone interaction; marks \textbf{active} chromatin \\
\hline
Deacetylation (HDAC) & H3K9, H3K27 & Restores $+$ charge; promotes chromatin compaction; repression \\
\hline
Methylation & H3K4me3 & Active promoters \\
 & H3K27me3 & Polycomb repression \\
 & H3K9me3 & Constitutive heterochromatin \\
\hline
Phosphorylation & H3S10 & Chromosome condensation during mitosis \\
\hline
Ubiquitylation & H2BK120ub & Elongation; promotes H3K4 methylation \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Epigenetic inheritance:} Some histone modifications are maintained through DNA replication, allowing stable inheritance of gene expression states.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{DNA Methylation}

\begin{diagbox}{23. DNA Methylation}

\textbf{DNA methylation} in mammals occurs predominantly on the \textbf{cytosine} of \textbf{CpG dinucleotides} (forming 5-methylcytosine, 5mC).

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Enzymes:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{DNMT3A / DNMT3B} (de novo methyltransferases) --- establish new methylation patterns.
  \item \textbf{DNMT1} (maintenance methyltransferase) --- copies methylation patterns to the daughter strand after replication (hemi-methylated $\to$ fully methylated).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Consequences of CpG methylation:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Prevents activator binding} at promoters → gene silencing.
  \item \textbf{Recruits methyl-CpG binding proteins} (e.g.\ MeCP2) that attract HDACs → chromatin compaction.
  \item \textbf{CpG islands} (clusters of CpGs near promoters) are usually \emph{unmethylated} at active genes; hypermethylation in cancer silences tumour-suppressor genes.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Genomic imprinting} is a biological process where only one parental allele is expressed; the silenced allele is marked by DNA methylation plus H3K27me3.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Insulator Sequences}

\begin{diagbox}{24. Insulator Sequences}

\textbf{Insulators} (boundary elements) are DNA sequences that \textbf{block} the effect of an enhancer on a gene if placed \textbf{between} them.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Two functions of insulators:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Enhancer blocking} --- prevent an enhancer from activating a gene on the other side of the insulator.
  \item \textbf{Barrier activity} --- prevent the spread of heterochromatin into adjacent active chromatin domains.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{CTCF} (CCCTC-binding factor) is the major vertebrate insulator-binding protein.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item CTCF bound at two sites flanking a gene can form a \textbf{chromatin loop domain}, insulating the gene from surrounding regulatory influences.
  \item Together with \textbf{cohesin}, CTCF organises the genome into \textbf{topologically associating domains (TADs)}.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\section{Gene Regulation in Prokaryotes}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Introduction to Gene Regulation}

\begin{diagbox}{25. Introduction to Gene Regulation in Prokaryotes}

\textbf{Gene regulation} means the level of gene expression can vary under different conditions.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Unregulated (constitutive) genes:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Constant levels of expression.
  \item Encode proteins continuously necessary for organism survival.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Why regulate genes?} Encoded proteins are produced \emph{only when required}, saving cellular resources.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Gene regulation is important for:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Metabolism} --- enzymes needed to metabolize certain sugars are made only when those sugars are present.
  \item \textbf{Cell division} --- proteins needed for cell division are made only when cells are preparing to divide.
  \item \textbf{Stress response} --- proteins produced when bacteria encounter heat shock or osmotic shock.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Levels at which gene expression can be regulated:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Transcriptional level} (most common in bacteria)
  \item \textbf{Translational level}
  \item \textbf{Post-transcriptional level} (e.g.\ mRNA stability and degradation rate)
  \item \textbf{Post-translational level}
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Two main types of regulatory proteins act at the transcriptional level:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Protein} & \textbf{Binds} & \textbf{Effect} & \textbf{Type of control} \\
\midrule
Repressor & DNA & Inhibits transcription & Negative control \\
Activator & DNA & Increases transcription & Positive control \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Effector molecules} bind to regulatory proteins but not directly to DNA.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Inducers} --- bind activators or block repressors $\Rightarrow$ increase transcription.
  \item \textbf{Corepressors} --- bind repressors and enhance repression $\Rightarrow$ decrease transcription.
  \item \textbf{Inhibitors} --- block activators $\Rightarrow$ decrease transcription.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{The lac Operon: Structure and Components}

\begin{diagbox}{26. The lac Operon: Structure and Components}

\textbf{Operon:} a regulatory unit consisting of a cluster of structural genes under the control of a single promoter.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{DNA regulatory elements of the lac operon:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Promoter (lacP)} --- RNA polymerase binding site.
  \item \textbf{Operator (lacO)} --- binding site for the lac repressor protein.
  \item \textbf{CAP site} --- binding site for Catabolite Activator Protein (CAP).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Structural genes and their protein products:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{lacZ} --- encodes $\beta$-galactosidase.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Cleaves lactose into glucose + galactose.
      \item Also converts lactose to \textbf{allolactose} (an isomer; the true inducer).
    \end{itemize}
  \item \textbf{lacY} --- encodes lactose permease (membrane protein for lactose transport).
  \item \textbf{lacA} --- encodes galactoside transacetylase (prevents toxic build-up by attaching acetyl groups).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{The lacI gene:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \emph{Not} considered part of the lac operon; has its own promoter (the \textit{i} promoter).
  \item Constitutively expressed at low levels.
  \item Encodes the \textbf{lac repressor protein} (only a small amount is needed).
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Negative Control: The lac Repressor}

\begin{diagbox}{27. Negative Control: The lac Repressor (Inducible)}

The lac operon uses \textbf{inducible, negative control}:

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{No lactose present (operon OFF):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item No inducer (allolactose) is present.
  \item Lac repressor binds the operator (lacO).
  \item RNA polymerase cannot transcribe the operon $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{operon repressed}.
  \item Result: lac proteins are not synthesized (no lactose to process).
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{Lactose present (operon ON):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A small amount of lactose enters and is converted to \textbf{allolactose} by $\beta$-galactosidase.
  \item Allolactose binds to the lac repressor $\Rightarrow$ repressor changes conformation.
  \item Repressor can no longer bind the operator.
  \item RNA polymerase transcribes the operon $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{operon induced}.
  \item lacZ, lacY, lacA proteins are synthesized to metabolize lactose.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.2cm}
\textbf{The cycle of induction and repression:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Lactose is depleted $\Rightarrow$ allolactose decreases $\Rightarrow$ allolactose released from repressor $\Rightarrow$ repressor binds operator $\Rightarrow$ operon turned off.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Important lac mutants:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Mutant} & \textbf{Defect} & \textbf{Phenotype} \\
\midrule
$\text{I}^-$ & No repressor made & Operon always ON (constitutive) \\
$\text{I}^S$ & Super repressor; cannot bind allolactose & Operon always OFF \\
$\text{Z}^-$ & No functional $\beta$-galactosidase & Cannot cleave lactose \\
$\text{Y}^-$ & No functional permease & Cannot import lactose \\
$\text{A}^-$ & No transacetylase & Cannot acetylate galactosides \\
$\text{O}^c$ & Operator mutant; repressor cannot bind & Constitutive transcription \\
$\text{P}^-$ & Promoter mutant; RNA pol binds weakly & Reduced/no transcription \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Merozygote (partial diploid) analysis:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A bacterium receiving an F$'$ factor carrying \textit{lacI}$^+$ will have two copies of the lacI gene.
  \item Even if the chromosome carries \textit{lacI}$^-$, the F$'$-encoded repressor is \textbf{diffusible} and can travel to the operator $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{repressor acts in trans}.
  \item This demonstrates the repressor is a \textbf{trans}-acting, diffusible protein.
  \item The operator (O$^c$) mutant is only constitutive when it is in \textit{cis} with the structural genes, demonstrating the operator acts \textbf{in cis}.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Positive Control: CAP and Catabolite Repression}

\begin{diagbox}{28. Positive Control: CAP and Catabolite Repression}

The lac operon is also regulated by \textbf{catabolite repression} (positive control mechanism).

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Key concept --- diauxic growth:} when lactose and glucose are both present, \textit{E.~coli} uses glucose first. Only when glucose is depleted does catabolite repression lift and the lac operon become fully expressed.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Effector molecule:} \textbf{cyclic AMP (cAMP)}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Enzyme \textbf{adenylyl cyclase} converts ATP $\rightarrow$ cAMP.
  \item Glucose \textbf{inhibits} adenylyl cyclase $\Rightarrow$ low cAMP levels when glucose is present.
  \item When glucose is absent, adenylyl cyclase is active $\Rightarrow$ high cAMP levels.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Catabolite Activator Protein (CAP):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item cAMP binds CAP $\Rightarrow$ cAMP--CAP complex forms.
  \item cAMP--CAP complex binds the \textbf{CAP site} near the lac promoter.
  \item This \textbf{increases transcription} (positive control).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Summary of all combinations:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Glucose} & \textbf{Lactose} & \textbf{cAMP level} & \textbf{Transcription} \\
\midrule
Absent  & Present & High & High (CAP active + repressor off) \\
Absent  & Absent  & High & Low (CAP active, but repressor on) \\
Present & Present & Low  & Low (CAP inactive, repressor off) \\
Present & Absent  & Low  & Very low (CAP inactive + repressor on) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{The trp Operon: Repressible Regulation}

\begin{diagbox}{29. The trp Operon: Repressible Regulation}

The \textbf{trp operon} controls biosynthesis of the amino acid \textbf{tryptophan}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Structural genes:} \textit{trpE}, \textit{trpD}, \textit{trpC}, \textit{trpB}, \textit{trpA} --- encode enzymes for tryptophan biosynthesis.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Regulatory genes:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textit{trpR} --- encodes the trp \textbf{repressor protein} (functions in repression).
  \item \textit{trpL} --- encodes a short \textbf{leader peptide} (functions in attenuation).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Low tryptophan (operon ON):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Tryptophan does not bind to the trp repressor.
  \item Repressor cannot bind the operator.
  \item RNA polymerase transcribes the entire operon.
  \item Tryptophan biosynthesis enzymes are produced.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{High tryptophan (operon OFF --- repressible):}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Tryptophan acts as a \textbf{corepressor} (effector molecule): it binds to the trp repressor.
  \item The tryptophan--repressor complex binds the operator.
  \item Transcription is inhibited.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Contrast with lac operon:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item lac operon: \textbf{inducible} (effector molecule \emph{inactivates} repressor).
  \item trp operon: \textbf{repressible} (effector molecule \emph{activates} repressor).
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Attenuation of the trp Operon}

\begin{diagbox}{30. Attenuation of the trp Operon}

\textbf{Attenuation} is a second mechanism regulating the trp operon that operates \textbf{independently of and in addition to} repression.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Key principle:} In bacteria, transcription and translation are \textbf{coupled} (occur simultaneously). The ribosome translates the mRNA as it is being transcribed.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Mechanism:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item The \textbf{attenuator} is a sequence in the \textit{trpL} (leader) region.
  \item Transcription begins, and the \textit{trpL} leader peptide contains two Trp codons.
  \item The leader mRNA can form alternative stem-loop secondary structures (hairpins):
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Terminator hairpin} (structures 3--4): leads to premature transcription termination (attenuation).
      \item \textbf{Antiterminator hairpin} (structures 2--3): allows transcription to continue.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{High tryptophan:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Tryptophanyl-tRNA (charged) is abundant.
  \item Ribosome translates the leader peptide rapidly, without stalling.
  \item Terminator hairpin forms $\Rightarrow$ transcription terminates at the attenuator $\Rightarrow$ structural genes NOT transcribed.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Low tryptophan:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Tryptophanyl-tRNA (charged) is scarce.
  \item Ribosome stalls at the Trp codons in the leader sequence.
  \item Antiterminator hairpin forms $\Rightarrow$ transcription continues into the structural genes $\Rightarrow$ tryptophan biosynthesis enzymes produced.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Translational Regulation}

\begin{diagbox}{31. Translational Regulation in Bacteria}

Gene expression in bacteria can also be regulated at the \textbf{translational level}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Translational repressors:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Regulatory proteins that recognize specific sequences within the mRNA.
  \item Generally act to \textbf{inhibit translation initiation}.
  \item Two mechanisms:
    \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
      \item Binding near the \textbf{Shine--Dalgarno sequence} and/or start codon $\Rightarrow$ sterically hinders ribosome from initiating translation.
      \item Binding outside the Shine--Dalgarno/start codon region $\Rightarrow$ conformational change in mRNA that also prevents initiation.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Antisense RNA:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A second way to regulate translation.
  \item \textbf{Antisense RNA} is an RNA strand complementary to a strand of mRNA (the ``sense'' strand).
  \item When antisense RNA base-pairs with the mRNA, it prevents ribosome binding and translation.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Osmoregulation in \textit{E.~coli} (antisense RNA example):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{ompF} gene: encodes \textbf{OmpF}, a porin (outer membrane channel protein) that allows passage of small molecules and ions; important for controlling membrane permeability during osmoregulation.
  \item \textbf{Low osmolarity:} ompF protein is preferentially produced.
  \item \textbf{High osmolarity:} expression of the \textit{micF} gene is induced.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item The \textit{micF} RNA is \textbf{antisense} to the ompF mRNA; it does not encode a polypeptide.
      \item micF RNA base-pairs with ompF mRNA by hydrogen bonding.
      \item Double-stranded RNA structure blocks ompF mRNA translation $\Rightarrow$ ompF protein synthesis decreases.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Post-translational Regulation and Feedback Inhibition}

\begin{diagbox}{32. Post-translational Regulation and Feedback Inhibition}

\textbf{Post-translational regulation} controls protein activity \emph{after} the protein has been synthesized.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Covalent modifications:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Irreversible:} proteolytic processing, attachment of prosthetic groups, sugars, or lipids.
  \item \textbf{Reversible} (transiently affect function):
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Phosphorylation ($-\mathrm{PO_4}$)
      \item Acetylation ($-\mathrm{COCH_3}$)
      \item Methylation ($-\mathrm{CH_3}$)
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Feedback inhibition:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A common mechanism to regulate \textbf{metabolic enzymes}.
  \item The \textbf{final product} of a biosynthetic pathway inhibits an enzyme that acts \textbf{early} in the pathway.
  \item Prevents over-accumulation of the final product.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Allosteric enzymes:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Enzymes regulated by feedback inhibition are often \textbf{allosteric}: they contain two distinct binding sites.
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Catalytic site} --- binds the substrate.
      \item \textbf{Regulatory site} --- binds the final product of the pathway (allosteric inhibitor).
    \end{itemize}
  \item Binding of the final product to the regulatory site changes the enzyme's conformation and reduces catalytic activity.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Classic example --- isoleucine biosynthesis:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Threonine $\xrightarrow{\text{Enzyme 1}}$ $\cdots$ $\rightarrow$ Isoleucine.
  \item Isoleucine is the final product and acts as an \textbf{allosteric inhibitor} of Enzyme~1 (the first step).
  \item When isoleucine accumulates, it shuts down its own production (feedback inhibition).
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Topic 13 --- Levels of Chromatin Compaction}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{13\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The 11-nm fibre (``beads on a string'') is the nucleosome array formed when DNA wraps around histone octamers. The DNA double helix is 2 nm; the 30-nm zig-zag fibre is the next level; radial loop domains reach 300 nm.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (D)} \textit{Rationale: The fully condensed metaphase chromosome is approximately 1{,}400 nm (1.4 $\mu$m) in diameter. The 300-nm and 700-nm structures are intermediate compaction states; the 30-nm fibre is the third level.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: Heterochromatin is enriched in repressive histone marks H3K9me3 and H3K27me3 and contains hypoacetylated histones, maintaining a compact, transcriptionally inactive state. H3K4me3 and acetylation marks are characteristic of euchromatin.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: MARs (Matrix Attachment Regions) tether the bases of chromatin loops to the nuclear matrix, creating topologically isolated loop domains.   \item \textit{Rationale: Condensin drives mitotic compaction; loss of function prevents cells from segregating chromosomes.}

        (a) During interphase, condensin resides in the \textbf{cytoplasm}. At the onset of mitosis (prophase), condensin is imported into the nucleus, binds chromosomes, and \textbf{compacts the radial loop domains} into the highly condensed metaphase chromosome.

        (b) SMC proteins form a ring-like structure with a hinge domain and two ATPase heads. ATP hydrolysis drives \textbf{conformational changes} that entrap and actively compact DNA strands, reducing their length.

        (c) Without condensin, chromosomes \textbf{fail to condense properly}, resulting in tangled chromosomes that cannot be correctly attached to spindle fibres. The outcome is \textbf{mitotic arrest} and/or \textbf{chromosome mis-segregation}, leading to aneuploidy and cell death.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 14 --- Genome Size and Repetitive Sequences}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{14\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: Highly repetitive sequences are present in tens of thousands to millions of copies and include satellite DNA (simple tandem repeats) found near centromeres and telomeres. Moderately repetitive sequences include rRNA genes and transposable elements; unique sequences are present once or a few times.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The C-value paradox is the observation that genome size (C-value, haploid DNA content) does not correlate with gene number or organismal complexity. The excess DNA in large genomes is primarily repetitive sequences.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: \textit{S.\ cerevisiae} has 16 haploid chromosomes. Humans have 23 haploid chromosomes. The human genome ($\sim$3{,}100 Mbp) is much larger than the yeast genome ($\sim$12 Mbp), despite yeast having fewer chromosomes.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: The larger genome is primarily composed of repetitive DNA sequences (highly and moderately repetitive), which are the main contributors to C-value differences between related species with similar gene numbers. Mechanisms include transposon amplification and unequal crossing over.}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Centromere, telomere, origin, and sequence element functions on linear chromosomes.}

        (a) The four elements:
        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Origins of replication} --- initiation sites for DNA replication ($\sim$every 100{,}000~bp in eukaryotes).
          \item \textbf{Centromere} --- site of kinetochore assembly; essential for correct attachment to the mitotic spindle.
          \item \textbf{Telomeres} --- repetitive sequences at both ends that protect chromosomes from exonucleolytic degradation and prevent end-joining.
          \item \textbf{Repetitive sequences} --- contribute to structural domains and, in some cases, gene regulation.
        \end{enumerate}

        (b) A chromosome lacking a centromere cannot attach to the spindle, and \textbf{fails to segregate} to daughter cells. It is typically lost within a few cell divisions.

        (c) Because linear chromosomes cannot be fully replicated at their ends by conventional DNA polymerases (the ``end-replication problem''), telomeres provide a buffer of repetitive sequence. Telomerase replenishes these sequences. Without telomeres, chromosomes \textbf{shorten with each division}, eventually losing essential genes, triggering DNA damage responses and cellular senescence or apoptosis.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 17 --- Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{17\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (D)} \textit{Rationale: The four essential PCR components are: (1) template DNA, (2) two sequence-specific primers, (3) dNTPs, and (4) a thermostable DNA polymerase (Taq). Restriction endonucleases are used to cut DNA but are not required for PCR amplification.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: The denaturation step requires $\sim$94--96$^\circ$C to break the hydrogen bonds between complementary bases and fully separate the two DNA strands. 50--65$^\circ$C is the annealing temperature; 72$^\circ$C is the extension temperature for Taq polymerase.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: After $n$ PCR cycles starting from 1 copy, the theoretical number of copies is $2^n$. After 30 cycles: $2^{30} = 1{,}073{,}741{,}824 \approx 10^9$ copies.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: The primer has 18 nt total; 60\% GC $\Rightarrow$ G+C $= 0.60\times18 \approx 11$; A+T $= 7$. $T_m = 4(11) + 2(7) = 44 + 14 = 58^\circ$C.}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Site-directed mutagenesis is an application of PCR-based methods.}

        (a) Steps: (i) Denature the cloned insert + vector to single-stranded DNA. (ii) Anneal a synthetic primer containing the desired mutation (creating a 1--3 nt mismatch at the mutation site). (iii) Extend the primer using Taq (or a high-fidelity polymerase) to copy the full plasmid. (iv) Treat with \textit{Dpn}I (which digests methylated, parental DNA) to eliminate the wild-type template. (v) Transform into bacteria; individual colonies carry plasmids.

        (b) The mutant clone is identified by \textbf{DNA sequencing} of the insert, confirming the desired mutation and the absence of unintended mutations.

        (c) Experimental strategy: (i) Express the wild-type and mutant proteins in a suitable expression system. (ii) Purify both proteins. (iii) Measure enzyme activity using a standard \textbf{biochemical assay} (e.g.\ substrate consumption or product formation over time). (iv) Compare kinetic parameters ($k_{\rm cat}$, $K_M$) between wild-type and mutant. (v) Include a \textbf{catalytic-dead mutant control} (known active-site residue mutated) as a negative control.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 18 --- Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{18\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: DNA is negatively charged because its phosphate backbone carries a net negative charge at physiological pH. In an electric field, negatively charged DNA migrates from the cathode toward the anode (``run to red''). The nitrogenous bases are uncharged under normal electrophoresis conditions.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: For linear dsDNA in agarose, fragment size (number of base pairs) is the primary determinant of migration rate because smaller fragments thread more easily through the gel matrix. GC content does not significantly affect migration under standard conditions.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: Supercoiled DNA is the most compact topological form and therefore migrates fastest through the agarose gel. Nicked circular DNA is relaxed and has the largest effective radius, so it migrates slowest. Linear DNA migrates at an intermediate rate.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: In EMSA, when a protein binds to the labelled DNA, the resulting protein--DNA complex has higher molecular mass than free DNA. This increased mass reduces the complex's mobility in the native gel, causing it to migrate more slowly and appear as a shifted (higher) band --- the diagnostic ``shift'' indicating protein--DNA interaction.}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Northern detects RNA, Western detects protein; both use hybridisation/antibody-based detection.}

        (a) \textbf{Northern blot}: isolate mRNA $\to$ separate by agarose gel electrophoresis under denaturing conditions $\to$ blot to nylon membrane $\to$ hybridise with labelled DNA probe complementary to target mRNA $\to$ wash off non-specific probe $\to$ detect labelled bands by autoradiography or fluorescence.

Together they confirm tissue-specific expression at both RNA and protein levels.

        (c) Two explanations for an oversized Northern band: (i) \textbf{Alternative splicing} of the $\beta$-globin pre-mRNA in this tissue retains an intron, producing a larger transcript. (ii) The probe detects a \textbf{related but larger transcript} from a homologous gene (cross-hybridisation).

\subsection{Topic 19 --- Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{19\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: In RNAi knockdown, the gene itself is intact (present in the genome), but the corresponding mRNA is targeted for degradation by the RISC complex. As a result, protein levels are greatly reduced or absent. This contrasts with a complete knockout, where the gene itself is absent.}

\subsection{Topic 20 --- Transcription Factors and Gene Activation}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{20\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: TFIID, through its TBP (TATA-binding protein) subunit, recognises and binds the TATA box at the core promoter. This is the nucleating event for pre-initiation complex assembly. TFIIB subsequently bridges TFIID to RNA pol II; TFIIH unwinds the DNA at the TSS and phosphorylates the pol II CTD.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: Regulatory transcription factors are expressed in specific cell types or in response to specific signals; they bind enhancers or silencers to control transcription of specific target genes. General TFs (e.g.\ TFIID, TFIIB, TFIIH) are required at every RNA pol II gene and are expressed ubiquitously.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The Mediator complex acts as a bridge between enhancer-bound activators and the pre-initiation complex (RNA pol II + general TFs) at the core promoter. Activators contact Mediator via their activation domains; Mediator in turn stabilises PIC assembly and stimulates pol II CTD phosphorylation to initiate elongation.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The acidic activation domain recruits Mediator subunits and/or components of TFIID, thereby stimulating PIC assembly. Without the activation domain, the factor can still occupy the enhancer via its zinc-finger domain but cannot recruit Mediator or stimulate PIC formation, so transcription falls to basal levels.}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} CTCF/cohesin-dependent insulation is mechanistically linked to TAD boundaries; its loss disrupts enhancer--promoter specificity.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item The insulator \textbf{blocks enhancer-to-promoter communication}: if CTCF is bound between them, the enhancer's effect cannot cross the boundary, reducing transcription of the target gene to basal levels.
    \item \textbf{CTCF} binds its 19-bp consensus motif at insulator sites; \textbf{cohesin} (loaded by NIPBL) is recruited to CTCF-bound sites and extrudes a chromatin loop until stalling.
          Two convergently oriented CTCF sites anchor a stable loop domain (TAD), keeping regulatory elements and genes within each TAD insulated from those in neighbouring TADs.
    \item \textbf{Mechanism 1 --- Enhancer hijacking:} Loss of a CTCF boundary allows an oncogene promoter to come under the influence of an enhancer from a neighbouring TAD, driving constitutive oncogene expression.
          \textbf{Mechanism 2 --- Silencer escape:} A tumour-suppressor gene previously insulated from a repressive domain becomes exposed to that domain, leading to its silencing.
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Topic 21 --- Chromatin Remodeling and Histone Modifications}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{21\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: ATP-dependent chromatin remodeling complexes (e.g.\ SWI/SNF, ISWI, NuRD) use ATP hydrolysis to reposition or eject nucleosomes, altering DNA accessibility for transcription factors. They do not directly add methyl or acetyl groups to histones (that is the role of HATs/HMTs) and do not degrade histones or methylate DNA.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: H3K4me3 (trimethylation of histone H3 lysine 4) is a hallmark of active gene promoters. It is read by PHD-finger proteins (e.g.\ TAF3, a TFIID subunit) that stabilise PIC assembly. H3K27me3 marks Polycomb-repressed genes; H3K9me3 marks constitutive heterochromatin; H3K36me3 marks actively transcribed gene bodies.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: Acetylation of histone lysine residues by HATs neutralises the positive (+) charge of the lysine $\varepsilon$-amino group, reducing the electrostatic attraction between histones and the negatively charged DNA phosphate backbone. This loosens the nucleosome and facilitates transcription factor access. Additionally, acetylated lysines are read by bromodomain-containing co-activators.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: HDAC inhibitors (e.g.\ vorinostat/SAHA) prevent HDACs from removing acetyl groups from histones, resulting in global hyperacetylation. Hyperacetylated chromatin is more open and accessible, causing decondensation of heterochromatin and re-expression of genes previously silenced by HDAC-mediated deacetylation (e.g.\ tumour-suppressor genes in cancer cells).}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} SWI/SNF is a tumour suppressor; its loss impairs accessibility of proliferation-limiting genes.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item Without SWI/SNF, nucleosomes are not repositioned at tumour-suppressor gene promoters $\Rightarrow$ TFIID cannot access the TATA box $\Rightarrow$ no PIC $\Rightarrow$ no transcription of e.g.\ \textit{CDKN2A} (p16$^{\text{INK4A}}$), \textit{RB1}, or \textit{PTEN} $\Rightarrow$ uncontrolled cell proliferation.
    \item \textbf{Strategy --- exploit synthetic lethality:} SWI/SNF-deficient cells become dependent on the parallel EZH2 (PRC2) pathway for survival. Inhibiting EZH2 (e.g.\ with tazemetostat) re-activates tumour-suppressor genes and selectively kills SWI/SNF$^{-}$ cancer cells while sparing normal cells that retain SWI/SNF and can compensate.
    \item Context-dependency arises from \textbf{subunit composition}: SWI/SNF exists as multiple sub-complexes (BAF, PBAF, ncBAF) with different subunits that recruit either co-activators or co-repressors.
          For example, ARID1A-containing BAF activates enhancers, while PBRM1-containing PBAF represses certain loci.
          Which genes are activated vs.\ repressed therefore depends on which SWI/SNF sub-complex variant is present.
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 22 --- DNA Methylation and Epigenetic Regulation}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{22\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: In mammals, DNA methylation occurs on cytosine residues within CpG dinucleotides, producing 5-methylcytosine (5mC). This is symmetrical: when CpG on one strand is methylated, the complementary CpG on the other strand is also methylated. Adenine GATC methylation occurs in bacteria (Dam methylase), not mammals.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: DNMT1 is the maintenance methyltransferase. It recognises hemi-methylated DNA (one parental strand methylated after replication) and methylates the newly synthesised daughter strand, faithfully propagating the methylation pattern through cell divisions. DNMT3A and DNMT3B are de novo methyltransferases that establish new methylation patterns during development.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (A)} \textit{Rationale: When CpG islands are hypermethylated, methyl-CpG binding domain (MBD) proteins (e.g.\ MeCP2, MBD2) bind the methylated cytosines and recruit HDAC/NuRD co-repressor complexes. Deacetylation of histones (and subsequent H3K9 methylation) leads to chromatin compaction and transcriptional silencing of the tumour-suppressor gene.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: On the wild-type paternal chromosome, the ICR is methylated, which blocks CTCF binding and allows the downstream enhancer to loop to \textit{IGF2}. If the paternal ICR loses its methylation, CTCF can now bind the paternal ICR, redirecting the enhancer to the non-coding RNA \textit{H19} instead of \textit{IGF2}. This silences the paternal \textit{IGF2} allele, leading to biallelic silencing and growth deficiency (Silver--Russell syndrome phenotype).}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Combinatorial logic enables a small TF vocabulary to specify many cell states; synergy arises from cooperative recruitment of co-activators.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item DNA methylation occurs on \textbf{cytosine} residues within \textbf{CpG dinucleotides}, producing \textbf{5-methylcytosine (5mC)}.
          CpG sites on both strands are methylated in a symmetrical pattern (each CpG on one strand is paired with a CpG on the complementary strand, read in the 5$'\to$3$'$ direction).
    \item \textbf{DNMT3A / DNMT3B:} \emph{de novo} methyltransferases --- add methyl groups to previously unmethylated CpGs during development.
          \textbf{DNMT1:} maintenance methyltransferase --- recognises hemi-methylated DNA (one strand methylated after replication) and methylates the new daughter strand, preserving the methylation pattern after each round of replication.
  \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} CpG island methylation silences genes by steric exclusion and recruitment of chromatin-condensing complexes.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item Unmethylated CpG islands are bound by \textbf{CpG-binding proteins} (e.g.\ CFP1/CXXC1) that recruit H3K4 methyltransferases (SET1/COMPASS) $\Rightarrow$ H3K4me3 $\Rightarrow$ open chromatin, allowing TF and TFIID binding.
    \item \textbf{Mechanism 1 --- Steric exclusion:} 5mC directly prevents binding of transcription factors (e.g.\ SP1, E2F) whose binding sites overlap CpG motifs.
          \textbf{Mechanism 2 --- MBD recruitment:} Methyl-CpG binding domain proteins (MeCP2, MBD2) bind 5mC and recruit HDAC/NuRD complexes $\Rightarrow$ deacetylation + H3K9 methylation $\Rightarrow$ heterochromatin $\Rightarrow$ silencing.
    \item \textbf{5-Azacytidine} (azacitidine) is incorporated into DNA in place of cytosine during replication.
          It forms a covalent adduct with DNMT1, trapping and degrading the enzyme $\Rightarrow$ DNMT1 cannot maintain methylation during subsequent replication $\Rightarrow$ passive demethylation over cell divisions $\Rightarrow$ CpG island becomes unmethylated $\Rightarrow$ tumour-suppressor gene is re-expressed.
  \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Imprinting is allele-specific silencing determined by parental origin; it is epigenetic because the DNA sequence is identical on both alleles.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item The \textbf{imprinting control region (ICR)} is differentially methylated between parental alleles (established in the germline by DNMT3A/3L).
          On the silenced allele, ICR methylation recruits MBD proteins + HDACs + H3K9 methyltransferases (e.g.\ G9a), generating compacted heterochromatin that spreads across the imprinted gene cluster.
          Cohesin + CTCF on the active allele (where the ICR is unmethylated) can insulate the gene from repressive marks.
    \item Normally, the paternal \textit{IGF2} is expressed because the paternal ICR is methylated, which \textbf{blocks CTCF binding} at the ICR, allowing the downstream enhancer to loop to \textit{IGF2}.
          If the paternal ICR loses methylation, CTCF binds $\Rightarrow$ the enhancer is redirected to the non-coding RNA \textit{H19} $\Rightarrow$ \textit{IGF2} on the paternal allele is \textbf{silenced} $\Rightarrow$ biallelic silencing $\Rightarrow$ growth deficiency (Silver--Russell syndrome phenotype).
    \item Imprinting is \textbf{epigenetic} because the maternal and paternal alleles have \emph{identical DNA sequences} but differ in methylation and histone modification; these marks are erased and re-established in the germline each generation, not encoded in the nucleotide sequence.
  \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Combinatorial logic enables a small TF vocabulary to specify many cell states; synergy arises from cooperative recruitment of co-activators.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item If there are $n$ transcription factors and each can be either ON or OFF, there are $2^n$ possible combinations.
          Even with 10 TFs, this gives 1{,}024 distinct patterns of gene expression.
          Because different target genes require different TF combinations, a limited TF repertoire can specify a large number of distinct transcriptional states.
    \item \textbf{Synergy (20-fold vs.\ additive $\sim$2-fold):}
          TF-A alone weakly recruits Mediator sub-complex~A; TF-B alone weakly recruits co-activator~B.
          When both are present simultaneously, their activation domains contact \emph{different} surfaces of Mediator in a cooperative manner, stabilising Mediator binding to the enhancer and dramatically accelerating PIC assembly $\Rightarrow$ superadditive (synergistic) transcription.
    \item \textbf{Cell-type specificity:} Both cell types express TF-A, but:
          \textbf{Muscle precursors} express \textbf{MyoD co-activators} (e.g.\ E-proteins as heterodimerisation partners) and have the \textit{MyoD} locus in a permissive chromatin state (H3K4me3 $+$ NFR at the \textit{MyoD} enhancer).
          \textbf{Liver precursors} express \textbf{ID proteins} (inhibitors of differentiation) that sequester E-proteins, preventing MyoD heterodimerisation, and the \textit{MyoD} locus is in a repressive state (H3K27me3, Polycomb).
          Therefore, TF-A alone is insufficient; the full activating combination (TF-A + MyoD + E-protein) is only assembled in muscle precursors.
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 23 --- The lac Operon: Negative and Positive Control}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{23\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: \textit{lacZ} encodes $\beta$-galactosidase, the enzyme that cleaves lactose into glucose and galactose (and also produces allolactose, the natural inducer). \textit{lacY} encodes lactose permease; \textit{lacA} encodes galactoside transacetylase; the lac repressor is encoded by \textit{lacI}, which is not part of the operon.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: \textit{lacI} has its own constitutive promoter (the \textit{i} promoter) and is expressed independently of the lac operon regulatory signals. It is considered a separate regulatory gene, not part of the operon. The lac operon consists only of the promoter, operator, and structural genes \textit{lacZ}, \textit{lacY}, \textit{lacA}.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The $\text{I}^S$ (super-repressor) cannot bind allolactose or IPTG (inducer) due to a mutation in its inducer-binding domain. Therefore, it permanently occupies the operator regardless of inducer concentration, resulting in constitutive repression. $\beta$-galactosidase activity is absent or very low even in the presence of excess IPTG.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: When glucose is depleted, the inhibition of adenylyl cyclase is relieved, causing cAMP levels to rise. High cAMP binds the CAP protein, forming the cAMP--CAP complex, which binds the CAP site upstream of the lac promoter and stimulates RNA polymerase binding and transcription. At high glucose, cAMP is low and CAP cannot bind, so the lac operon is poorly transcribed even if lactose is present.}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Comparing lac (inducible/negative) and trp (repressible/negative) operons reveals the versatility of the repressor-based strategy.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Negative control:} a repressor protein inhibits transcription by binding the operator; removing the repressor (or preventing its binding) activates the operon.
          \textbf{Positive control:} an activator protein stimulates transcription when it binds to DNA (e.g.\ CAP at the CAP site); removal of the activator decreases transcription.
    \item \textbf{lac operon (inducible):} Allolactose (inducer) binds the lac repressor $\Rightarrow$ conformational change $\Rightarrow$ repressor cannot bind operator $\Rightarrow$ transcription ON. The effector \emph{inactivates} the repressor.
          \textbf{trp operon (repressible):} Tryptophan (corepressor) binds the trp aporepressor $\Rightarrow$ conformational change $\Rightarrow$ repressor--corepressor complex binds operator $\Rightarrow$ transcription OFF. The effector \emph{activates} the repressor.
    \item Expression predictions:
          (i) lac, glucose absent/lactose absent: \textbf{Low} (CAP active, repressor ON $\Rightarrow$ no transcription; basal leaky level).
          (ii) lac, glucose absent/lactose present: \textbf{High} (CAP active + repressor OFF $\Rightarrow$ full transcription).
          (iii) trp, tryptophan absent: \textbf{High} (trp repressor inactive $\Rightarrow$ operator unoccupied $\Rightarrow$ full transcription; also attenuation is reduced).
          (iv) trp, tryptophan present: \textbf{Low} (trp repressor--corepressor complex binds operator + attenuation terminates early transcripts).
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 24 --- The trp Operon and Attenuation}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{24\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The trp operon is repressible because it is normally ON when tryptophan is scarce (the trp aporepressor is inactive alone). When tryptophan accumulates, it acts as a corepressor that activates the trp repressor, turning the operon OFF. This prevents over-production of tryptophan --- the pathway end product represses its own synthesis.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The trp repressor is synthesised as an inactive aporepressor. For it to become active and bind the operator, the corepressor tryptophan must bind the aporepressor, inducing a conformational change that increases the protein's affinity for the operator DNA. Without tryptophan, the repressor cannot bind the operator and the operon remains active.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: The antiterminator (2--3 hairpin) forms when tryptophan is SCARCE: the ribosome stalls at the Trp codons in region 1 of the leader mRNA (because Trp-tRNA is limiting). The stalled ribosome occupies region 1 but not region 2, freeing region 2 to base-pair with region 3, forming the antiterminator. This prevents the terminator (3--4 hairpin) from forming and allows transcription to continue into the structural genes.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: In a \textit{trpR} null mutant, the repression mechanism is abolished. At high tryptophan, the repressor cannot repress transcription. However, attenuation is still functional: abundant Trp-tRNA allows rapid translation of the leader peptide, the terminator hairpin forms, and many transcripts terminate early. The result is intermediate (elevated but not maximal) trp operon expression --- higher than wild-type at high Trp, but lower than at low Trp.}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Comparing both operons illuminates the versatility of repressor-based regulation and the distinction between inducible and repressible systems.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Key difference in effector molecule role:}
          \textit{lac operon:} allolactose (inducer) binds the lac repressor and \emph{reduces} its affinity for the operator $\Rightarrow$ operon turned ON.
          \textit{trp operon:} tryptophan (corepressor) binds the trp aporepressor and \emph{increases} its affinity for the operator $\Rightarrow$ operon turned OFF.
          In both cases, the repressor cannot bind DNA by itself when conditions do not require the response.
    \item \textbf{Inducible} (lac): the default state is OFF (repressor blocks transcription); an inducer molecule \emph{induces} (turns on) expression by removing the repressor. Useful for genes whose products are needed only when a substrate is present.
          \textbf{Repressible} (trp): the default state is ON (repressor inactive, transcription proceeds); accumulation of the end product \emph{represses} expression. Useful for biosynthetic genes whose products must be made unless they are already abundant.
    \item \textbf{Experimental design:} Construct a merozygote: one DNA molecule carries $\text{O}^c\,\text{Z}^+$; the other (F$'$) carries $\text{O}^+\,\text{Z}^-$.
          If the operator acts \textit{in cis}, only the structural genes on the same chromosome as $\text{O}^c$ (i.e.\ $\text{Z}^+$) will be expressed constitutively, even in the absence of inducer.
          The $\text{Z}^-$ gene on the F$'$ (associated with wild-type O$^+$) will remain regulated (expressed only in the presence of inducer).
          \textbf{Confirming result:} $\beta$-galactosidase ($\text{Z}^+$) activity is constitutive (high without inducer); permease or other proteins from the F$'$ operon are inducible. This proves the operator affects only genes in \textit{cis} on the same DNA molecule.
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 25 --- Translational and Post-translational Regulation}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{25\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{Answer: (C)} \textit{Rationale: Feedback inhibition acts on the enzyme catalysing the FIRST committed step of the pathway. This is the most metabolically efficient point of inhibition because it prevents all subsequent intermediates from accumulating, conserving ATP and precursors. Inhibiting a later step would allow intermediates before that step to accumulate, causing metabolic imbalance.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: In the isoleucine biosynthesis pathway, threonine deaminase catalyses the first committed step (conversion of threonine to $\alpha$-ketobutyrate). When isoleucine accumulates, it binds the allosteric/regulatory site of threonine deaminase, causing a conformational change that reduces catalytic activity and decreases flux through the pathway.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: micF RNA is antisense to the 5' region of ompF mRNA. At high osmolarity, micF RNA is transcribed and base-pairs with the Shine--Dalgarno sequence and/or AUG region of ompF mRNA via Watson--Crick hydrogen bonding. This prevents ribosome binding to the Shine--Dalgarno sequence, blocking translation initiation. The duplex also targets ompF mRNA for degradation.}

  \item \textbf{Answer: (B)} \textit{Rationale: Translational regulation acts on existing mRNA molecules already present in the cell, providing a faster response than transcriptional regulation, which requires transcription of new mRNA. Under sudden osmotic stress, cells cannot wait for new transcription; translational control of existing ompF mRNA provides immediate adjustment of membrane permeability.}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Post-translational control via covalent modification and allosteric feedback inhibition is the fastest level of metabolic fine-tuning.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Osmoregulation:} Bacteria live in environments where solute concentration can change rapidly between hypotonic and hypertonic conditions. Without osmoregulation, cells would swell (burst) in hypotonic conditions or shrink (lose turgor) in hypertonic conditions. Bacteria must control membrane permeability to maintain internal water balance and survive.
    \item \textbf{Step-by-step mechanism at high osmolarity:}
          (i) High osmolarity is sensed by the cell (via the EnvZ/OmpR two-component system).
          (ii) The \textit{micF} gene is transcribed; micF RNA is produced.
          (iii) micF RNA is \textbf{antisense} to the 5$'$ end of the ompF mRNA --- their sequences are complementary.
          (iv) micF RNA base-pairs with the ompF mRNA by Watson--Crick hydrogen bonding, forming a double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) duplex in the region of the Shine--Dalgarno sequence and/or start codon of ompF mRNA.
          (v) The ribosome cannot bind the blocked Shine--Dalgarno/start codon region $\Rightarrow$ translation initiation is prevented.
          (vi) The micF:ompF RNA duplex is also targeted for degradation by RNase~III (in some contexts) $\Rightarrow$ further reducing ompF mRNA levels.
          (vii) Result: ompF protein synthesis decreases $\Rightarrow$ outer membrane becomes less permeable $\Rightarrow$ water loss is minimised under hypertonic stress.
    \item \textbf{Effectiveness of antisense RNA:}
          (i) \textbf{Speed} --- does not require de novo protein synthesis; the RNA is rapidly transcribed and acts immediately.
          (ii) \textbf{Specificity} --- base-pairing is highly sequence-specific (complementary to the target mRNA), ensuring only the correct mRNA is regulated.
          (iii) \textbf{Nature of interaction:} micF RNA is \emph{complementary} to the ompF mRNA at the 5$'$ untranslated region (near the Shine--Dalgarno sequence); they form a stable dsRNA structure via Watson--Crick base-pairing that blocks ribosome access to the start codon.
  \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Understanding two translational repressor mechanisms and comparing translational vs.\ transcriptional regulation reveals their complementary roles.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Mechanism 1 --- Shine--Dalgarno/start codon binding:} The translational repressor protein binds directly to the Shine--Dalgarno sequence and/or AUG start codon region of the mRNA. The ribosome's 16S rRNA cannot pair with the Shine--Dalgarno sequence $\Rightarrow$ 30S ribosomal subunit cannot assemble the initiation complex $\Rightarrow$ translation does not initiate.
          \textbf{Mechanism 2 --- Distal binding with conformational change:} The repressor binds outside the Shine--Dalgarno/start codon region. This induces a conformational change in the mRNA secondary structure that sequesters the Shine--Dalgarno sequence in a stem-loop $\Rightarrow$ ribosome access is blocked indirectly $\Rightarrow$ translation initiation is prevented.
    \item \textbf{Mutant Shine--Dalgarno:} The translational repressor can no longer bind the mRNA.
          \textbf{In the absence of repressor:} the mutation in the Shine--Dalgarno sequence may also reduce ribosome binding efficiency $\Rightarrow$ basal translation may be reduced compared to wild-type.
          \textbf{In the presence of repressor:} since the repressor cannot bind, the mRNA is \textbf{translated at an unregulated (constitutive) level}; the regulatory response is abolished. The mRNA will be translated regardless of the physiological signal that normally triggers translational repression.
    \item \textbf{Translational vs.\ transcriptional regulation:}
          Translational regulation acts on \emph{existing} mRNA $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{faster response} (no need to wait for new transcription).
          Transcriptional regulation is \emph{slower} but more energy-efficient long-term (no wasted mRNA synthesis).
          Translational regulation is generally \textbf{reversible} (repressor dissociates when the regulatory signal disappears); transcriptional regulation is similarly reversible but operates more slowly.
          \textbf{Physiological advantage:} translational regulation is advantageous when a \textbf{rapid response} is required (e.g.\ sudden osmotic stress, heat shock) where waiting for transcription would be too slow, or when the same mRNA must be regulated differently in different cellular compartments or conditions.
  \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale:} Post-translational control via covalent modification and allosteric feedback inhibition is the fastest level of metabolic fine-tuning.
  \begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
    \item \textbf{Irreversible modifications:}
          (1) Proteolytic cleavage (e.g.\ zymogen activation: trypsinogen $\rightarrow$ trypsin).
          (2) Attachment of prosthetic groups, lipids, or sugars (e.g.\ lipid anchoring, glycosylation).
          \textbf{Reversible modifications:}
          (1) Phosphorylation ($-\mathrm{PO_4}$, added by kinases, removed by phosphatases).
          (2) Acetylation ($-\mathrm{COCH_3}$, added by acetyltransferases, removed by deacetylases).
    \item \textbf{Why inhibit the first committed step:}
          If a later enzyme in the pathway is inhibited, intermediates before that enzyme still accumulate, consuming ATP and precursors unnecessarily and possibly causing metabolic imbalances.
          Inhibiting the \textbf{first committed step} prevents all intermediates from accumulating; the entire metabolic flux through the pathway is stopped at the very beginning $\Rightarrow$ maximum energy conservation and no build-up of potentially toxic intermediates.
    \item \textbf{Branched pathways --- isoenzyme or cumulative feedback inhibition:}
          \textbf{Isoenzymes:} Two distinct enzymes (isoenzymes) catalyse the same first committed step; each isoenzyme is specifically inhibited by the end product of only one branch. This allows each branch to be independently regulated without cross-inhibition.
          \textbf{Cumulative feedback inhibition:} Each end product \emph{partially} inhibits the common enzyme; when both end products accumulate, their partial inhibitory effects are additive (cumulative), achieving complete inhibition of the shared first step. This allows proportional regulation: if only one end product accumulates, flux through the pathway is only partially reduced, maintaining production of the other product.
  \end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}
