\textbf{Purpose:}  This diagnostic is designed to help you \emph{identify knowledge gaps
before studying}, so that you can focus your limited review time on the SAT math skills
that need the most attention.

\bigskip
\textbf{How to use this document:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Step 1 --- Pre-Diagnostic Self-Rating (Section 3):}  Before answering any
        questions, rate your confidence on each SAT math skill using the 1--5 scale.
        Be honest --- this is for your benefit only.
  \item \textbf{Step 2 --- Diagnostic Knowledge Check (Sections 4 \& 5):}  Answer every
        question \emph{without} referring to your notes, calculator, or textbook.  Leave
        blanks rather than guessing randomly.
  \item \textbf{Step 3 --- Self-Score and Gap Analysis:}  Use the Answer Key to score each
        subsection (10 points each), then transfer your scores to the Skill Gap Analysis table.
  \item \textbf{Step 4 --- Build Your Study Plan:}  Any subsection where you scored below
        70\% (\textless\,7/10) is a priority gap.  Fill in the personalized study plan
        to target those areas first.
  \item \textbf{Step 5 --- Post-Activity Re-Rating:}  After completing your focused review,
        return to Section 3 and re-rate yourself to measure your growth.
\end{enumerate}

\bigskip
\textbf{Time estimate:}  60--80 minutes (diagnostic portion); additional time for study plan.
\end{instructbox}

        $x\times0.50=0.5x$;\; $0.5x\times1.50=0.75x$ (only $75\%$ of original, a $25\%$ decrease).
\end{enumerate}

\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.3 Unit Conversions}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=111]
  \item \textbf{5{,}500 meters.}  $5.5\text{ km}\times1000\text{ m/km}=5{,}500$ m.
  \item \textbf{225 minutes.}  $3\times60+45=180+45=225$ min.
  \item \textbf{$\approx12.8$ km/L.}
        $\dfrac{30\text{ mi}}{\text{gal}}\times\dfrac{1.609\text{ km}}{1\text{ mi}}\times\dfrac{1\text{ gal}}{3.785\text{ L}}=\dfrac{30\times1.609}{3.785}\approx12.8$ km/L.
  \item \textbf{20 sq yards.}
        Area $=12\times15=180$ sq ft;\; $1\text{ yd}=3\text{ ft}\Rightarrow1\text{ sq yd}=9\text{ sq ft}$;\; $180\div9=20$ sq yd.
  \item \textbf{$\approx22.2^\circ$C.}
        $C=\dfrac{5}{9}(72-32)=\dfrac{5}{9}(40)=\dfrac{200}{9}\approx22.2^\circ$C.
  \item \textbf{4 items/min.}  $240\text{ items/hr}\div60=4$ items/min.
  \item \textbf{250 cm.}  $2.5\text{ m}\times100\text{ cm/m}=250$ cm.
  \item \textbf{0.75 L.}  $750\text{ mL}\div1000=0.75$ L.
  \item \textbf{A.}  Multiply by $\dfrac{1\text{ mi}}{5280\text{ ft}}\times\dfrac{3600\text{ s}}{1\text{ hr}}$ to cancel ft and s, leaving mi/hr.
  \item \textbf{True.}  Dimensional analysis relies on cancelling matching units in numerator and denominator.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{5.4 Data Interpretation: Tables and Graphs}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=121]
  \item \textbf{138.75.}  Mean $=\dfrac{120+145+130+160}{4}=\dfrac{555}{4}=138.75$.
  \item \textbf{16 students.}  Below 80: $4\,(\text{60--69})+12\,(\text{70--79})=16$.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{5}{6}$.}
        Total passed $=25+5=30$;\; $P(\text{studied}\mid\text{passed})=\dfrac{25}{30}=\dfrac{5}{6}$.
  \item \textbf{\$1{,}000.}  Food $=25\%\times\$4000=0.25\times4000=\$1{,}000$.
  \item \textbf{30--39.}  That bar has height 12, the tallest.
  \item \textbf{Rate of change $=4$.}
        $\Delta y/\Delta x=(7-3)/(2-1)=4$ (constant for all pairs: $(11-7)/1=4$, etc.).
  \item \textbf{$5^\circ$F/day.}  Total rise $=80-60=20^\circ$F over 4 days (Mon\to Fri): $20\div4=5^\circ$F/day.
  \item \textbf{False.}  Bar graphs display categorical data; histograms display continuous data in intervals.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{3}{8}$.}  $\dfrac{30}{80}=\dfrac{3}{8}$ (simplify by dividing by 10).
  \item \textbf{C --- Line graph.}  Line graphs show change in a quantity over time.
\end{enumerate}

\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.5 Scatterplots and Line of Best Fit}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=131]
  \item \textbf{Positive.}  Both variables increase together $\Rightarrow$ positive correlation.
  \item \textbf{30.}  Substitute $x=8$: $\hat{y}=2.5(8)+10=20+10=30$.
  \item \textbf{2.5 points.}  The slope of the line of best fit represents the average change in $\hat{y}$ per 1-unit increase in $x$.
  \item \textbf{B --- The predicted value when $x=0$.}  The $y$-intercept is the $\hat{y}$ value when $x=0$.
  \item \textbf{Strong.}  $|r|=0.92$ is close to 1, indicating a strong correlation.
  \item \textbf{Observed (actual); predicted.}  Residual $=y_{\text{obs}}-\hat{y}$.
  \item \textbf{D --- $r=-0.95$.}  The strongest relationship has $|r|$ closest to 1; $|-0.95|=0.95$ is largest.
  \item \textbf{False.}  Correlation measures association; only a controlled experiment can establish causation.
  \item \textbf{16.}  Substitute $x=7$: $\hat{y}=3(7)-5=21-5=16$.
  \item \textbf{C --- $r=0$.}  No apparent pattern $\Rightarrow$ no linear relationship $\Rightarrow r\approx0$.
\end{enumerate}

\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.6 Probability}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=141]
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{1}{3}$.}  $P(\text{red})=\dfrac{4}{4+6+2}=\dfrac{4}{12}=\dfrac{1}{3}$.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{1}{12}$.}  Independent events: $P(\text{H})\times P(\text{4})=\dfrac{1}{2}\times\dfrac{1}{6}=\dfrac{1}{12}$.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{1}{13}$.}  4 kings in 52 cards: $\dfrac{4}{52}=\dfrac{1}{13}$.
  \item \textbf{0.7.}  Mutually exclusive: $P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)=0.3+0.4=0.7$.
  \item \textbf{75\%.}  Inclusion-exclusion: $P(M\cup S)=0.60+0.45-0.30=0.75=75\%$.
  \item \textbf{0.2.}  Multiplication rule: $P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B\mid A)=0.5\times0.4=0.2$.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{5}{14}$.}
        Without replacement: $P(\text{both red})=\dfrac{5}{8}\times\dfrac{4}{7}=\dfrac{20}{56}=\dfrac{5}{14}$.
  \item \textbf{True.}  By the probability axioms, $\sum P(\text{all outcomes})=1$.
  \item \textbf{$\dfrac{2}{5}$.}  Numbers $>6$: $\{7,8,9,10\}$;\; $P=\dfrac{4}{10}=\dfrac{2}{5}$.
  \item \textbf{$P(A)\cdot P(B)$.}  Independence definition: $P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B)$.
\end{enumerate}

\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.7 Statistical Inference and Sampling}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=151]
  \item \textbf{Random (simple random).}  Every member has an equal chance of selection.
  \item \textbf{Convenience bias.}  Surveying one class (easy to access) may not represent the whole school.
  \item \textbf{48\% to 56\%.}  $52\%-4\%=48\%$;\; $52\%+4\%=56\%$.
  \item \textbf{B --- Decreases.}  Larger sample $\Rightarrow$ smaller standard error $\Rightarrow$ smaller margin of error.
  \item \textbf{Randomized controlled experiment (RCT).}  Random assignment allows causal inference.
  \item \textbf{Stratified sampling.}  Population divided into subgroups (strata); random sample drawn from each.
  \item \textbf{C --- There is an association between breakfast and grades.}
        Observational studies can only establish association, not causation.
  \item \textbf{True} (generally, when the sample is also representative; a larger biased sample is not more accurate).
  \item \textbf{B --- Only those who choose to participate respond.}
        Voluntary response bias: self-selected responders are not representative.
  \item \textbf{95.}  A 95\% confidence interval means 95\% of such intervals (from many repetitions) contain the true parameter.
\end{enumerate}

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.8 Measures of Center and Spread}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=161]
  \item \textbf{Mean $=6$.}  $\dfrac{4+7+9+3+7}{5}=\dfrac{30}{5}=6$.
  \item \textbf{Median $=9.5$.}
        Ordered: $\{2,5,8,11,14,17\}$ (6 values);\; median $=\dfrac{8+11}{2}=\dfrac{19}{2}=9.5$.
  \item \textbf{Mode $=5$.}  5 appears 3 times in $\{3,5,5,7,8,5,9\}$ — more than any other value.
  \item \textbf{Range $=15$.}  Max $-$ min $=22-7=15$.
  \item \textbf{B --- Most values lie within 5 units of the mean.}
        Standard deviation measures typical distance from the mean.
  \item \textbf{C --- Mean.}  The mean shifts toward the outlier; median and mode are resistant.
  \item \textbf{B --- Data set B has greater spread.}
        Data set A $=\{10,10,10,10\}$ has SD $=0$;\; Data set B has non-zero spread.
  \item \textbf{IQR $=6$.}
        Ordered: $\{2,4,5,7,9,10,13\}$; median (Q2) $=7$;\; lower half $\{2,4,5\}$: Q1 $=4$;\;
        upper half $\{9,10,13\}$: Q3 $=10$;\; IQR $=10-4=6$.
  \item \textbf{True.}  In a symmetric distribution, the mean and median coincide.
  \item \textbf{C --- IQR.}  IQR uses only the middle 50\% of data, so it is unaffected by extreme values.
\end{enumerate}

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\hrulefill
\subsection*{5.9 Evaluating Statistical Claims: Observational Studies and Experiments}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em,label=\textbf{Q\arabic*.},start=171]
  \item \textbf{Cannot; manipulate (control).}
        Observational studies have no intervention, so researchers cannot control variables.
  \item \textbf{Causation.}
        Random assignment in an RCT rules out confounders, supporting causal conclusions.
  \item \textbf{Confounding (lurking).}
        A confounding variable is associated with both the explanatory and response variables.
  \item \textbf{B --- Tracking students' diet and grades without intervention.}
        No manipulation of diet = observational.
  \item \textbf{Participant (subject); researcher (experimenter).}
        Neither knows the treatment assignment in a double-blind study.
  \item \textbf{Weather/rainfall (or urban density).}
        Rain causes people to carry umbrellas AND increases traffic accidents.
  \item \textbf{Belief in the treatment (psychological expectation of improvement).}
        The placebo effect is an improvement due to expectation, not actual treatment.
  \item \textbf{True.}  Random sampling supports generalization to the broader population.
  \item \textbf{C --- In this sample, more sleep is associated with higher grades.}
        Small observational study $\Rightarrow$ association only, limited generalizability.
  \item \textbf{Causation; association.}
        Experiments $\Rightarrow$ causation;\; observational studies $\Rightarrow$ association only.
\end{enumerate}
