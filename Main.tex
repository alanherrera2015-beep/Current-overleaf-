\section{Problem 1: The Flag-Raising Girder}
% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\begin{planbox}
\textbf{Problem Statement}

You are managing a new office building when the 10~lb automatic flag raising system attached to the end of a
horizontal girder fails. The maintenance technician for the building volunteers to climb out onto the girder
wearing a safety harness and go to its end to fix the mechanism. Will you approve the technician's plan?

A quick review of the building's plans shows that the girder is a 120~lb aluminum I-beam that is very strong and
rigid. One end of the girder is bolted to the wall of the building. Nine feet away, the other end of the girder is
supported by a strong, lightweight cable. The cable goes up from the end of the girder furthest from the
building at an angle of $30^\circ$ to the girder until it reaches the vertical wall where it is bolted. The
technician weighs 150~lbs including equipment. From the specifications of the building, the bolts attaching
both the cable and the girder to the building have been tested to hold a force of 500~lbs. First you check to
see if the bolts attaching the cable to the building will hold. If that is safe, then you do the same for the
bolts holding the girder.
\end{planbox}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Forces acting on the girder:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $W_g = 120\,\mathrm{lb}$ (weight of girder, downward, at $x = L/2 = 4.5\,\mathrm{ft}$)
  \item $W_t = 150\,\mathrm{lb}$ (technician, downward, at $x = L = 9\,\mathrm{ft}$)
  \item $W_f = 10\,\mathrm{lb}$ (flag system, downward, at $x = L = 9\,\mathrm{ft}$)
  \item $T$ (cable tension at angle $30^\circ$ above horizontal, at $x = L = 9\,\mathrm{ft}$, directed toward the upper-left)
  \item $\vec{R} = (R_x,\, R_y)$ (reaction force from wall bolt, at $x = 0$)
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Given:} $L = 9\,\mathrm{ft}$, bolt rated for $500\,\mathrm{lb}$.
\end{diagbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Solution}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{keybox}

\textbf{Step 1 --- Find the cable tension $T$ (check cable bolt)}

Apply the rotational equilibrium condition $\sum\tau = 0$ about the wall bolt. Choosing this pivot eliminates the unknown wall-bolt reaction from the torque equation.

The cable pulls the end of the girder at angle $30^\circ$ above horizontal. Its moment arm is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the cable's line of action. Because the cable is attached at the far end ($x = L$), the moment arm equals $L\sin(30^\circ)$---equivalently, the torque of the cable tension about the pivot is simply
\[
  \tau_T = T\sin(30^\circ)\cdot L
\]
because only the \emph{vertical} component of $T$ (perpendicular to the horizontal girder) creates a torque about the horizontal pivot.

Setting the sum of torques equal to zero (counterclockwise positive):
\[
  T\sin(30^\circ)\cdot L
  - W_g\cdot\frac{L}{2}
  - W_t\cdot L
  - W_f\cdot L = 0
\]
\[
  T\,(0.5)\,(9\,\mathrm{ft})
  = (120\,\mathrm{lb})(4.5\,\mathrm{ft})
  + (150\,\mathrm{lb})(9\,\mathrm{ft})
  + (10\,\mathrm{lb})(9\,\mathrm{ft})
\]
\[
  4.5\,T = 540 + 1350 + 90 = 1980\,\mathrm{lb\cdot ft}
\]
\[
  \boxed{T = 440\,\mathrm{lb}}
\]

\textit{Check cable bolt:} $T = 440\,\mathrm{lb} < 500\,\mathrm{lb}$ \checkmark \quad \textbf{The cable bolt is safe.}

\bigskip
\textbf{Step 2 --- Find the wall-bolt reaction force $\vec{R}$ (check girder bolt)}

The cable tension components are:
\[
  T_x = T\cos(30^\circ) = 440\,\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 381.1\,\mathrm{lb}
  \quad(\text{horizontal; cable pulls girder end in }{-x}\text{ direction, toward wall})
\]
\[
  T_y = T\sin(30^\circ) = 440\,(0.5) = 220\,\mathrm{lb}
  \quad(\text{upward})
\]

Apply force equilibrium $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$. The cable tension has a component $T_x$ pulling the girder end toward the wall ($-x$). The wall bolt must push the girder away from the wall ($+x$) to maintain equilibrium, so:
\[
  \sum F_x = 0:\quad R_x - T_x = 0
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  R_x = T\cos(30^\circ) \approx 381.1\,\mathrm{lb}
\]
\[
  \sum F_y = 0:\quad R_y + T_y - W_g - W_t - W_f = 0
\]
\[
  R_y = (120 + 150 + 10)\,\mathrm{lb} - 220\,\mathrm{lb}
       = 280 - 220 = 60\,\mathrm{lb}
\]

The magnitude of the force on the wall bolt holding the girder:
\[
  R = \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2}
    = \sqrt{(381.1)^2 + (60)^2}
    = \sqrt{145237 + 3600}
    = \sqrt{148837}
    \approx \boxed{385.8\,\mathrm{lb}}
\]

\textit{Check girder bolt:} $R \approx 386\,\mathrm{lb} < 500\,\mathrm{lb}$ \checkmark \quad \textbf{The girder bolt is safe.}

\bigskip
\textbf{Conclusion:} Both bolts are below their 500~lb rated strength. \textbf{You should approve the technician's plan.}

\end{keybox}

\begin{warnbox}
\textbf{Key Conceptual Points}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Always choose the pivot at the point with the most unknown forces to simplify the torque equation.
  \item The torque of a force equals (force magnitude) $\times$ (perpendicular distance from pivot to line of action), equivalent to $F_\perp \cdot r$ or $F \cdot r_\perp$.
  \item Both translational \emph{and} rotational equilibrium must be checked.
  \item The wall bolt experiences the \emph{vector sum} of all force components, not just one direction.
\end{itemize}
\end{warnbox}

\section{Problem 2: Spinal Loading During Improper Lifting}
% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\begin{planbox}
\textbf{Problem Statement}

You are studying spinal damage caused by routine activities. Before starting this study, you need to determine
how the force on the spine at the fifth lumbar vertebra, where the spine is connected to the pelvis, depends on
the weight of a box lifted improperly from the floor. In this case, a person bends over so their back is
horizontal and lifts the box straight up.

To model the situation, you decide to represent the spine as a uniform horizontal rod with a known length $L$
and negligible weight. You also neglect the weight of the back. One end of the rod is where the shoulders
support the weight $W$ of the box as it is lifted off the floor. The other end of the rod is where the spine is
attached to its pivot at the fifth lumbar vertebra. Although the back muscles are complicated, for this simple
model you assume that the principal muscle lifting the spine is attached two-thirds of the way up the spine at
an angle of $2^\circ$ to the horizontal spine.
\end{planbox}

\begin{diagbox}{Free-Body Diagram --- Horizontal Spine}

\textbf{Coordinate system:} $+x$ horizontal (away from pelvis toward shoulders), $+y$ upward\\[4pt]
\textbf{Pivot:} Fifth lumbar vertebra at $x = 0$

\end{center}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Forces acting on the spine:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item $W$ (box weight, downward) at $x = L$ (shoulder end)
  \item $F_m$ (back muscle force, at angle $2^\circ$ to horizontal) at $x = \tfrac{2}{3}L$
  \item $\vec{F}_S = (F_{Sx},\, F_{Sy})$ (joint reaction force at fifth lumbar, at $x = 0$)
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent The muscle is nearly parallel to the spine. It pulls mostly horizontally (compressing the spine) with a small upward component that provides the restoring torque.
\end{diagbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Solution}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{keybox}

\textbf{Step 1 --- Find the back muscle force $F_m$}

Apply rotational equilibrium $\sum\tau = 0$ about the pivot at the fifth lumbar vertebra.

Only the \emph{component of $F_m$ perpendicular to the spine} (i.e., the vertical component $F_m\sin 2^\circ$) contributes a torque. Counterclockwise is positive:
\[
  F_m\sin(2^\circ)\cdot\frac{2L}{3} - W\cdot L = 0
\]
\[
  F_m\sin(2^\circ) = \frac{W\cdot L}{\dfrac{2L}{3}} = \frac{3W}{2}
\]
\[
  F_m = \frac{3W}{2\sin(2^\circ)} = \frac{3W}{2\times 0.03490} = \frac{3W}{0.06981}
  \approx \boxed{42.98\,W}
\]

\textit{Result:} The back muscle must exert a force approximately \textbf{43 times the weight of the box}! This enormous force is required because the muscle moment arm ($\tfrac{2}{3}L\,\sin 2^\circ$) is extremely small compared to the box moment arm ($L$).

\bigskip
\textbf{Step 2 --- Find the force on the fifth lumbar vertebra}

Apply translational equilibrium:

\[
  \sum F_x = 0:\quad F_{Sx} - F_m\cos(2^\circ) = 0
  \;\Longrightarrow\;
  F_{Sx} = F_m\cos(2^\circ) \approx 42.98W\times 0.99939 \approx 42.95\,W
\]
\[
  \sum F_y = 0:\quad F_{Sy} + F_m\sin(2^\circ) - W = 0
\]
\[
  F_{Sy} = W - F_m\sin(2^\circ) = W - \frac{3W}{2} = -\frac{W}{2}
\]
(The negative sign indicates a downward component at the vertebra.)

The magnitude of the total force on the fifth lumbar vertebra:
\[
  F_S = \sqrt{F_{Sx}^2 + F_{Sy}^2}
      = \sqrt{(42.95W)^2 + (0.5W)^2}
      = W\sqrt{1844.7 + 0.25}
      = W\sqrt{1844.9}
      \approx \boxed{42.95\,W}
\]

\textbf{The force on the fifth lumbar vertebra is approximately 43 times the weight of the box.}

For example, if the box weighs 30~lb, the spine experiences a compressive force of approximately $43 \times 30 = 1290\,\mathrm{lb}$ --- nearly two-thirds of a ton!

\bigskip
\textbf{Conclusion:} The force on the spine scales linearly with the box weight:
\[
  F_S \approx \frac{3\cos(2^\circ)}{2\sin(2^\circ)}\,W \approx \frac{3}{2\tan(2^\circ)}\,W \approx 42.95\,W
\]

\end{keybox}

\begin{warnbox}
\textbf{Key Conceptual Points}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item A small angle ($2^\circ$) leads to a tiny torque arm for the muscle, requiring an enormous muscle force to balance a moderate load.
  \item The dominant force at the lumbar vertebra is the large \emph{horizontal} compressive component $F_m\cos(2^\circ)$, which is almost entirely due to the muscle.
  \item This result explains why improper lifting (back horizontal) causes far more spinal stress than proper lifting (back upright), where the geometry is far more favorable.
  \item The force on the spine is \emph{independent of the length $L$}: longer or shorter spines all experience the same ratio $F_S/W \approx 43$.
\end{itemize}
\end{warnbox}

\section{Problem 3: Spring-Ball Device Inside a Cylindrical Pipe}
% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\begin{planbox}
\textbf{Problem Statement}

You have been asked to evaluate a device designed to keep thin-walled cylindrical pipes from collapsing.
The design has three identical very small balls connected in an equilateral triangle by three identical springs.
When inserted into a pipe, the springs are compressed so that each ball exerts a force on the wall of the pipe.
You worry that the thin-walled pipe might be damaged by the device, so you decide to calculate the push of
one of the balls on the inside of the circular pipe wall.

You know the spring constant $k$ and the compression distance $d$ of each spring when it is in the pipe.
You also know that the sum of the angles of any triangle is $180^\circ$ and an equilateral triangle has equal
angles.
\end{planbox}

  \draw[<->,gray!55,thin] (0,0) -- (0,1.5)
    node[midway,right,gray!55] {$R$};
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Key geometry:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Since the triangle is equilateral, all three angles are $60^\circ$ (because $3 \times 60^\circ = 180^\circ$).
  \item Each ball is at the corner of an equilateral triangle inscribed in the pipe's circular cross-section.
  \item Each ball is connected to \emph{two} springs (to the other two balls).
  \item By symmetry, the line from the center of the pipe to a ball is exactly bisected by the $60^\circ$ interior angle at that ball, so \emph{each spring makes an angle of $30^\circ$ with the outward radial direction} at the ball.
\end{itemize}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Forces on one ball:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item Spring force 1: $F_s = kd$, directed $30^\circ$ away from radial (toward one neighbor)
  \item Spring force 2: $F_s = kd$, directed $30^\circ$ away from radial (toward other neighbor)
  \item Normal force from pipe wall: $N$, directed radially inward (toward center)
\end{itemize}
\end{diagbox}

% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Solution}
% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\begin{keybox}

\textbf{Step 1 --- Determine the angle each spring makes with the radial direction}

Since the triangle is equilateral, each interior angle is:
\[
  \theta_{\text{interior}} = \frac{180^\circ}{3} = 60^\circ
\]

At any given ball (vertex), the two springs point toward the other two vertices. In an equilateral triangle, the circumcenter, centroid, and incenter all coincide at the same point. The line from any vertex through this common center is also the angle bisector at that vertex. Therefore, the outward radial direction at a vertex (pointing away from the center) bisects the interior $60^\circ$ angle, and each spring makes an angle of:
\[
  \phi = \frac{60^\circ}{2} = 30^\circ
\]
with the outward radial direction.

\bigskip
\textbf{Step 2 --- Find the net outward (radial) force from the springs on the ball}

Each compressed spring pushes the ball radially outward (as well as tangentially). By symmetry, the two tangential components cancel exactly. The radial component of each spring force is:
\[
  F_{\text{radial, one spring}} = F_s\cos(30^\circ) = kd\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}
\]

Both springs contribute equal outward components, so the total outward spring force on the ball is:
\[
  F_{\text{total, outward}} = 2\,F_s\cos(30^\circ) = 2\,kd\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = kd\sqrt{3}
\]

\bigskip
\textbf{Step 3 --- Find the normal force $N$ (ball's push on the pipe)}

For the ball to be in equilibrium, the inward normal force from the pipe wall must equal the outward spring force:
\[
  \sum F_{\text{radial}} = 0:\quad kd\sqrt{3} - N = 0
\]
\[
  \boxed{N = kd\sqrt{3}}
\]

By Newton's third law, the ball pushes on the pipe wall with the same magnitude:
\[
  \text{Force of ball on pipe wall} = kd\sqrt{3}
\]

\bigskip
\textbf{Verification --- tangential equilibrium:}

The tangential (perpendicular-to-radial) components of the two spring forces are $+F_s\sin(30^\circ)$ and $-F_s\sin(30^\circ)$, which cancel exactly. No tangential friction is required. \checkmark

\end{keybox}

\begin{warnbox}
\textbf{Key Conceptual Points}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Why $30^\circ$?} The interior angle of an equilateral triangle is $60^\circ$. The outward radial direction bisects this angle, so each spring makes $30^\circ$ with radial.
  \item The $\sqrt{3}$ factor arises from $2\cos(30^\circ) = \sqrt{3}$. It means each ball pushes harder than a single spring force: $kd\sqrt{3} \approx 1.73\,kd$.
  \item By symmetry, all three balls exert the same normal force on the pipe, so the pipe experiences three equal, equally-spaced contact forces.
  \item This result is independent of the pipe radius $R$: the geometry only depends on the triangle's angles, not its size.
  \item To avoid pipe damage, ensure $kd\sqrt{3}$ is less than the pipe wall's rated lateral load capacity.
\end{itemize}
\end{warnbox}

\begin{instructbox}
\textbf{General Strategy for Static Equilibrium Problems}

\begin{enumerate}[label=\arabic*.]
  \item \textbf{Identify the system} and draw a complete free-body diagram.
  \item \textbf{Choose a convenient pivot} for the torque equation (eliminate as many unknowns as possible).
  \item \textbf{Write the torque equation} $\sum \tau = 0$. Only forces perpendicular to the moment arm contribute.
  \item \textbf{Write force equations} $\sum F_x = 0$ and $\sum F_y = 0$.
  \item \textbf{Solve the system} of equations. Always check units and signs.
  \item \textbf{Check your answer} against physical intuition or additional constraints (e.g., rated bolt strength).
\end{enumerate}
\end{instructbox}
