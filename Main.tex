\section{Chromosome Organization}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

\subsection{Introduction to Chromosomes and the Genome}

\begin{diagbox}{1. Introduction to Chromosomes and the Genome}

\textbf{Chromosomes} are structures that contain the genetic material.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item They are complexes of \textbf{DNA and proteins}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
The \textbf{genome} comprises \emph{all} the genetic material that an organism possesses.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Bacterial genome:} a single circular chromosome.
  \item \textbf{Eukaryotic genome:} a complete set of nuclear chromosomes.
  \item Eukaryotes also possess a \textbf{mitochondrial genome}.
  \item Plants additionally possess a \textbf{chloroplast genome}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Main function} of genetic material: store the information required to produce an organism.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Organization of Eukaryotic Chromosomes}

\begin{diagbox}{2. Organization of Eukaryotic Chromosomes}

Eukaryotic species contain \textbf{one or more sets of chromosomes} (several different linear chromosomes).
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Humans have \textbf{2 sets of 23 chromosomes} (46 total in the diploid genome: 6{,}200~Mbp).
  \item Each chromosome contains a \textbf{single, linear molecule of DNA} (tens to hundreds of millions of bp; a few hundred to several thousand genes).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Lower eukaryotes} (e.g.\ yeast, \textit{Saccharomyces cerevisiae}):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Genes are relatively small; few, short introns.
  \item Genome $\approx$ 12~Mbp, organised in 16 chromosomes.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Higher eukaryotes} (e.g.\ mammals):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Genes are long and tend to have many introns (100 to $>$10{,}000~bp).
  \item Diploid human genome: 6{,}200~Mbp in 46 chromosomes.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Four essential chromosomal elements:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Origins of replication} --- interspersed every $\sim$100{,}000~bp.
  \item \textbf{Centromere} --- recognition site for kinetochore proteins.
  \item \textbf{Telomeres} --- specialised sequences at both ends of the linear chromosome.
  \item \textbf{Repetitive sequences} --- near centromeric/telomeric regions and throughout.
\end{enumerate}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Genome Sizes and Repetitive Sequences}

\begin{diagbox}{3. Genome Sizes and Repetitive Sequences}

\textbf{Genome size} is \emph{not} simply related to organismal complexity (C-value paradox).
Eukaryotic species generally have more DNA than bacteria but vary substantially among themselves.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Difference in size is due to repetitive DNA, not extra genes.}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Three categories of repetitive sequences:}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5cm}|p{5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Category} & \textbf{Copy Number} & \textbf{Examples} \\
\hline
Unique (non-repetitive) & Once or a few times & Structural genes, intergenic regions; 41\% of human genome \\
\hline
Moderately repetitive & Hundreds to thousands & rRNA genes, histones, regulatory sequences, transposable elements \\
\hline
Highly repetitive & Tens of thousands to millions & Short sequences (a few to several hundred nt each) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Chromosome Territories}

\begin{diagbox}{4. Chromosome Territories}

During interphase, chromosomes occupy \textbf{distinct regions (territories)} within the nucleus.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Particular chromosomes preferentially occupy specific nuclear regions.
  \item This spatial organisation is non-random and influences gene regulation.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

\begin{diagbox}{5. Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

The DNA--protein complex is called \textbf{chromatin}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Nucleosome} --- the repeating structural unit of eukaryotic chromatin:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Histone octamer:} two copies each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4 (core histones).
  \item \textbf{146~bp} of DNA wrapped $\sim$1.65 times around the octamer.
  \item \textbf{Linker histone H1} binds DNA in the linker region between nucleosomes.
  \item Non-histone proteins also associate with chromatin.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Five types of histones:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item H2A, H2B, H3, H4 --- \emph{core histones} (two of each per octamer).
  \item H1 --- \emph{linker histone} (binds between nucleosomes).
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Heterochromatin versus Euchromatin}

\begin{diagbox}{6. Heterochromatin versus Euchromatin}

The compaction level of interphase chromosomes is \emph{not} completely uniform.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5.5cm}|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Heterochromatin} & \textbf{Euchromatin} \\
\hline
Compaction & Tightly compacted & Less condensed \\
\hline
Transcription & Inactive (in general) & Transcriptionally active \\
\hline
Protein access & Little to no access & DNA accessible to proteins \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Levels of Chromatin Compaction}

\begin{diagbox}{7. Levels of Chromatin Compaction Leading to a Metaphase Chromosome}

Eukaryotic chromosomal DNA undergoes successive levels of compaction:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{2~nm} --- DNA double helix.
  \item \textbf{11~nm} --- DNA wrapping around nucleosomes (``beads on a string'').
  \item \textbf{30~nm} --- Zig-zag (solenoid) structure via histone H1 and non-histone proteins.
  \item \textbf{300~nm} --- Radial loop domains: MARs (matrix attachment regions) bind the nuclear matrix.
  \item \textbf{700~nm} --- Radial loops more compacted.
  \item \textbf{1{,}400~nm} --- Metaphase chromosome; protein filaments (scaffold) give the chromosome its shape.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{DNase~I} cuts chromatin into repeating units containing $\sim$200~bp of DNA, demonstrating the nucleosomal repeat unit.

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Condensin and Cohesin}

\begin{diagbox}{8. Condensin and Cohesin}

Two multiprotein complexes organise metaphase chromosomes, both containing \textbf{SMC proteins} (Structural Maintenance of Chromosomes) that use ATP energy to alter chromosome structure.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{5.5cm}|p{5.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Complex} & \textbf{Timing} & \textbf{Function} \\
\hline
Condensin & Cytoplasmic in interphase; enters nucleus at mitosis & Chromosome condensation; compacts radial loops \\
\hline
Cohesin & Present during S phase onward & Sister chromatid cohesion; also regulates transcription \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Cornelia de Lange Syndrome (CdLS; MIM~122470):} caused by mutations in cohesin complex genes.
Hallmarks: craniofacial dysmorphisms (synophrys, arched eyebrows, thin downturned upper lip, long philtrum), hirsutism,
intellectual disability, microcephaly, growth retardation, and limb anomalies (micromelia, oligodactyly, phocomelia).

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Bacterial Chromosome Organization}

\begin{diagbox}{9. Bacterial Chromosome Organization}

Bacterial chromosomal DNA is typically a \textbf{circular molecule}.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textit{Escherichia coli}: $\sim$4.6~million bp.
  \item \textit{Haemophilus influenzae}: $\sim$1.8~million bp.
  \item Contains thousands of genes, mostly protein-encoding (\textbf{structural genes}).
  \item Non-transcribed DNA between genes = \textbf{intergenic regions}.
  \item \textbf{One origin of replication.}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{The nucleoid:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Region of the cell where the bacterial chromosome resides.
  \item \emph{Not} bounded by a membrane (no nucleus).
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Compaction via loop domains:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item DNA must be compacted $\sim$1{,}000-fold to fit inside the cell.
  \item \textit{E.\ coli} forms 50--100 loop domains, each containing 40{,}000--80{,}000~bp.
  \item Micro-domain number varies with chromosome size and species.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Introduction to Biotechnology}

\begin{diagbox}{10. Introduction to Biotechnology}

\textbf{Biotechnology:} technologies that involve the use of living organisms,
or their products, to benefit humans.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Common uses of microorganisms:}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|p{8cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Application} & \textbf{Examples} \\
\hline
Production of medicines & Antibiotics, vitamins; human insulin in recombinant \textit{E.\ coli} \\
\hline
Food fermentation & Cheese, yogurt, vinegar, wine, beer \\
\hline
Biological control & Plant disease, insect pest, weed control \\
\hline
Symbiotic nitrogen fixation & Prevention of frost formation \\
\hline
Bioremediation & Cleanup of petroleum hydrocarbons and synthetic pollutants \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Human insulin:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item A hormone consisting of two polypeptide chains (A and B).
  \item Regulates glucose uptake into fat and muscle cells; produced by $\beta$~cells of the pancreas.
  \item Persons with insulin-dependent diabetes cannot synthesise enough insulin.
  \item Synthesised in recombinant \textit{E.\ coli} for therapeutic use.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference}

\begin{diagbox}{11. Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference (RNAi)}

\textbf{Gene knockouts (KO):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Used to study how loss of gene function affects an organism.
  \item The genomic DNA is perturbed to \emph{permanently prevent} expression of a specific gene.
  \item No mRNA or protein product from the knocked-out gene.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{RNA interference (RNAi) --- gene knockdown:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) results in \textbf{sequence-specific gene silencing}.
  \item The target mRNA is degraded post-transcriptionally.
  \item The gene is still present; mRNA is made but degraded.
  \item Useful for transient, post-transcriptional knockdown.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
\hline
\textbf{Feature} & \textbf{Knockout} & \textbf{Knockdown (RNAi)} \\
\hline
Gene present? & No & Yes \\
mRNA produced? & No & Yes (then degraded) \\
Protein produced? & No & No (or reduced) \\
Permanence & Permanent & Transient \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gene Cloning}

\begin{diagbox}{12. Gene Cloning}

\textbf{Gene cloning:} isolating and producing many copies of a gene.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Two DNA molecules required:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Chromosomal (genomic) DNA} --- source of the gene of interest.
  \item \textbf{Vector (plasmid) DNA} --- carrier for the insert; replicates independently of host chromosome.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Vector features (example: pMSH6-MYC):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Bacterial origin of replication} (\textit{ori}) for replication in bacteria.
  \item \textbf{Antibiotic resistance gene} (e.g.\ ampicillin resistance --- \textit{amp}$^R$) for selection.
  \item \textbf{ARSH4 + CEN6} for replication and maintenance in yeast.
  \item \textbf{Leucine marker} ($LEU2$) for yeast selection in leucine-deficient medium.
  \item \textbf{Shuttle vector:} can replicate in both bacteria and yeast.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Steps in gene cloning:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Extract and purify genomic DNA from the organism of interest.
  \item Digest both chromosomal DNA and vector with the same restriction enzyme.
  \item Ligate the insert into the vector $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{recombinant vector}.
  \item Transform \textbf{competent cells} (cells capable of taking up DNA).
  \item Select transformants on antibiotic plates.
  \item Identify recombinant clones by \textbf{blue-white screening} (lacZ).
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Blue-white screening (LacZ):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Blue colonies:} vector re-circularised without insert; lacZ intact; $\beta$-galactosidase cleaves X-gal $\Rightarrow$ blue pigment.
  \item \textbf{White colonies:} insert disrupts lacZ $\Rightarrow$ no $\beta$-galactosidase activity $\Rightarrow$ no blue pigment.
  \item Select \emph{white} colonies to find recombinant plasmids.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Amplification:} a single bacterium harbouring a plasmid gives rise to millions of cells overnight ($\sim$25--50 plasmid copies per cell; division every 20~min).

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Restriction Enzymes}

\begin{diagbox}{13. Restriction Enzymes}

\textbf{Restriction endonucleases (restriction enzymes):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Naturally produced by many bacteria to protect against foreign DNA (bacteriophages).
  \item Cleave DNA at specific \textbf{palindromic recognition sequences}.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Palindromic sequences:} the sequence on one strand (read 5$'\!\to\!$3$'$) is identical to the complementary strand read in the opposite direction.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{EcoRI example:}
\[
  5'\text{-}\underbrace{\texttt{G\,A\,A\,T\,T\,C}}_{\text{EcoRI site}}\text{-}3'
  \qquad
  3'\text{-}\underbrace{\texttt{C\,T\,T\,A\,A\,G}}_{}  \text{-}5'
\]
Cuts between G and A on each strand, generating \textbf{sticky (cohesive) ends}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Generating compatible ends:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Both insert and vector digested with the same enzyme $\Rightarrow$ complementary overhangs.
  \item DNA ligase seals the nick to form a \textbf{recombinant molecule}.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\begin{diagbox}{14. Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\textbf{PCR:} a method to amplify a specific DNA sequence \emph{without} vectors or host cells.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Required starting materials:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Template DNA} --- contains the region to be amplified.
  \item \textbf{Two oligonucleotide primers} ($\sim$15--20~nt) complementary to each end of the target.
  \item \textbf{Deoxynucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs)} --- precursors for DNA synthesis.
  \item \textbf{Taq polymerase} --- thermostable DNA polymerase from \textit{Thermus aquaticus}; survives high-temperature denaturation steps.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{The three steps of each PCR cycle:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Denaturation} ($\sim$94--96$^\circ$C) --- strands separate.
  \item \textbf{Annealing} ($\sim$50--65$^\circ$C) --- primers bind to complementary template.
  \item \textbf{Extension/Synthesis} ($\sim$72$^\circ$C) --- Taq extends from each primer.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Amplification:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item 20 cycles $\Rightarrow$ $2^{20} \approx 1{,}000{,}000$-fold increase.
  \item 30 cycles $\Rightarrow$ $2^{30} \approx 1{,}000{,}000{,}000$-fold increase.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Site-Directed Mutagenesis}

\begin{diagbox}{15. Site-Directed Mutagenesis}

\textbf{Site-directed mutagenesis:} alters the sequence of a cloned gene at a \emph{specific, defined position}.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Denature the vector + insert to single-stranded DNA.
  \item Anneal a synthetic primer containing the desired mutation (creates a mismatch).
  \item Extend to produce a heteroduplex containing one mutant strand.
  \item Transform into bacteria; identify mutant by DNA sequencing.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Uses:} study how specific mutations affect:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Gene expression.
  \item Protein function.
  \item Organismal phenotype.
\end{itemize}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting Techniques}

\begin{diagbox}{16. Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting Techniques}

\textbf{Gel electrophoresis} separates DNA (or RNA/protein) by size in an electric field.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Negatively charged DNA migrates from the negative electrode (cathode) toward the positive electrode (anode) (``run to red!'').
  \item Smaller fragments migrate faster through the gel matrix.
  \item \textbf{Ethidium bromide} (or safer alternatives) intercalates into DNA and fluoresces under UV light.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Topology and migration rate (slowest to fastest):}
\[
  \text{Nicked circle} > \text{Linear} > \text{Supercoiled}
\]

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Electrophoretic Mobility Shift Assay (EMSA):}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Detects protein--DNA interactions.
  \item A protein bound to a labelled DNA fragment slows its migration (``shift'').
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Western blot:} detects a specific protein.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Proteins separated by SDS-PAGE.
  \item Transferred to nylon membrane.
  \item Primary antibody binds the target protein.
  \item Secondary antibody (linked to alkaline phosphatase) binds the primary antibody.
  \item Addition of XP (5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl phosphate) $\Rightarrow$ dark purple band.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Northern blot:} detects a specific RNA (mRNA).
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item mRNA isolated and separated by gel electrophoresis.
  \item Blotted to nylon membrane.
  \item Hybridised with a labelled DNA probe complementary to the target mRNA.
  \item Labelled bands appear where complementary mRNA is present.
\end{enumerate}

\end{diagbox}

\subsection{Topic 12 --- Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

\begin{planbox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{12\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Standard]}
        Describe the composition of a nucleosome core particle, including the number and types of histone proteins present and the amount of DNA wrapped around it.

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        A researcher treats isolated nuclei with increasing concentrations of DNase~I and separates the digestion products by gel electrophoresis.
        (a) What band pattern (in base pairs) is expected at low DNase~I concentrations, and why?
        (b) What structural feature of chromatin explains this pattern?
        (c) What happens to the pattern at very high DNase~I concentrations, and what does this imply about nuclease-sensitive sites?

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        Histone H1 is described as a ``linker histone.''
        (a) Where precisely does H1 bind relative to the nucleosome core?
        (b) How does H1 binding contribute to the formation of the 30-nm fibre?
        (c) If H1 is experimentally removed from chromatin, predict the structural consequence and its effect on gene transcription.

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        A eukaryotic chromosome contains 150~million base pairs of DNA.
        (a) How many nucleosome core particles are present, given that 146~bp wraps each octamer and the linker DNA averages 54~bp per nucleosome?
        (b) If the DNA contour length before packaging is $L_0$ and the 11-nm fibre is $\sim$6-fold shorter, estimate the compaction at the nucleosome level.
        (c) What additional compaction is achieved by forming the 30-nm fibre from the 11-nm fibre?

\end{enumerate}
\end{planbox}

\subsection{Topic 13 --- Levels of Chromatin Compaction}

\begin{planbox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{13\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Standard]}
        List the six successive levels of chromatin compaction from the DNA double helix to the metaphase chromosome, including the diameter at each level.

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        Compare and contrast heterochromatin and euchromatin with respect to:
        (a) degree of compaction during interphase,
        (b) transcriptional activity,
        (c) histone modification patterns,
        (d) accessibility to transcription factors.

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        Matrix attachment regions (MARs) anchor chromatin loops to the nuclear matrix.
        (a) Explain how the formation of radial loop domains contributes to DNA compaction.
        (b) If an average loop in a human chromosome contains 100{,}000~bp, and a given chromosome has 300 such loops, calculate the total DNA length in that chromosome.
        (c) Explain how loop domain organisation might influence the regulation of adjacent genes.

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Difficult]}
        During the cell cycle, chromatin transitions between interphase and metaphase states.
        (a) Describe the role of condensin in chromosome condensation, including where condensin localises during interphase versus mitosis.
        (b) Explain how SMC proteins use ATP to compact chromosomes.
        (c) What would be the consequence for cell division if condensin function were abolished?

\end{enumerate}
\end{planbox}

\subsection{Topic 14 --- Genome Size and Repetitive Sequences}

\begin{planbox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{14\Alph*.}]

  \item \textbf{[5 pts --- Standard]}
        Define the three major categories of repetitive sequences in eukaryotic genomes (unique, moderately repetitive, highly repetitive), and give one example of each.

\subsection{Topic 12 --- Nucleosomes and Chromatin Structure}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{12\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: The nucleosome core particle has a precisely defined composition and DNA wrap length that must be memorised.}

        A nucleosome core particle consists of:
        \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item A \textbf{histone octamer}: two copies each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4 (8 histone molecules total).
          \item \textbf{146~bp} of double-stranded DNA wrapped $\approx$1.65 times around the octamer.
          \item The linker histone H1 (not part of the core) binds the DNA where it enters and exits the octamer.
        \end{itemize}

  \item \textit{Rationale: DNase~I preferentially cleaves linker DNA (between nucleosomes), revealing the $\sim$200-bp nucleosomal repeat unit.}

        (a) At low DNase~I concentrations, a \textbf{ladder pattern} of bands at multiples of $\sim$200~bp (200, 400, 600, 800~bp, etc.) is expected because DNase~I cuts predominantly in the linker regions between nucleosomes.

        (b) The ladder reflects the regular spacing of nucleosomes: each repeat unit is $\sim$200~bp (146~bp core + $\sim$54~bp linker).

        (c) At very high DNase~I concentrations, even the DNA within the core particle is accessible at \textbf{nuclease-hypersensitive sites}, yielding a faint smear with a predominant $\sim$146-bp protected fragment (the core particle DNA). This demonstrates that the linker region and certain regulatory regions are more exposed than the tightly wrapped core DNA.

  \item \textit{Rationale: H1 bridges adjacent nucleosomes and drives formation of the 30-nm fibre.}

        (a) H1 binds to the \textbf{linker DNA} at the entry/exit points of DNA on the nucleosome core, securing the last few bp of DNA on the octamer.

        (b) H1 promotes the \textbf{zig-zag (30-nm) fibre} by bridging neighbouring nucleosomes and neutralising the negatively charged linker DNA, allowing nucleosomes to stack.

        (c) Removal of H1 causes \textbf{decompaction} of the 30-nm fibre back to the 11-nm ``beads on a string'' state. Genes in these regions become more accessible to transcription factors, leading to \textbf{increased transcriptional activity}.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Each nucleosome occupies 146 + 54 = 200~bp; total nucleosome number = total bp / 200. Compaction ratios follow from geometry.}

        (a) Number of nucleosomes $= \dfrac{150{,}000{,}000\;\text{bp}}{200\;\text{bp/nucleosome}} = \mathbf{750{,}000}$ nucleosomes.

        (b) The DNA contour length $= 150\times10^6\;\text{bp} \times 0.34\;\text{nm/bp} = 51\;\text{mm}$.
        At the 11-nm (nucleosome array) level, the DNA is $\sim$6-fold compacted, giving a fibre length of $\approx\mathbf{8.5\;\text{mm}}$.

        (c) The 30-nm fibre further compacts the 11-nm fibre by $\approx$\textbf{6-fold} (i.e.\ from 11-nm to 30-nm diameter accompanies a $\sim$6$\times$ linear compaction), yielding a total compaction of $\approx$36-fold over naked DNA at this stage.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 13 --- Levels of Chromatin Compaction}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{13\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: The six compaction levels must be memorised with their characteristic dimensions.}

        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\arabic*.]
          \item \textbf{2~nm} --- DNA double helix.
          \item \textbf{11~nm} --- Nucleosome arrays (``beads on a string'').
          \item \textbf{30~nm} --- Zig-zag/solenoid fibre (H1 and non-histone proteins).
          \item \textbf{300~nm} --- Radial loop domains (MARs anchor loops to nuclear matrix).
          \item \textbf{700~nm} --- Further compacted radial loops.
          \item \textbf{1{,}400~nm} --- Metaphase chromosome (protein scaffold maintains shape).
        \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Heterochromatin and euchromatin differ fundamentally in compaction and accessibility.}

        (a) \textbf{Heterochromatin} is tightly compacted during interphase; \textbf{euchromatin} is less condensed.

        (b) Heterochromatin is transcriptionally \textbf{inactive}; euchromatin is transcriptionally \textbf{active}.

        (c) Heterochromatin is enriched in \textbf{repressive marks} (e.g.\ H3K9me3, H3K27me3) and \textbf{hypoacetylated} histones. Euchromatin carries \textbf{activating marks} (e.g.\ H3K4me3, H3K27ac) and \textbf{hyperacetylated} histones.

        (d) Heterochromatin is largely \textbf{inaccessible} to transcription factors and RNA polymerase; euchromatin DNA is \textbf{accessible}.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Loop domain size $\times$ loop number = total DNA; loops isolate gene regulatory domains.}

        (a) MARs tether the base of each chromatin loop to the nuclear matrix. Each loop therefore forms a miniature compacted domain, contributing an additional $\sim$10-fold compaction over the 30-nm fibre.

        (b) Total DNA $= 300\;\text{loops} \times 100{,}000\;\text{bp/loop} \times 0.34\;\text{nm/bp} = 10.2\;\text{mm}$.

        (c) Because each loop is a \textbf{topologically isolated domain}, regulatory elements (enhancers, silencers) within one loop act primarily on genes within the same loop. This prevents inappropriate cross-activation of genes in adjacent loops and enables precise spatial gene regulation.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Condensin drives mitotic compaction; loss of function prevents cells from segregating chromosomes.}

        (a) During interphase, condensin resides in the \textbf{cytoplasm}. At the onset of mitosis (prophase), condensin is imported into the nucleus, binds chromosomes, and \textbf{compacts the radial loop domains} into the highly condensed metaphase chromosome.

        (b) SMC proteins form a ring-like structure with a hinge domain and two ATPase heads. ATP hydrolysis drives \textbf{conformational changes} that entrap and actively compact DNA strands, reducing their length.

        (c) Without condensin, chromosomes \textbf{fail to condense properly}, resulting in tangled chromosomes that cannot be correctly attached to spindle fibres. The outcome is \textbf{mitotic arrest} and/or \textbf{chromosome mis-segregation}, leading to aneuploidy and cell death.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 14 --- Genome Size and Repetitive Sequences}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{14\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: Three categories must be defined by copy number, and an example provided for each.}

        \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Unique (non-repetitive)}: present once or a few times; comprises structural genes and intergenic sequences ($\sim$41\% of human genome). \textit{Example}: the $\beta$-globin gene.
          \item \textbf{Moderately repetitive}: present hundreds to thousands of times. \textit{Example}: rRNA genes, histone genes, transposable elements (e.g.\ SINEs, LINEs).
          \item \textbf{Highly repetitive}: present tens of thousands to millions of times; each copy is short. \textit{Example}: satellite DNA (simple tandem repeats near centromeres).
        \end{itemize}

  \item \textit{Rationale: The C-value paradox is a classic genetics concept; repetitive DNA explains size differences.}

        (a) The \textbf{C-value paradox}: genome size (C-value) does not correlate simply with gene number or organismal complexity.

        (b) \textbf{Repetitive DNA sequences} (primarily highly and moderately repetitive) account for the size difference.

        (c) Two mechanisms:
        \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Transposon amplification}: transposable elements replicate and insert themselves at new genomic locations, increasing copy number.
          \item \textbf{Unequal crossing over (recombination)}: misaligned homologous chromosomes recombine unequally, expanding tandem repeat arrays.
        \end{itemize}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Quantitative comparison of yeast and human genomes.}

        \begin{center}
        \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
        \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|}
        \hline
        \textbf{Feature} & \textbf{\textit{S.\ cerevisiae}} & \textbf{\textit{H.\ sapiens}} \\
        \hline
        Genome size & $\sim$12~Mbp & $\sim$3{,}100~Mbp (haploid) \\
        \hline
        Haploid chromosome no. & 16 & 23 \\
        \hline
        Introns & Very few, short ($<$500~bp) & Many, long (100 to $>$10{,}000~bp) \\
        \hline
        Unique sequences & $\sim$70\% & $\sim$41\% \\
        \hline
        \end{tabular}
        \end{center}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Centromere, telomere, origin, and sequence element functions on linear chromosomes.}

        (a) The four elements:
        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Origins of replication} --- initiation sites for DNA replication ($\sim$every 100{,}000~bp in eukaryotes).
          \item \textbf{Centromere} --- site of kinetochore assembly; essential for correct attachment to the mitotic spindle.
          \item \textbf{Telomeres} --- repetitive sequences at both ends that protect chromosomes from exonucleolytic degradation and prevent end-joining.
          \item \textbf{Repetitive sequences} --- contribute to structural domains and, in some cases, gene regulation.
        \end{enumerate}

        (b) A chromosome lacking a centromere cannot attach to the spindle, and \textbf{fails to segregate} to daughter cells. It is typically lost within a few cell divisions.

        (c) Because linear chromosomes cannot be fully replicated at their ends by conventional DNA polymerases (the ``end-replication problem''), telomeres provide a buffer of repetitive sequence. Telomerase replenishes these sequences. Without telomeres, chromosomes \textbf{shorten with each division}, eventually losing essential genes, triggering DNA damage responses and cellular senescence or apoptosis.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 15 --- Bacterial Chromosome Organization}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{15\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: The nucleoid is the defining feature of prokaryotic chromosome organisation.}

        The bacterial chromosome resides in the \textbf{nucleoid} region --- an irregular, electron-dense area within the cytoplasm. It is \textbf{not enclosed by a membrane}. The bacterial chromosome is typically a single, \textbf{circular double-stranded DNA} molecule (unlike the multiple linear chromosomes of eukaryotes), and bacteria generally have \textbf{one chromosome} (though some have two, e.g.\ \textit{Vibrio cholerae}).

  \item \textit{Rationale: Contour length and fold-compaction calculations.}

        (a) Contour length $= 4.6\times10^6\;\text{bp} \times 0.34\;\text{nm/bp} = 1.564\;\text{mm} \approx \mathbf{1.6\;\text{mm}}$.

        (b) Cell length $\approx 2\;\mu\text{m} = 0.002\;\text{mm}$. Compaction $= 1.564 / 0.002 \approx \mathbf{780}$-fold (conventionally stated as $\sim$1{,}000-fold).

        (c) Two mechanisms:
        \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{DNA supercoiling}: negative supercoils are introduced by DNA gyrase, compacting the circular chromosome.
          \item \textbf{Nucleoid-associated proteins (NAPs)}: proteins such as HU, H-NS, and IHF bind and bend DNA, organising it into loop domains analogous to eukaryotic MARs.
        \end{itemize}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Verify loop domain arithmetic; compare with eukaryotic radial loops.}

        (a) With 50 loops $\times$ 80{,}000~bp/loop $= 4.0$~Mbp; with 100 loops $\times$ 40{,}000~bp/loop $= 4.0$~Mbp. Both bracket the 4.6-Mbp genome with intermediate values. \textbf{Verified.}

        (b) In bacteria, loop domain bases are anchored by \textbf{nucleoid-associated proteins} (e.g.\ HU, H-NS, MukBEF --- an SMC complex) and by \textbf{RNA molecules} that tether DNA loops.

        (c) Both bacteria (loop domains) and eukaryotes (radial loop domains) organise chromosomal DNA into topologically isolated loop structures. Key differences: eukaryotic loops are anchored to the \textbf{nuclear matrix} via MARs; bacterial loops are anchored to the \textbf{nucleoid core} by proteins and RNA. Eukaryotic loops are larger and more numerous.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Condensin compacts; cohesin coheres and regulates; NIPBL loads cohesin.}

        (a) \textbf{Condensin} drives chromosome \emph{condensation} (compaction of radial loops at mitosis). \textbf{Cohesin} maintains \emph{cohesion} between sister chromatids from S phase until anaphase; it also facilitates transcriptional regulation by promoting enhancer--promoter looping.

        (b) Cohesin forms a ring-shaped SMC complex that topologically entraps two DNA strands. When an enhancer and a promoter are each located within the same cohesin-encircled loop, direct interaction is facilitated, activating transcription. Loss of cohesin impairs these \textbf{enhancer--promoter contacts}, dysregulating developmental gene expression.

        (c) Cornelia de Lange Syndrome hallmarks: (i) craniofacial features --- synophrys (fused eyebrows), arched eyebrows, thin downturned upper lip, long philtrum; (ii) \textbf{intellectual disability} and microcephaly; (iii) \textbf{growth retardation} and limb anomalies (oligodactyly, micromelia, or phocomelia).

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 16 --- Restriction Enzymes and Gene Cloning}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{16\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: Definition and palindrome recognition are foundational concepts.}

        A \textbf{restriction endonuclease} is an enzyme produced by bacteria that recognises and cleaves specific short DNA sequences, protecting the cell from foreign DNA. A \textbf{palindromic sequence} reads identically on both strands in the 5$'\!\to\!$3$'$ direction. EcoRI recognition and cleavage:
        \[
          5'\!\text{-}\mathbf{G} \downarrow \text{AATTC-}3'
          \qquad
          3'\!\text{-CTTAA} \uparrow \mathbf{G}\text{-}5'
        \]
        Cleavage between G and A on each strand generates 4-nt 5$'$ sticky ends (\texttt{AATT}).

  \item \textit{Rationale: The complete cloning workflow integrates multiple molecular techniques.}

        (a) Workflow: (i) Extract and purify genomic DNA from human cells. (ii) Digest genomic DNA and plasmid vector with EcoRI. (iii) Mix digested fragments and vector; add T4 DNA ligase to join compatible ends. (iv) Transform competent \textit{E.\ coli} cells with ligation mixture. (v) Plate on ampicillin-containing agar with X-gal and IPTG. (vi) Select \textbf{white colonies} (recombinant plasmids have disrupted \textit{lacZ}). (vii) Pick white colonies, extract plasmid, verify insert by PCR and sequencing.

        (b) \textbf{Competent cells} have been chemically or electrically treated to allow plasmid DNA uptake. Successfully transformed cells are identified by \textbf{growth on ampicillin} (only cells harbouring the $amp^R$ plasmid survive).

        (c) \textbf{Blue-white screening}: lacZ-encoded \textbf{$\beta$-galactosidase} cleaves X-gal into a blue product. An insert disrupts lacZ $\Rightarrow$ no $\beta$-galactosidase $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{white colony} (recombinant). Uninterrupted lacZ $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{blue colony} (re-circularised vector).

  \item \textit{Rationale: Shuttle vectors must carry host-specific elements for two different organisms.}

        (a) The four elements:
        \begin{center}
        \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
        \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        \textbf{Element} & \textbf{Function} & \textbf{Host} \\
        \hline
        Bacterial \textit{ori} & Replication in bacteria & Bacteria \\
        $amp^R$ & Selection in bacteria & Bacteria \\
        ARSH4 + CEN6 & Replication \& maintenance in yeast & Yeast \\
        $LEU2$ & Selection in yeast & Yeast \\
        \hline
        \end{tabular}
        \end{center}

        (b) $LEU2$ encodes an enzyme in leucine biosynthesis. Yeast strains used as hosts carry a \textit{leu2} deletion (auxotrophic); they cannot grow on leucine-deficient medium \emph{unless} they carry the $LEU2$ plasmid. Growth on $-$Leu medium \textbf{selects for cells harbouring the plasmid}.

        (c) \textbf{Antibiotic resistance} (e.g.\ $amp^R$) kills cells that lack the plasmid and is used in bacteria. \textbf{Auxotrophic markers} complement a nutritional deficiency and allow growth on selective medium lacking the nutrient; they are preferred in yeast because yeast are naturally resistant to most antibiotics at useful concentrations.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Probabilistic insertion, antibiotic selection, and copy-number amplification.}

        (a) With thousands of genomic fragments and only one vector molecule per ligation event, the probability that any given fragment encounters and ligates into a given vector molecule is very low. Additionally, the ligation conditions are designed so that \textbf{one insert enters one vector}: at low insert:vector molar ratios, re-ligation of a single insert per vector is statistically favoured.

        (b) Only bacteria that have taken up a plasmid acquire the $amp^R$ gene and can produce $\beta$-lactamase, which inactivates ampicillin. Cells without the plasmid \textbf{die in ampicillin medium}.

        (c) Starting: 1 cell/mL. After 20 divisions: $2^{20} = 1{,}048{,}576$ cells/mL $\approx \mathbf{10^6}$ cells/mL.
        Plasmid copies/cell $\approx 37$ (midpoint of 25--50). Total copies/mL $\approx 10^6 \times 37 \approx \mathbf{3.7\times10^7}$ copies/mL.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 17 --- Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{17\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: PCR components and cycle steps are foundational knowledge.}

        \textbf{Four starting materials:}
        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Template DNA} --- provides the sequence to amplify.
          \item \textbf{Two primers} (15--20~nt each) --- define the boundaries of the amplified region; must be complementary to the flanking sequences.
          \item \textbf{dNTPs} (dATP, dTTP, dGTP, dCTP) --- building blocks for new DNA strands.
          \item \textbf{Taq polymerase} --- thermostable DNA polymerase; synthesises the new strand from primers.
        \end{enumerate}

        \textbf{Three steps per cycle:}
        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item \textbf{Denaturation} $\sim$94--96$^\circ$C --- hydrogen bonds broken; strands separate.
          \item \textbf{Annealing} $\sim$50--65$^\circ$C --- primers bind complementary template.
          \item \textbf{Extension} $\sim$72$^\circ$C --- Taq extends primers 5$'\!\to\!$3$'$.
        \end{enumerate}

  \item \textit{Rationale: Exponential amplification formula; practical vs.\ theoretical yield.}

        (a) After 30 cycles: $2^{30} = 1{,}073{,}741{,}824 \approx \mathbf{10^9}$ copies.

        (b) Practical yields are lower because: (i) \textbf{Taq polymerase efficiency} is not 100\% per extension step; (ii) \textbf{primer competition and non-specific annealing} reduce productive amplification; (iii) \textbf{dNTP and primer depletion} in later cycles limit the reaction; (iv) \textbf{product inhibition} (double-stranded product must re-denature).

        (c) Taq polymerase is isolated from \textit{Thermus aquaticus}, a bacterium that thrives in hot springs ($\sim$70--75$^\circ$C). It remains active at the \textbf{denaturation temperature} ($\sim$95$^\circ$C) used in PCR, unlike mesophilic DNA polymerases which are irreversibly denatured.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Primer design and melting temperature calculation.}

        (a) The \textbf{DNA sequence flanking the target locus} must be known, because primers must be complementary to these flanking sequences. Without prior sequence knowledge, primers cannot be designed.

        (b) The primer is 18~nt; 60\% GC $\Rightarrow$ G+C $= 0.60\times18 = 10.8\approx11$; A+T $= 7$.
        \[
          T_m = 4(11) + 2(7) = 44 + 14 = \mathbf{58^\circ\text{C}}
        \]

        (c) \textbf{Too low annealing temperature}: primers bind \textbf{non-specifically} to partially complementary sites elsewhere in the genome $\Rightarrow$ multiple non-specific bands. \textbf{Too high annealing temperature}: primers \textbf{fail to bind} (even correct sites), reducing or abolishing amplification.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Site-directed mutagenesis is an application of PCR-based methods.}

        (a) Steps: (i) Denature the cloned insert + vector to single-stranded DNA. (ii) Anneal a synthetic primer containing the desired mutation (creating a 1--3 nt mismatch at the mutation site). (iii) Extend the primer using Taq (or a high-fidelity polymerase) to copy the full plasmid. (iv) Treat with \textit{Dpn}I (which digests methylated, parental DNA) to eliminate the wild-type template. (v) Transform into bacteria; individual colonies carry plasmids.

        (b) The mutant clone is identified by \textbf{DNA sequencing} of the insert, confirming the desired mutation and the absence of unintended mutations.

        (c) Experimental strategy: (i) Express the wild-type and mutant proteins in a suitable expression system. (ii) Purify both proteins. (iii) Measure enzyme activity using a standard \textbf{biochemical assay} (e.g.\ substrate consumption or product formation over time). (iv) Compare kinetic parameters ($k_{\rm cat}$, $K_M$) between wild-type and mutant. (v) Include a \textbf{catalytic-dead mutant control} (known active-site residue mutated) as a negative control.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 18 --- Gel Electrophoresis and Molecular Blotting}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{18\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: Charge and size are the two key properties governing gel electrophoresis of DNA.}

        DNA is negatively charged due to the phosphate backbone. In an electric field, it migrates from the \textbf{negative electrode (cathode) toward the positive electrode (anode)} (``run to red''). In agarose, smaller fragments migrate faster through the gel matrix because they encounter less resistance; larger fragments are retarded. \textbf{Size} (number of base pairs, for linear dsDNA) determines migration rate.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Topology determines effective hydrodynamic size.}

        (a) Migration order (slowest to fastest): $\mathbf{\text{Nicked circle} > \text{Linear} > \text{Supercoiled}}$.
        Nicked circles are relaxed and migrate slowly due to their large effective radius. Linear DNA migrates at an intermediate rate. Supercoiled DNA is compact and migrates fastest.

        (b) Ethidium bromide intercalates into DNA, reducing the negative supercoiling of supercoiled DNA. At low concentrations, EtBr reduces (then eliminates) negative supercoils, converting supercoiled to a more relaxed form. At saturating concentrations, EtBr introduces positive supercoils, causing the formerly fast-migrating supercoiled band to slow down and migrate \textbf{slower than linear} DNA.

        (c) A 1-kb ladder contains DNA fragments at known sizes (e.g.\ 100, 200, 500, 1{,}000, 2{,}000, 3{,}000~bp). After electrophoresis, plot \textbf{log(size) vs.\ migration distance} for ladder bands to construct a standard curve. Read off the migration distance of the unknown fragment and interpolate its size from the standard curve.

  \item \textit{Rationale: EMSA detects protein--DNA interaction via reduced mobility.}

        (a) Without protein: the labelled DNA fragment migrates as a single band at its expected position. With protein: the protein--DNA complex has a higher molecular mass and migrates \textbf{more slowly}, appearing as a shifted band higher in the gel (``shift'').

        (b) To determine $K_D$: add increasing concentrations of the transcription factor to a fixed amount of labelled DNA. At each concentration, quantify the fraction of DNA in the shifted (bound) versus free band. The $K_D$ equals the protein concentration at which \textbf{50\% of the DNA is bound} (half-shifted).

        (c) \textbf{Specificity control}: (i) Compete the interaction with unlabelled specific DNA (cold competitor) at increasing concentrations --- the shift should disappear. (ii) Add excess unlabelled \emph{non-specific} DNA --- the shift should remain. (iii) Use a mutated probe that lacks the binding site --- no shift should be observed.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Northern detects RNA, Western detects protein; both use hybridisation/antibody-based detection.}

        (a) \textbf{Northern blot}: isolate mRNA $\to$ separate by agarose gel electrophoresis under denaturing conditions $\to$ blot to nylon membrane $\to$ hybridise with labelled DNA probe complementary to target mRNA $\to$ wash off non-specific probe $\to$ detect labelled bands by autoradiography or fluorescence.
        \textbf{Western blot}: separate proteins by SDS-PAGE $\to$ transfer to nitrocellulose or PVDF membrane $\to$ block with non-specific protein $\to$ add primary antibody (binds target protein) $\to$ add secondary antibody conjugated to alkaline phosphatase $\to$ add XP substrate $\to$ detect dark purple band.

        (b) \textbf{Northern}: $\beta$-globin mRNA is present in brain cells but absent in intestinal cells; brain cells \textbf{express} the $\beta$-globin gene while intestinal cells do \textbf{not}. \textbf{Western}: $\beta$-globin protein is present in brain but not intestinal cells, consistent with the mRNA expression data. Together they confirm tissue-specific expression at both RNA and protein levels.

        (c) Two explanations for an oversized Northern band: (i) \textbf{Alternative splicing} of the $\beta$-globin pre-mRNA in this tissue retains an intron, producing a larger transcript. (ii) The probe detects a \textbf{related but larger transcript} from a homologous gene (cross-hybridisation).

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}

\subsection{Topic 19 --- Gene Knockouts and RNA Interference}

\begin{keybox}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, label=\textbf{19\Alph*.}]

  \item \textit{Rationale: Distinguish knockout (permanent, DNA-level) from knockdown (transient, RNA-level).}

        \begin{center}
        \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.4}
        \begin{tabular}{|l|c|c|c|}
        \hline
        \textbf{} & \textbf{Gene present?} & \textbf{mRNA?} & \textbf{Protein?} \\
        \hline
        Gene knockout & No & No & No \\
        \hline
        RNAi knockdown & Yes & No (degraded) & No (or greatly reduced) \\
        \hline
        \end{tabular}
        \end{center}

  \item \textit{Rationale: RNAi mechanism is RISC-mediated cleavage; experimental design integrates multiple techniques.}

        (a) \textbf{Complete knockout} permanently eliminates gene function, but may be lethal if the gene is essential, preventing study of its role in later processes. \textbf{RNAi knockdown} reduces (not eliminates) gene expression transiently and can be conditionally induced, but incomplete knockdown may allow residual protein function; off-target silencing is also a concern.

        (b) Mechanism of RNAi:
        \begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em]
          \item Exogenous or endogenous \textbf{dsRNA} is processed by the RNase~III enzyme \textbf{Dicer} into short interfering RNA (siRNA) duplexes of $\sim$21--23~nt.
          \item siRNA is incorporated into the \textbf{RISC} (RNA-Induced Silencing Complex).
          \item The passenger strand is discarded; the guide strand remains and directs RISC to the \textbf{complementary mRNA}.
          \item The RISC endonuclease (Argonaute/Slicer) \textbf{cleaves} the target mRNA, which is then degraded.
        \end{enumerate}

        (c) Experimental design: (i) Design siRNA complementary to the Gene~X mRNA. (ii) Transfect into synchronised cells at G1. (iii) Add BrdU and monitor entry into S phase (BrdU incorporation). (iv) Compare BrdU-positive cells in siRNA-treated versus control (scrambled siRNA) cells. (v) Verify knockdown by RT-qPCR and Western blot for Gene~X mRNA and protein. (vi) A \textbf{decrease in BrdU incorporation} in Gene~X-depleted cells indicates that Gene~X is required for S phase entry.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Recombinant insulin production involves reverse genetics and expression systems.}

        (a) Steps: (i) Isolate mRNA for human insulin (or A/B chain mRNAs) from pancreatic $\beta$~cells. (ii) Use \textbf{reverse transcriptase} to synthesise cDNA. (iii) Amplify the insulin cDNA by \textbf{PCR} with appropriate primers. (iv) Clone the cDNA into a bacterial \textbf{expression vector} containing a strong promoter (e.g.\ $T7$ or \textit{lac}). (v) Transform into \textit{E.\ coli}; induce expression. (vi) Purify the expressed insulin protein.

        (b) Bacteria are preferred because: they grow rapidly and cheaply; they can produce large quantities of recombinant protein; the biology is well-understood; and scale-up is straightforward.

        (c) \textbf{Challenge}: \textit{E.\ coli} lacks the machinery to form \textbf{disulfide bonds} in the cytoplasm and does not process proinsulin to mature insulin (proinsulin has a connecting C peptide). \textbf{Solution}: Produce the A and B chains separately, then chemically or enzymatically fold and oxidise them to form the correct disulfide bonds in vitro; alternatively, express proinsulin in the periplasm (oxidising environment) and cleave the C-peptide enzymatically.

  \item \textit{Rationale: Cohesin-mediated gene regulation and disease mechanism.}

        (a) Cohesin forms loops that juxtapose enhancers and promoters, activating specific developmental genes. Loss of cohesin disrupts these loops $\Rightarrow$ enhancers can no longer reach their target promoters $\Rightarrow$ \textbf{reduced expression of genes} normally activated by those enhancers. This leads to dysregulation of multiple developmental pathways simultaneously.

        (b) Different missense mutations alter the cohesin complex to varying degrees: a mutation in a non-critical residue may only \textbf{partially impair} the protein-interaction surface of the ring, causing mild dysregulation. A mutation at a critical ATPase or hinge residue causes \textbf{severe loss of function}. Additionally, dominant-negative effects, haploinsufficiency, and genetic background modifiers contribute to variable expressivity.

        (c) \textbf{NIPBL} (Nipped-B-like) is the \textbf{cohesin loader}; it recruits the cohesin ring to chromosomes at specific loading sites (especially replication forks and gene regulatory elements). Without functional NIPBL, cohesin is not efficiently loaded onto chromosomes, reducing the number of active enhancer--promoter loops and impairing gene regulation. A \textit{de~novo} mutation means the patient has a \textbf{new (non-inherited) mutation} not present in either parent; because CdLS is typically caused by heterozygous loss-of-function mutations (\textbf{haploinsufficiency}), a single defective copy of \textit{NIPBL} is sufficient to cause the syndrome.

\end{enumerate}
\end{keybox}
