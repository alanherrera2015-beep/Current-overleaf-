\begin{conceptbox}[Quick Guide to Math Symbols Used in This Document]
Every time you see a letter or symbol in a formula, it stands for a number with units.
Here is a cheat-sheet for the most common ones:

\smallskip
\begin{tabular}{cp{0.82\linewidth}}% p-column lets long descriptions wrap
  $m$ & mass -- how much \textbf{stuff} is in an object (kilograms, kg) \\
  $g$ & gravity pull -- always $9.8$ m/s$^2$ downward on Earth \\
  $W = mg$ & weight -- how hard gravity pulls the object (Newtons, N) \\
  $F$ & any force -- a push or a pull (Newtons, N) \\
  $k$ & spring stiffness -- bigger $k$ means stiffer spring (N/m) \\
  $x$ or $\delta$ & how much a spring is squished or stretched (meters, m) \\
  $\tau$ & torque -- a \textbf{turning} or \textbf{spinning} force (N$\cdot$m) \\
  $r$ & how far from the turning point a force is applied (meters, m) \\
  $v$ & speed (meters per second, m/s) \\
  $KE$ & kinetic energy -- energy of motion ($\frac{1}{2}mv^2$, Joules, J) \\
  $PE$ & potential energy -- energy stored by height ($mgh$, Joules, J) \\
  $W_{\text{work}}$ & work done by a force (Joules, J); \textit{note: $W$ by itself usually means weight ($mg$); context tells which meaning applies} \\
  $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ & square root -- the number that, multiplied by itself, gives what's inside \\
  $\sum$ & ``the sum of'' (add everything up) \\
  $\Rightarrow$ & ``which gives us'' or ``therefore'' \\
\end{tabular}

\smallskip
\textbf{Remember:} When you see $A = B$, both sides are equal. You can always multiply
or divide \emph{both} sides by the same number without breaking the equation.
\end{conceptbox}

\section*{Problem 1: Shock Absorber Springs (Advanced)}
%==============================================

\begin{problembox}[Problem 1 -- Chapters 8 \& 10: Asymmetric Spring Suspension]
A 1600 kg SUV has its center of mass $a = 0.80$ m behind the front axle and $b = 1.20$ m
in front of the rear axle (wheelbase $L = 2.00$ m). The center of mass is also
$d = 0.10$ m to the \emph{right} of the vehicle centerline. The front wheel track
(distance between the two front wheels) is $W = 1.60$ m. The front-left spring has
constant $k_L = 24{,}000$ N/m; the front-right spring has $k_R = 30{,}000$ N/m.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
  \item Find the total vertical force $R_f$ on the front axle.
        (\textit{Hint: take torques about the rear axle.})
  \item Taking torques about the \emph{left} front wheel (left-right axis), find
        the individual spring forces $F_R$ (right) and $F_L$ (left).
  \item Find the compression of each spring ($x_L$ and $x_R$). Are they equal?
  \item Find the total elastic potential energy stored in the two front springs.
  \item When the SUV drives over a speed bump, the dynamic load on the front axle
        momentarily \emph{doubles}. Find the new right-spring compression $x_R'$.
        What percentage increase is this compared to the static value?
\end{enumerate}
\end{problembox}

\section*{Solution 1: Shock Absorber Springs (Advanced)}
%==============================================

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 1(a) -- Total front-axle load]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} Imagine sitting on a seesaw. If you scoot toward one
end, that end drops -- it has to push up harder to stay balanced. A car's weight is
not split 50/50 front and back; it depends on where the heavy engine sits. We will
treat the car like a seesaw balanced between the two axles, and use \emph{torques}
(spinning forces) to figure out how much each axle pushes up.

\smallskip
\textbf{What we know:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $m = 1600$ kg \quad (how heavy the SUV is)
  \item $g = 9.8$ m/s$^2$ \quad (Earth's gravity pull)
  \item $b = 1.20$ m \quad (center of mass is 1.20 m in front of the rear axle)
  \item $L = 2.00$ m \quad (total wheelbase -- distance from rear to front axle)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 1 -- Find the car's total weight.}

Weight = mass $\times$ gravity.
\begin{equation}
W = m \times g = 1600 \times 9.8 = \boxed{15{,}680\;\text{N}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: the SUV weighs 15,680 Newtons. A Newton is about the weight
of a small apple, so this is like stacking 15,680 apples on the car.}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Take torques about the rear axle.}

A \textbf{torque} is a turning force: torque $=$ force $\times$ distance from the pivot.
We choose the rear axle as our pivot (turning point) because that makes the unknown
rear-axle force disappear -- it has \emph{zero} distance from the pivot, so its
torque is zero.

Two torques must balance (since the car is not spinning):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Clockwise torque} from car weight: $\;W \times b = 15{,}680 \times 1.20$
  \item \textbf{Counterclockwise torque} from front axle push-up: $\;R_f \times L = R_f \times 2.00$
\end{itemize}

Setting them equal (balanced seesaw):
\begin{equation}
R_f \times L = W \times b
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: ``front axle force times its distance from pivot'' equals
``car weight times its distance from pivot.''}

Solve for $R_f$ by dividing both sides by $L$:
\begin{equation}
R_f = W \times \frac{b}{L} = 15{,}680 \times \frac{1.20}{2.00}
= 15{,}680 \times 0.60 = \boxed{9{,}408\;\text{N}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: the fraction $\frac{1.20}{2.00} = 0.60$ means the center of
mass is 60\% of the way from the rear axle to the front axle. So the front axle
carries 60\% of the total weight. That is why $9{,}408 = 0.60 \times 15{,}680$.}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 1(b) -- Individual spring forces]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} We just found how hard the two \emph{front} springs
together push up (9,408 N). Now we do the same left-to-right seesaw trick to split
that force between the \emph{left} spring and the \emph{right} spring. Because the
center of mass is slightly to the right, the right spring has to push up harder.

\smallskip
\textbf{What we know:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item $R_f = 9{,}408$ N \quad (total front-axle force, from part~(a))
  \item $W = 1.60$ m \quad (track width -- distance between the two front wheels)
  \item $d = 0.10$ m \quad (center of mass is 0.10 m to the \emph{right} of center)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 1 -- Find the distances from each wheel to the center of mass (CM).}

The half-track is $W/2 = 1.60/2 = 0.80$ m from center to each wheel. The CM is
0.10 m to the right, so:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Left wheel to CM $= 0.80 + 0.10 = \mathbf{0.90}$ m
        \textit{(the CM is farther from the left wheel, so the left spring works
        like the short-side seat of a seesaw)}
  \item Right wheel to CM $= 0.80 - 0.10 = \mathbf{0.70}$ m
        \textit{(the CM is closer to the right wheel)}
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Take torques about the left front wheel to find $F_R$.}

Picking the left wheel as pivot eliminates $F_L$ (it is at the pivot, so its torque
is zero). Balance the torques:
\begin{align}
F_R \times 1.60 &= R_f \times 0.90 \\
\intertext{\textit{(Right spring force $\times$ its distance from left wheel) = (Total front force $\times$ distance from left wheel to CM)}}
F_R &= \frac{9{,}408 \times 0.90}{1.60} = \frac{8{,}467}{1.60} = \boxed{5{,}292\;\text{N (right spring)}}
\end{align}
\textit{In plain words: the right spring carries about 56\% of the front-axle load
($5292/9408 \approx 0.56$) because the CM is closer to it.}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Find $F_L$ using force balance.}

All the upward spring forces must equal the total downward load:
\begin{equation}
F_L + F_R = R_f \quad\Longrightarrow\quad F_L = R_f - F_R = 9{,}408 - 5{,}292 = \boxed{4{,}116\;\text{N (left spring)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Check: $4116 + 5292 = 9408$ \checkmark. The right spring holds about 28\%
more force than the left.}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 1(c) -- Spring compressions]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} Hooke's Law says the harder you push on a spring, the
more it squishes. But a \emph{stiffer} spring squishes less for the same push. Think
of a car spring vs.\ a mattress spring -- the car spring barely moves even under a
huge force. The formula is:
\[
x = \frac{F}{k}
\]
\textit{In plain words: compression $x$ = (force pushing down) $\div$ (spring stiffness).
Bigger force $\Rightarrow$ more squish. Bigger $k$ $\Rightarrow$ less squish.}

\smallskip
\textbf{Left spring} ($k_L = 24{,}000$ N/m, $F_L = 4{,}116$ N):
\begin{equation}
x_L = \frac{F_L}{k_L} = \frac{4{,}116}{24{,}000} = \boxed{0.172\;\text{m} = 17.2\;\text{cm}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $4116 \div 24000 = 0.172$. Then $0.172 \times 100 = 17.2$ cm.}

\textbf{Right spring} ($k_R = 30{,}000$ N/m, $F_R = 5{,}292$ N):
\begin{equation}
x_R = \frac{F_R}{k_R} = \frac{5{,}292}{30{,}000} = \boxed{0.176\;\text{m} = 17.6\;\text{cm}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $5292 \div 30000 = 0.176$. Then $0.176 \times 100 = 17.6$ cm.}

\textbf{Are they equal?} Almost! The difference is only $17.6 - 17.2 = 0.4$ cm (4 mm).
Even though the right spring carries more force \emph{and} is stiffer, the two effects
nearly cancel each other out. Engineers choose spring rates this way on purpose so the
car sits level.

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 1(d) -- Elastic potential energy]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} A squished spring stores energy, just like a stretched
rubber band stores energy. The formula is:
\[
U = \tfrac{1}{2} k x^2
\]
\textit{In plain words: energy stored = (half the stiffness) $\times$ (compression)$^2$.
We square the compression because a spring that's squished twice as far stores
\emph{four} times the energy (not just twice).}

\smallskip
\textbf{Left spring:} $k_L = 24{,}000$ N/m, $x_L = 0.172$ m

First, square the compression: $x_L^2 = 0.172^2 = 0.172 \times 0.172 = 0.029584\;\text{m}^2$
\begin{equation}
U_L = \tfrac{1}{2}(24{,}000)(0.172)^2 = 12{,}000 \times 0.029584 = \mathbf{355.0\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $\frac{1}{2} \times 24000 = 12000$. Then $12000 \times 0.029584 = 355.0$ J.}

\textbf{Right spring:} $k_R = 30{,}000$ N/m, $x_R = 0.176$ m

First, square the compression: $x_R^2 = 0.176^2 = 0.176 \times 0.176 = 0.030976\;\text{m}^2$
\begin{equation}
U_R = \tfrac{1}{2}(30{,}000)(0.176)^2 = 15{,}000 \times 0.030976 = \mathbf{464.6\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $\frac{1}{2} \times 30000 = 15000$. Then $15000 \times 0.030976 = 464.6$ J.}

\textbf{Total energy (add both):}
\begin{equation}
U_{\text{total}} = U_L + U_R = 355.0 + 464.6 = \boxed{819.6\;\text{J} \approx 820\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}
\textit{That is enough energy to lift a 1 kg book about 82 m into the air -- stored
in just two car springs!}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 1(e) -- Dynamic loading (doubled load)]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} When a car hits a speed bump, the road pushes the wheel
upward extra hard for a moment -- this is called a ``dynamic load.'' If the total force
on a spring doubles, Hooke's Law ($x = F/k$) tells us the compression doubles too.
This is because the formula is a straight line: double the force, double the stretch.

\smallskip
\textbf{Step 1 -- Find the new right-spring force.}

The dynamic load doubles the entire front axle, so the right spring force also doubles:
\begin{equation}
F_R' = 2 \times F_R = 2 \times 5{,}292 = 10{,}584\;\text{N}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: the bump pushes up with twice as much force, so the spring
is pushed down with twice as much force.}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Find the new compression using Hooke's Law.}
\begin{equation}
x_R' = \frac{F_R'}{k_R} = \frac{10{,}584}{30{,}000} = \boxed{0.353\;\text{m} = 35.3\;\text{cm}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $10584 \div 30000 = 0.3528 \approx 0.353$ m.}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Percentage increase.}

\textit{``Percentage increase'' means: how much did it grow, compared to where it
started, expressed as a percent?}
\begin{equation}
\text{Percentage increase} = \frac{x_R' - x_R}{x_R} \times 100\%
= \frac{0.353 - 0.176}{0.176} \times 100\%
= \frac{0.177}{0.176} \times 100\% \approx \boxed{100\%}
\end{equation}
\textit{Exactly a 100\% increase, meaning it doubled! Because $F = kx$ is a straight
line, doubling the force \emph{always} doubles the compression. Real car engineers
design the suspension so it can travel at least twice the static compression -- so the
spring does not hit its limit on a bump.}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 2(b) -- Torque equation and bicep force]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} We take torques about the \emph{elbow joint}. Why the
elbow? Because the unknown joint force $\vec{R}$ acts \emph{at} the elbow -- it is
zero distance from the pivot, so its torque is zero. It disappears from the equation!
That leaves us one equation with one unknown: $F_B$.

\textbf{Key geometry fact:} When the forearm is tilted $\phi = 30^\circ$ below
horizontal, a vertical (downward) weight at distance $r$ along the forearm creates a
torque equal to $\tau = W \times r\cos\phi$. The $\cos\phi$ factor shrinks the torque
because the forearm is no longer perfectly horizontal -- imagine laying a ruler flat
vs.\ angled; a weight on the angled ruler has less turning effect.

\smallskip
\textbf{Step 1 -- Calculate the torques from the two weights (both clockwise, negative):}

For the \emph{forearm's own weight} (acts at midpoint, $L/2 = 0.175$ m from elbow):
\begin{align}
\tau_{\text{arm}} &= W_{\text{arm}} \times (L/2) \times \cos\phi \\
&= 14.7 \times 0.175 \times \cos 30^\circ \\
&= 14.7 \times 0.175 \times 0.866 \\
&= \mathbf{2.23\;\text{N$\cdot$m}}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $14.7 \times 0.175 = 2.5725$. Then $2.5725 \times 0.866 = 2.23$.}

For the \emph{chart's weight} (acts at far end, $L = 0.35$ m from elbow):
\begin{align}
\tau_c &= W_c \times L \times \cos\phi \\
&= 24.5 \times 0.35 \times 0.866 \\
&= \mathbf{7.43\;\text{N$\cdot$m}}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $24.5 \times 0.35 = 8.575$. Then $8.575 \times 0.866 = 7.43$.}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Set the total torque to zero (equilibrium):}

The bicep pulls upward at angle $\beta = 80^\circ$ from the forearm axis. Only the
component perpendicular to the forearm creates torque:
\begin{align}
F_B \times \sin\beta \times d &= \tau_{\text{arm}} + \tau_c \\
F_B \times \sin 80^\circ \times 0.05 &= 2.23 + 7.43
\end{align}
\textit{In plain words: (bicep force) $\times$ (fraction pointing sideways) $\times$
(distance from elbow) = (total clockwise torque from weights).}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Solve for $F_B$:}

Compute the left side coefficient: $\sin 80^\circ = 0.9848$, so:
\begin{align}
F_B \times 0.9848 \times 0.05 &= 9.66 \\
F_B \times 0.04924 &= 9.66
\end{align}

Divide both sides by $0.04924$:
\begin{equation}
F_B = \frac{9.66}{0.04924} = \boxed{196\;\text{N}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: $9.66 \div 0.04924 = 196$. That is about 44 lbs of bicep
force just to hold a chart weighing 5.5 lbs! The muscle attachment is only 5 cm
from the elbow, creating a terrible mechanical disadvantage -- like trying to push
a door open by pressing near the hinge instead of near the handle.}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 2(c) -- Elbow joint reaction force]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} Now that we know the bicep force, we use the rule
``all forces must balance'' ($\sum F = 0$). Whatever the muscles and weights push
or pull, the elbow joint must push back with exactly the right force so the forearm
does not fly off or collapse.

\smallskip
\textbf{Step 1 -- Figure out the direction of the bicep force.}

The forearm points $30^\circ$ below horizontal. The bicep pulls at $80^\circ$ from
the forearm axis (upward toward the shoulder). So the bicep's direction from
horizontal is $80^\circ - 30^\circ = 50^\circ$ above horizontal.

\textbf{Step 2 -- Break bicep force into horizontal and vertical pieces.}

(Right = $+x$, Up = $+y$)
\begin{align}
F_{B,x} &= -F_B \cos 50^\circ = -196 \times 0.6428 = \mathbf{-125.9\;\text{N}}
\quad\textit{(leftward)}\\
F_{B,y} &= +F_B \sin 50^\circ = +196 \times 0.7660 = \mathbf{+150.1\;\text{N}}
\quad\textit{(upward)}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step for $x$: $\cos 50^\circ = 0.6428$, then $196 \times 0.6428 = 125.9$.
Step by step for $y$: $\sin 50^\circ = 0.7660$, then $196 \times 0.7660 = 150.1$.}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Horizontal force balance} ($\sum F_x = 0$, nothing else acts horizontally):
\begin{equation}
R_x + F_{B,x} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad R_x = -F_{B,x} = +125.9\;\text{N}
\quad\textit{(rightward)}
\end{equation}
\textit{The joint pushes right to cancel the bicep's leftward pull.}

\textbf{Step 4 -- Vertical force balance} ($\sum F_y = 0$):
\begin{align}
R_y + F_{B,y} - W_{\text{arm}} - W_c &= 0 \\
R_y &= W_{\text{arm}} + W_c - F_{B,y} = 14.7 + 24.5 - 150.1 = \mathbf{-110.9\;\text{N}} \\
&\quad\textit{(downward)}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $14.7 + 24.5 = 39.2$. Then $39.2 - 150.1 = -110.9$ N.
Negative means the joint pushes downward -- it has to push the forearm down because
the powerful bicep is pulling it up too hard.}

\textbf{Step 5 -- Find the magnitude using the Pythagorean theorem.}

The joint force has a rightward and a downward piece. To find the total magnitude,
treat them like the legs of a right triangle ($a^2 + b^2 = c^2$):
\begin{align}
|\vec{R}| &= \sqrt{R_x^2 + R_y^2} = \sqrt{(125.9)^2 + (110.9)^2} \\
&= \sqrt{15{,}851 + 12{,}299} \\
&= \sqrt{28{,}150} = \boxed{167.8\;\text{N}}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $125.9^2 = 15851$; $110.9^2 = 12299$; $15851 + 12299 = 28150$;
$\sqrt{28150} \approx 167.8$ N.
The joint force ($\approx 38$ lbs) is 7 times larger than the chart ($\approx 5.5$ lbs).}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 2(d) -- Comparison with horizontal forearm]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} When the arm tilts down, the weights do less turning.
Think of it this way: if you lay a ruler flat and hang a weight on the end, it
creates maximum turning force (torque). If you tilt the ruler downward, gravity
is now pulling partly along the ruler rather than straight sideways -- it
creates less turning force. Less turning force from the weights means the bicep
does not have to work as hard.

\smallskip
For $\phi = 0^\circ$ (horizontal arm), $\cos 0^\circ = 1$ (the maximum value):
\begin{align}
\tau_{\text{arm}}' &= 14.7 \times 0.175 \times 1 = 2.57\;\text{N$\cdot$m}\\
\tau_c' &= 24.5 \times 0.35 \times 1 = 8.58\;\text{N$\cdot$m}
\end{align}
\textit{Notice $2.57 > 2.23$ and $8.58 > 7.43$ -- the horizontal arm creates bigger
torques because $\cos 0^\circ = 1 > \cos 30^\circ = 0.866$.}

Solve for $F_B'$ (same formula as before, same denominator $0.04924$):
\begin{equation}
F_B' = \frac{\tau_{\text{arm}}' + \tau_c'}{0.04924} = \frac{2.57 + 8.58}{0.04924} = \frac{11.15}{0.04924} = \boxed{226\;\text{N}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $2.57 + 8.58 = 11.15$. Then $11.15 \div 0.04924 = 226$ N.}

\textbf{Conclusion:} Horizontal arm requires $F_B' = 226$ N versus only $F_B = 196$ N
when tilted $30^\circ$ down. The tilted arm needs \emph{less} bicep force (about 13\%
less). So it is genuinely easier to hold something with your arm slightly angled
downward -- not just more comfortable, but mechanically easier!

\end{solutionbox}

\section*{Solution 3: Superman Rescue (Advanced -- 2D)}
%==============================================

\begin{solutionbox}[{Solution 3(a) -- Person's speed at $h = 80\,\mathrm{m}$}]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} When something falls, height (stored energy) turns into
speed (moving energy). The higher it falls, the faster it goes. We use energy
conservation: all the height energy becomes speed energy. No energy is lost (we
ignore air resistance).

\smallskip
\textbf{What we know:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Person started at $H_p = 200$ m and is now at $h = 80$ m.
  \item She has fallen $\Delta h = 200 - 80 = 120$ m.
  \item $g = 9.8$ m/s$^2$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Energy conservation} (height energy $\to$ speed energy):
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\frac{1}{2}mv_p^2}_{\text{kinetic energy gained}} = \underbrace{mg\,\Delta h}_{\text{height energy lost}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: the energy she gained by falling equals the energy she had
from being high up.}

Divide both sides by $m$ (mass cancels!):
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{2}v_p^2 = g\,\Delta h \quad\Longrightarrow\quad v_p^2 = 2g\,\Delta h
\end{equation}

Take the square root of both sides to find $v_p$:
\begin{equation}
v_p = \sqrt{2g\,\Delta h} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.8 \times 120} = \sqrt{2352}
= \boxed{48.5\;\text{m/s (downward)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $2 \times 9.8 = 19.6$. Then $19.6 \times 120 = 2352$.
Then $\sqrt{2352} \approx 48.5$ m/s. That is about 108 mph -- very fast!}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[{Solution 3(b) -- Superman's velocity at h = 80 m}]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} Once Superman turns off his flight power, he moves like
a ball thrown sideways off a cliff -- his left-right (horizontal) speed stays the
same forever, while gravity pulls him faster and faster downward. We handle the two
directions separately because they are completely independent.

\smallskip
\textbf{What we know:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Superman starts at $H_S = 180$ m, falls to $h = 80$ m, so $\Delta h_S = 180 - 80 = 100$ m.
  \item His initial horizontal speed $u = 15.0$ m/s (rightward).
  \item His initial vertical speed $= 0$ (he was flying horizontally, not up or down).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Horizontal component} (no horizontal force, so speed does not change):
\begin{equation}
v_{Sx} = u = \boxed{15.0\;\text{m/s (rightward)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: without any sideways push or pull, the sideways speed stays
the same -- just like a ball rolling on a frictionless table keeps going at the same
speed.}

\textbf{Vertical component} (same energy formula as part (a), starting from rest vertically):
\begin{equation}
v_{Sy} = \sqrt{2g\,\Delta h_S} = \sqrt{2 \times 9.8 \times 100} = \sqrt{1960}
= \boxed{44.3\;\text{m/s (downward)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $2 \times 9.8 = 19.6$. Then $19.6 \times 100 = 1960$.
Then $\sqrt{1960} \approx 44.3$ m/s.}

\textbf{Total speed} (Pythagorean theorem -- the two speed components are at right angles):
\begin{equation}
|v_S| = \sqrt{v_{Sx}^2 + v_{Sy}^2} = \sqrt{(15.0)^2 + (44.3)^2}
= \sqrt{225 + 1962} = \sqrt{2187} = \boxed{46.8\;\text{m/s}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $15^2 = 225$; $44.3^2 \approx 1962$; $225 + 1962 = 2187$;
$\sqrt{2187} \approx 46.8$ m/s. (Note: $44.3^2 = 1962.49$, rounded to $1962$ for brevity.)
Superman is zooming mostly downward (44 m/s) with a smaller sideways drift (15 m/s).}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 3(c) -- Combined velocity after the catch (2D momentum)]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} When Superman grabs the person, they stick together.
This is like two ice skaters colliding and hugging -- they end up moving together.
\emph{Momentum} (mass $\times$ velocity) cannot be created or destroyed. We conserve
momentum \emph{separately} in the left-right direction and the up-down direction
because the two directions do not mix.

Think of it like billiard balls on a table: a ball moving sideways can only change
the sideways speed of another ball, not its up-down speed.

\smallskip
\textbf{Before the catch:}
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
 & $x$ (rightward $+$) & $y$ (downward $+$) \\
\midrule
Superman ($M = 90$ kg) & $+15.0$ m/s & $+44.3$ m/s \\
Person ($m = 70$ kg)   & $0$ m/s & $+48.5$ m/s \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\textbf{$x$-direction momentum conservation:}

Total $x$-momentum before = Total $x$-momentum after
\begin{equation}
M \cdot v_{Sx} + m \cdot 0 = (M + m)\,v_{fx}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: only Superman had horizontal momentum (the person was falling
straight down). After the catch, both move together at $v_{fx}$.}
\begin{equation}
90 \times 15.0 = 160 \times v_{fx}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
v_{fx} = \frac{1350}{160} = \boxed{8.44\;\text{m/s (rightward)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $90 \times 15.0 = 1350$. Then $1350 \div 160 = 8.44$ m/s.}

\textbf{$y$-direction momentum conservation (downward positive):}

Total $y$-momentum before = Total $y$-momentum after
\begin{align}
M \cdot v_{Sy} + m \cdot v_{py} &= (M + m)\,v_{fy} \\
90 \times 44.3 + 70 \times 48.5 &= 160 \times v_{fy} \\
3987 + 3395 &= 160 \times v_{fy} \\
7382 &= 160 \times v_{fy}
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
v_{fy} = \frac{7382}{160} = \boxed{46.1\;\text{m/s (downward)}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $90 \times 44.3 = 3987$; $70 \times 48.5 = 3395$;
$3987 + 3395 = 7382$; $7382 \div 160 = 46.1$ m/s.}

\textbf{Combined speed} (Pythagorean theorem again):
\begin{equation}
v_f = \sqrt{v_{fx}^2 + v_{fy}^2} = \sqrt{(8.44)^2 + (46.1)^2}
= \sqrt{71.2 + 2125.2} = \sqrt{2196.4} = \boxed{46.9\;\text{m/s}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $8.44^2 = 71.2$; $46.1^2 = 2125.2$; $71.2 + 2125.2 = 2196.4$;
$\sqrt{2196.4} \approx 46.9$ m/s. They are still falling very fast -- mostly straight
down. Superman has a lot of work to do!}

\end{solutionbox}

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution 3(d) -- Work by Superman's muscles]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} Superman and the person are at 80 m, falling at 46.9 m/s.
He must bring them to a \emph{complete stop} ($v = 0$) exactly at the ground ($h = 0$).
We use the \textbf{work-energy theorem}: the total work done equals the change in
kinetic energy. Both gravity (helping, pushing them down) and Superman's muscles
(braking, slowing them down) do work during the remaining 80 m.

\smallskip
\textbf{Step 1 -- Find the kinetic energy just after the catch.}

Kinetic energy (KE) = $\frac{1}{2} \times \text{mass} \times \text{speed}^2$
\begin{equation}
KE_i = \frac{1}{2}(M+m)v_f^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 160 \times (46.9)^2
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $46.9^2 = 46.9 \times 46.9 = 2199.6$. Then $\frac{1}{2} \times 160 = 80$.
Then $80 \times 2199.6 = 175{,}968$ J.}
\begin{equation}
KE_i = 80 \times 2199.6 = \mathbf{175{,}968\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Find the work done by gravity over the 80 m to the ground.}

Gravity does \emph{positive} work as they fall (gravity acts downward and they move
downward -- force and motion in the same direction):
\begin{equation}
W_g = (M+m)\,g\,h = 160 \times 9.8 \times 80 = \mathbf{125{,}440\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $160 \times 9.8 = 1568$. Then $1568 \times 80 = 125{,}440$ J.}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Apply the work-energy theorem.}

At the end they are stopped: final $KE_f = 0$.

The theorem says: total work done $=$ final KE $-$ initial KE
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{total}} = KE_f - KE_i = 0 - 175{,}968 = -175{,}968\;\text{J}
\end{equation}

Total work = gravity's work + muscle work:
\begin{align}
W_g + W_{\text{muscles}} &= -175{,}968 \\
125{,}440 + W_{\text{muscles}} &= -175{,}968
\end{align}

Subtract $125{,}440$ from both sides:
\begin{equation}
W_{\text{muscles}} = -175{,}968 - 125{,}440 = \boxed{-301{,}408\;\text{J} \approx -301\;\text{kJ}}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: the negative sign means Superman's muscles do \emph{braking}
work -- they remove energy from the system, like brakes on a car. He must absorb
301,000 J, which is roughly the energy in a stick of dynamite! That is why
Superman is a superhero.}

\end{solutionbox}

\vspace{1.5em}

%==============================================
\section*{Solution A: Two-Spring Diving Board}
%==============================================

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution A -- Two-Spring System]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} The diving board is perfectly still -- nothing is moving
or spinning. That means two things must be true at the same time:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item All upward forces must equal all downward forces (force balance).
  \item All clockwise torques must equal all counterclockwise torques (torque balance).
\end{enumerate}
Two rules $\Rightarrow$ two equations $\Rightarrow$ we can solve for two unknowns
($\delta_1$ and $\delta_2$).

\smallskip
\textbf{Forces acting (using Hooke's Law, $F = kx$):}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Spring 1 pushes up: $F_1 = k_1 \delta_1 = 8000\,\delta_1$ \quad at $x = 0$
  \item Spring 2 pushes up: $F_2 = k_2 \delta_2 = 12{,}000\,\delta_2$ \quad at $x = 2.0$ m
  \item Board weight pulls down: $W_B = Mg = 25 \times 9.8 = 245$ N \quad at $x = 1.5$ m (center)
  \item Diver weight pulls down: $W_D = mg = 70 \times 9.8 = 686$ N \quad at $x = 3.0$ m
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Force balance} (up $=$ down):
\begin{equation}
8000\,\delta_1 + 12{,}000\,\delta_2 = 245 + 686 = 931\;\text{N}
\label{eq:forceA}
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: total spring push-up equals total weight push-down.}

\textbf{Torque balance about $x = 0$ (Spring~1's location as pivot).}

Choosing Spring~1 as the pivot makes $F_1$ vanish (it is at the pivot, distance = 0).
Now we only have three torques to balance:
\begin{align}
\underbrace{12{,}000\,\delta_2 \times 2.0}_{\text{Spring 2 torque (CCW)}}
&= \underbrace{245 \times 1.5}_{\text{board weight torque (CW)}}
+ \underbrace{686 \times 3.0}_{\text{diver torque (CW)}} \\
24{,}000\,\delta_2 &= 367.5 + 2058 = 2425.5
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $245 \times 1.5 = 367.5$; $686 \times 3.0 = 2058$;
$367.5 + 2058 = 2425.5$.}

Divide both sides by $24{,}000$:
\begin{equation}
\delta_2 = \frac{2425.5}{24{,}000} = \boxed{0.101\;\text{m} = 10.1\;\text{cm}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $2425.5 \div 24000 = 0.10106 \approx 0.101$ m.}

\textbf{Substitute $\delta_2$ into the force balance} (Equation~\ref{eq:forceA}):
\begin{align}
8000\,\delta_1 &= 931 - 12{,}000 \times 0.101 \\
8000\,\delta_1 &= 931 - 1212 = -281
\end{align}
\begin{equation}
\delta_1 = \frac{-281}{8000} = \boxed{-0.035\;\text{m} = -3.5\;\text{cm}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $12000 \times 0.101 = 1212$; $931 - 1212 = -281$;
$-281 \div 8000 = -0.0351$ m.}

\textbf{What does negative $\delta_1$ mean?} Normally a spring is compressed (squished).
A negative value means Spring~1 is being \emph{stretched} (pulled apart) instead!
The heavy diver at the far right tips the board so far that the left end lifts up,
stretching Spring~1. A spring can only do this if it is bolted down (attached on
both ends) -- otherwise the board just lifts off.

\end{solutionbox}

\vspace{1.5em}

%==============================================
\section*{Solution B: Variable-Force Braking}
%==============================================

\begin{solutionbox}[Solution B -- Variable Force Stopping Distance]

\textbf{Plain-English idea:} The brakes get stronger as the car slides farther -- maybe
the brakes heat up and grip better. To find the work done by a force that \emph{changes},
we use calculus (integration). Think of it like adding up many tiny rectangles under a
graph of force vs.\ distance. Then the work-energy theorem connects work to speed change.

\smallskip
\textbf{What we know:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item Mass: $m = 1500$ kg; initial speed: $v_0 = 25$ m/s; final speed: $0$ (stopped)
  \item Braking force: $F(x) = F_0 + bx = 5000 + 200x$ (grows with distance $x$)
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 1 -- Initial kinetic energy.}
\begin{equation}
KE_i = \frac{1}{2}mv_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} \times 1500 \times 25^2
= 750 \times 625 = \mathbf{468{,}750\;\text{J}}
\end{equation}
\textit{Step by step: $25^2 = 625$; $\frac{1}{2} \times 1500 = 750$; $750 \times 625 = 468{,}750$ J.}

\textbf{Step 2 -- Work done by the braking force (using integration).}

Work $=$ area under the force-distance graph. For $F(x) = 5000 + 200x$:
\begin{equation}
W = \int_0^{d}(5000 + 200x)\,dx
\end{equation}
\textit{In plain words: we add up (integrate) the force for every tiny slice of distance,
from the start ($x=0$) to the stopping point ($x=d$). Integrating $5000$ gives $5000d$.
Integrating $200x$ gives $100x^2$ (use the power rule: $\int x\,dx = \frac{x^2}{2}$,
so $\int 200x\,dx = 200 \cdot \frac{x^2}{2} = 100x^2$):}
\begin{equation}
W = 5000d + 100d^2
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 3 -- Set work equal to initial KE (all KE is removed by braking).}
\begin{equation}
5000d + 100d^2 = 468{,}750
\end{equation}

Rearrange to standard quadratic form ($ax^2 + bx + c = 0$). Divide everything by 100:
\begin{equation}
d^2 + 50d - 4687.5 = 0
\end{equation}
\textit{Here $a = 1$, $b = 50$, $c = -4687.5$.}

\textbf{Step 4 -- Use the quadratic formula.}

The quadratic formula solves $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:
\[
d = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}
\]
\textit{The $\pm$ gives two solutions. We always pick the \emph{positive} one (negative
distance makes no physical sense).}
\begin{align}
d &= \frac{-50 + \sqrt{50^2 - 4(1)(-4687.5)}}{2(1)} \\
&= \frac{-50 + \sqrt{2500 + 18750}}{2} \\
&= \frac{-50 + \sqrt{21250}}{2}
\end{align}
\textit{Step by step: $50^2 = 2500$; $4 \times 4687.5 = 18750$; $2500 + 18750 = 21250$;
$\sqrt{21250} \approx 145.8$.}
\begin{equation}
d = \frac{-50 + 145.8}{2} = \frac{95.8}{2} = \boxed{47.9\;\text{m}}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Step 5 -- Compare to constant braking (force $= F_0 = 5000$ N only).}

With constant force, work $= F_0 \times d_{\text{const}}$. Set equal to KE:
\begin{equation}
d_{\text{const}} = \frac{KE_i}{F_0} = \frac{468{,}750}{5000} = \boxed{93.75\;\text{m}}
\end{equation}
\textit{The variable braking stops the car in 47.9 m versus 93.75 m for constant
braking -- \emph{half the distance}! The growing braking force (brakes getting stronger)
does much more work in the same distance, removing energy faster.}

\end{solutionbox}

\section*{Common Exam Mistakes to Avoid}

\begin{warningbox}[Top Mistakes on PHYSICS 1101 Exams]
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Torque sign errors:} Always define CCW as $+$ and CW as $-$, and stick
        to it throughout.
  \item \textbf{Moment arms when arm is tilted:} For a vertical weight $W$ at distance
        $r$ along a tilted beam, the moment arm is $r\cos\phi$ (not just $r$)!
  \item \textbf{Dynamic vs.\ static loads:} $F = kx$ is linear, so doubling load doubles
        compression; tripling load triples compression.
  \item \textbf{Unit conversions:} km/h to m/s: divide by 3.6. Always do this before
        plugging into any formula.
  \item \textbf{Integration limits:} Always integrate from 0 to $d$ (stopping distance)
        for braking problems.
  \item \textbf{Quadratic roots:} Physical answers (distance, speed) must be positive --
        discard the negative root.
\end{enumerate}
\end{warningbox}
