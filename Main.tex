\textbf{Module Learning Objectives} (connected to the course learning objective: \emph{Interactions in communities}).
After completing this module you are expected to answer the following questions so that someone with an
undergraduate Biology knowledge can fully understand your answers.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Describe all types of interspecific interactions.
  \item Giving examples, describe how a niche of a species forms with biotic and abiotic factors of its environment.  Compare fundamental and realized niche.
  \item Compare intraspecific and interspecific interactions.
  \item Describe different types of competition avoidance in nature.
  \item Explain how the Lotka--Volterra model helps understand if two competing species would co-exist in a habitat.
  \item Describe major symbiotic interactions with examples.
  \item Describe different types of ecological/community successions.
  \item Demonstrate the use of the Shannon--Weiner index to measure biodiversity.
\end{itemize}

% ── Section 3 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q2 --- Niche: Biotic, Abiotic and Evolutionary Factors]

\textit{Biotic factors} (any three): interspecific interactions including coevolution; diet and foraging behaviour; trophic position; life history timing; morphological specialisations; habitat specialisations.

\textit{Abiotic factors} (any three): temperature range tolerance; oxygen/CO$_2$ levels; pH; salinity; diurnal and seasonal light cycles; substrate type; nutrient availability.

\textit{Multidimensional niche:} Each environmental variable is one axis; the organism's tolerable range on every axis together defines an $n$-dimensional hypervolume representing all conditions under which the species can persist.

\textbf{Ecological niche:} The persistence/role of an individual, population, or species based on the \emph{totality of environmental factors} at a given locale.

\textbf{Evolutionary niche:} The fitness/phenotype-changing \emph{selection pressures} affecting a population in a given environment (i.e., how the environment shapes the evolution of traits).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 4 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q3 --- Fundamental vs.\ Realized Niche]

\textbf{Fundamental niche:} The full range of conditions and resources under which a species \emph{could} survive and reproduce if there were no biotic limiting factors; primarily determined by \textbf{abiotic} factors.

\textbf{Realized niche:} The actual subset of conditions the species occupies in the wild, after accounting for biotic interactions (predators, competitors, parasites, disease); primarily determined by \textbf{biotic} factors.

\textit{Example biotic factor:} A superior competitor excludes the focal species from part of its temperature range, reducing its realized niche.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 5 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q4 --- Intraspecific vs.\ Interspecific Competition]

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.5}
\begin{tabular}{|p{4cm}|p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|}
\hline
& \textbf{Intraspecific} & \textbf{Interspecific} \\
\hline
Individuals involved & Same species & Different species \\
\hline
Typical intensity & Often higher (identical resource needs) & Varies; depends on niche overlap \\
\hline
Main evolutionary consequence & Natural selection; can drive niche expansion & Character displacement; competitive exclusion or coexistence \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Scenario answers: (a) IA \quad (b) IE \quad (c) IE

\textbf{By mechanism:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Interference competition (d):} Two species directly interact (fight, display, produce toxins) for the resource.  Example: two male deer fighting for territory.
  \item \textbf{Exploitative competition (e):} One species uses the resource, making it unavailable for the other species without direct interaction.  Example: two plant species both absorbing the same soil nutrients.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{By outcome:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{Contest competition (f):} Only one or a few individuals acquire the resource; clear winner/loser scenario (e.g., territorial defence).
  \item \textbf{Scramble competition (g):} All competitors attempt to acquire the resource; everyone obtains a share, but possibly insufficient amounts (e.g., many seedlings competing for limited light).
\end{itemize}
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 6 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q5 --- Competition Avoidance]

\textbf{Mechanism 1 --- Resource partitioning:} Species divide up a resource in time, space, or type so that direct competition is reduced.
\textit{Example:} Five warbler species partition the vertical zones of a spruce tree (Bay-breasted uses lower branches; Cape May uses the crown, etc.).
Does it affect morphology? Yes --- can lead to character displacement.

\textbf{Mechanism 2 --- Character displacement:} When two species co-occur, natural selection drives their morphologies (e.g., beak depth) further apart, reducing niche overlap and competition.
\textit{Example:} Darwin's finch species on the same Gal\'{a}pagos island have more different bill sizes than populations of the same species on islands where they occur alone.

\textbf{Competitive exclusion principle:} Two species occupying exactly the same ecological niche cannot coexist indefinitely; one will competitively exclude the other.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 7 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q6 --- Lotka--Volterra Competition Model]

(a) $K_1$, $K_2$ = carrying capacities; $\alpha$, $\beta$ = interspecific competition coefficients.

(b) Species 1 wins when $K_1 > K_2/\beta$ \emph{and} $K_1/\alpha > K_2$ (Species 1's isocline lies entirely outside Species 2's isocline).

(c) Stable coexistence occurs when each species inhibits itself more than it inhibits the other species: $K_1 < K_2/\beta$ \emph{and} $K_2 < K_1/\alpha$ (isoclines cross with a stable equilibrium point).

(d) $N_2$-intercept $= K_1/\alpha = 200/0.5 = \mathbf{400}$.

(e) When the two ZNGIs cross, there is an equilibrium point at which both species can potentially coexist; stability depends on the slopes (i.e., the relative magnitudes of intra- vs.\ interspecific competition).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

(a) Pioneer communities: \textbf{high} GCP; \textbf{low} biodiversity.\\
(b) Climax community: $P \approx R$ (production $\approx$ respiration); biomass accumulates \textbf{very slowly}.\\
(c) \textit{Microsuccession (serule):} A decomposing log undergoes a predictable series of microbial and invertebrate communities; each community alters the substrate (temperature, moisture, chemistry), enabling the next community.  The log is eventually decomposed, eliminating the microhabitat without reaching a climax.

% ── Section 10 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q9 --- Shannon--Weiner Diversity Index]

(a) Both communities: $S = 4$ species.

(b) \textbf{Community A:} $p_1=p_2=0.40$, $p_3=p_4=0.10$
\begin{align*}
H_A &= -[2(0.40\ln 0.40) + 2(0.10\ln 0.10)]\\
    &= -[2(-0.3665) + 2(-0.2303)]\\
    &= -[-0.7330 - 0.4606]\\
    &\approx \mathbf{1.19}
\end{align*}

(c) \textbf{Community B:} $p_1=p_2=0.10$, $p_3=0.70$, $p_4=0.10$
\begin{align*}
H_B &= -[3(0.10\ln 0.10) + 0.70\ln 0.70]\\
    &= -[3(-0.2303) + (-0.2497)]\\
    &= -[-0.6909 - 0.2497]\\
    &\approx \mathbf{0.94}
\end{align*}

(d) \textbf{Community A} has higher evenness; its species are distributed more equally
    (40:40:10:10) compared to Community B (10:10:70:10), where one species dominates.

(e) \textit{Species evenness} is the relative homogeneity (equitability) of individuals
    among species --- how similar the proportions of different species are in a community.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 11 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q10 --- Biodiversity in Communities]

(a) \textbf{Dominant species:} The species that is most abundant or makes up the greatest biomass in a community.
Example: Beech--maple forest (named for the dominant trees); mussel zone in an intertidal community.

(b) \textbf{Keystone species:} Has effects on community structure and species diversity far larger than its abundance would predict; often controls populations of other species or maintains habitat structure.
Example: Sea otter (controls sea urchin populations, thereby maintaining kelp forests).

(c) \textbf{Ecological engineer:} Beaver --- builds dams that create wetland habitats, alter water flow, and increase habitat diversity for many species.

(d) Ranked highest to lowest: Tropical rainforest (1) $\approx$ Coral reef (2) $>$ Estuary (3) $>$ Arctic tundra (4) $>$ Agricultural land (5).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 12 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q11 --- Predator--Prey Relationships]

(a) Predator and prey populations \textbf{oscillate out of phase with} each other.
When hare numbers are high, lynx numbers subsequently \textbf{increase} because abundant prey supports higher predator survival and reproduction.

(b) Defense matching:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item Mechanical defense: \textbf{C} (honey locust thorns)
  \item Chemical defense: \textbf{D} (foxglove cardiac glycosides) [also acceptable: B]
  \item Cryptic coloration: \textbf{A} (stick insect)
  \item Aposematic coloration: \textbf{B} (monarch butterfly --- also chemical; the bright orange coloration warns predators)
\end{itemize}

(c) \textbf{Batesian mimicry:} A harmless species (mimic) resembles a dangerous or unpalatable species (model) to deter predators; the mimic gains protection but the model does not benefit.
\textbf{M\"{u}llerian mimicry:} Two or more genuinely unpalatable/dangerous species resemble each other; predators learn to avoid the shared warning pattern faster, benefiting all co-models.
\textbf{Mertensian mimicry:} A deadly/highly dangerous species (the mimic) resembles a less dangerous but aversive species (the model), because predators must survive a non-lethal encounter with the model to \emph{learn} to avoid the warning pattern; if the mimic were the only species encountered, predators could not survive to learn.  Example: a highly venomous coral snake species (mimic) converges in coloration on a moderately venomous coral snake species (model) that predators can survive and learn to avoid.
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 13 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q12 --- Community Equilibrium and Trophic Cascades]

(a) A \textbf{trophic cascade} is a powerful indirect interaction that propagates through multiple trophic levels when one component of a community changes.  The Yellowstone example is a \textbf{top-down} cascade (apex predator suppresses herbivore, which releases vegetation).

(b) Cause-and-effect chain:
Wolf reintroduced $\rightarrow$ elk/bison populations \textbf{decrease} $\rightarrow$ overgrazing \textbf{reduced} $\rightarrow$ riparian vegetation \textbf{increases} $\rightarrow$ river banks stabilise $\rightarrow$ fish \& bird diversity \textbf{increases}.

(c) \textbf{Community stability} (or \emph{ecological resilience}).

(d) Example: \textit{Agricultural land clearing} removes native vegetation and biodiversity, simplifies food chains, disrupts nutrient cycling, and increases community instability (e.g., pest outbreaks, soil erosion).
\end{keybox}

\bigskip

% ── Section 14 ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{keybox}[Q13 --- Eco-agriculture, Conservation Farming and Regenerative Agriculture]

\textbf{Three main practices and their ecological benefits:}

\begin{center}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.6}
\begin{tabular}{|p{3.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|p{4.5cm}|}
\hline
\textbf{Practice} & \textbf{Description} & \textbf{Ecological benefit} \\
\hline
Minimum/no tillage & Seeds/fertiliser placed by dribbling into undisturbed soil & Conserves soil moisture, organic matter, nutrients, structure, and soil biodiversity \\
\hline
Soil cover (dead \& live mulch) & Crop residue left plus N-fixing cover crops grown between main crops & Provides all tillage benefits and adds fixed nitrogen; suppresses weeds; reduces erosion \\
\hline
Biodiversity through crop rotation, intercropping, agroforestry & Multiple crop species grown together or in sequence, often with trees & Hosts diverse symbionts; prevents single-nutrient depletion; nutrient cycling from different soil layers; synergy among crops including pest control; food security \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

(b) Conventional monocultures: \textbf{low} biodiversity; \textbf{unstable} ecologically.  Eco-agriculture increases biodiversity through \textbf{crop rotation}, \textbf{intercropping/multiple cropping}, and \textbf{agroforestry}.

(c) N-fixing cover crops add biologically fixed nitrogen to the soil (via root nodules with \textit{Rhizobium} or free-living bacteria), reducing the need for synthetic fertilisers and improving soil fertility for subsequent crops.

(d) The answer is \textbf{Surface water runoff} --- minimum tillage actually \emph{reduces} runoff by improving soil structure and infiltration; it does not increase it.  All other options (moisture, biodiversity, organic matter) are conserved by no-till.
\end{keybox}

\textbf{Part D: Short Answer -- Key Points}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{EcoRI palindrome explanation:} Reading 5' to 3' on the top strand gives GAATTC. The complementary strand (3'-CTTAAG-5') reads GAATTC when read 5' to 3' (right to left). Because the sequence is identical in the 5' to 3' direction on both strands, it is palindromic.
    \item \textit{KO vs. Knockdown:} Gene knockout -- gene is permanently removed/inactivated; no gene, no mRNA, no protein. Gene knockdown (RNAi) -- gene intact; mRNA is produced then degraded by dsRNA; reduced or absent protein. Knockout is permanent; knockdown is often temporary/reversible.
    \item \textit{Blue-white screening:} Insert cloned into MCS within lacZ disrupts the gene, so no functional $\beta$-galactosidase is made; X-gal not cleaved $\rightarrow$ white colonies (have insert). Uninterrupted lacZ $\rightarrow$ functional $\beta$-galactosidase $\rightarrow$ cleaves X-gal $\rightarrow$ blue colonies (no insert).
    \item \textit{PCR components:} (1) Template DNA -- contains target sequence; (2) Two oligonucleotide primers (15--20 nt) -- flank the target region; (3) dNTPs -- building blocks for new DNA; (4) Taq polymerase -- thermostable enzyme that synthesizes new strands.
    \item \textit{25-cycle calculation:} $2^{25} = 33{,}554{,}432$ copies ($\approx$33.5 million-fold amplification). Each cycle doubles the number of DNA copies.
    \item \textit{Taq vs.\ E.\ coli polymerase:} Taq polymerase is thermostable (from \textit{Thermus aquaticus}). Regular \textit{E. coli} DNA polymerase would denature and become non-functional during the high-temperature (94--95°C) denaturation step.
    \item \textit{PCR cycle steps:} (1) Denaturation ($\sim$94°C) -- heat separates double-stranded DNA into single strands; (2) Annealing ($\sim$50--65°C) -- primers bind to complementary sequences flanking the target; (3) Extension/Synthesis ($\sim$72°C) -- Taq polymerase extends from primers to synthesize new complementary strands.
    \item \textit{Smaller fragments migrate faster:} The gel matrix acts as a molecular sieve. Smaller fragments experience less frictional resistance and pass through the pores more easily, traveling farther in a given time.
    \item \textit{Supercoiled faster than nicked circles:} Supercoiled DNA is compact and passes through the gel easily. Nicked circles are relaxed open rings with a larger effective radius; their flexible open conformation causes more resistance, so they migrate slowest.
    \item \textit{EMSA principle:} Free labeled DNA migrates to a defined position. When a protein binds the DNA, the larger protein-DNA complex migrates slower (shifts to a higher position on the gel). A shift indicates a protein-DNA interaction.
    \item \textit{Western vs. Northern:} Western -- detects proteins; uses antibodies (primary binds target protein, secondary carries alkaline phosphatase). Northern -- detects specific RNA (mRNA); uses a labeled DNA probe complementary to target mRNA.
    \item \textit{Role of ori:} The origin of replication is the sequence recognized by the host cell's replication machinery. It determines in which organisms the vector can replicate and how many copies are maintained per cell.
    \item \textit{Role of ampR:} Ampicillin resistance allows selective growth on ampicillin-containing plates; only bacteria that took up the plasmid survive, enabling selection of transformed cells.
    \item \textit{Site-directed mutagenesis:} Uses synthetic oligonucleotide primers containing the desired mutation. The mutant primer is used in PCR or annealed to a template plasmid; the resulting plasmid carries the specific mutation. Researchers use this to determine how individual residues affect protein structure, function, and phenotype.
    \item \textit{Recombinant insulin production:} (1) Clone the human insulin gene into a bacterial expression plasmid; (2) Transform into competent \textit{E. coli}; (3) Bacteria express the insulin gene and produce human insulin protein; (4) Insulin is purified from bacterial cultures. This provides a safe, abundant, species-appropriate supply of insulin for diabetic patients who lack functional $\beta$ cells.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=red!5!white,colframe=red!75!black,title=Part H: Short Answer/Essay Answers,breakable]

\textbf{1. Steps in Gene Cloning:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Obtain chromosomal DNA from organism of interest (genomic DNA extraction)
    \item Cut both chromosomal DNA and vector DNA with the same restriction enzyme
    \item Mix the DNA fragments with the cut vector; allow sticky ends to base-pair
    \item Add DNA ligase to covalently link the DNA backbones, creating recombinant vectors
    \item Transform competent bacterial cells with the recombinant vectors
    \item Plate cells on selective media containing antibiotic and X-gal
    \item Select white colonies (contain insert) for further analysis
    \item Culture selected bacteria to amplify the cloned gene
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{2. Blue-White Screening Mechanism:}

Blue-white screening uses the lacZ gene, which encodes $\beta$-galactosidase. The cloning site (MCS) is located within the lacZ gene.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Blue colonies:} The vector re-circularized without an insert. The lacZ gene is intact and functional. $\beta$-galactosidase is produced and cleaves X-gal to produce a blue pigment. These colonies do NOT contain the gene of interest.
    
    \item \textbf{White colonies:} The vector contains an insert within the lacZ gene. The insertion disrupts the lacZ gene, so no functional $\beta$-galactosidase is produced. X-gal is not cleaved, so no blue pigment forms. These colonies contain the recombinant plasmid with the gene of interest.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{3. Four Starting Materials for PCR:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Template DNA:} Contains the region to be amplified
    \item \textbf{Oligonucleotide primers:} Two synthetic primers (15-20 nucleotides) complementary to sequences flanking the target region
    \item \textbf{dNTPs:} Deoxynucleoside triphosphates provide building blocks for DNA synthesis
    \item \textbf{Taq DNA polymerase:} Thermostable enzyme from Thermus aquaticus that synthesizes new DNA strands and survives repeated heating cycles
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{4. Three Steps of Each PCR Cycle:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Denaturation (94-95°C):} High temperature separates the double-stranded DNA into single strands
    \item \textbf{Annealing (50-65°C):} Temperature is lowered to allow primers to bind (anneal) to their complementary sequences on the template DNA
    \item \textbf{Extension/Synthesis (72°C):} Taq polymerase synthesizes new DNA strands by adding dNTPs to the 3' end of each primer, extending along the template
\end{enumerate}

After 20 cycles: $2^{20} \approx 1$ million copies\\
After 30 cycles: $2^{30} \approx 1$ billion copies

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{5. Gene Knockout vs. Gene Knockdown:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Gene Knockout (KO):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The gene itself is permanently inactivated or removed from the genome
        \item No gene present $\rightarrow$ No mRNA produced $\rightarrow$ No protein produced
        \item Permanent and irreversible
        \item Used to study complete loss of gene function
    \end{itemize}
    
    \item \textbf{Gene Knockdown (via RNAi):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item The gene remains intact in the genome
        \item mRNA is produced but is targeted for degradation by double-stranded RNA (dsRNA)
        \item Works post-transcriptionally
        \item Gene present $\rightarrow$ mRNA made but degraded $\rightarrow$ Reduced or no protein
        \item Can be temporary/reversible
        \item Reduces but may not completely eliminate gene expression
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{6. Gel Electrophoresis Principles:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item DNA is negatively charged (due to phosphate groups)
    \item When placed in an electrical field, DNA migrates from the negative electrode (cathode) toward the positive electrode (anode)
    \item ``Run to red!'' - DNA runs toward the red/positive end
    \item Smaller fragments migrate faster and travel farther
    \item Larger fragments migrate slower and stay closer to the wells
    \item DNA can also be separated by topology (shape):
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Nicked circles: slowest (least compact)
        \item Linearized: intermediate
        \item Supercoiled: fastest (most compact)
    \end{itemize}
    \item Ethidium bromide intercalates into DNA and fluoresces under UV light, allowing visualization
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{7. Comparison of Blotting Techniques:}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Technique} & \textbf{Detects} & \textbf{Probe/Detection} & \textbf{Purpose} \\
\hline
Western & Proteins & Antibodies (1° and 2°) & Identify specific proteins \\
\hline
Northern & RNA (mRNA) & Labeled DNA probe & Detect specific mRNA \\
\hline
EMSA & Protein-DNA & Labeled DNA fragment & Detect protein-DNA binding \\
 & interactions & & \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{8. Western Blotting Procedure:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Separate proteins by gel electrophoresis
    \item Transfer proteins to a membrane
    \item Add primary antibody that binds specifically to the protein of interest
    \item Add secondary antibody that binds to the primary antibody; secondary antibody is attached to alkaline phosphatase enzyme
    \item Add colorless substrate XP (5-bromo-4-chloro-3-indolyl phosphate)
    \item Alkaline phosphatase converts XP to a dark purple dye, revealing location of target protein
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{9. Applications of Microorganisms in Biotechnology:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Production of medicines:} Antibiotics, vitamins, synthesis of human insulin in recombinant E. coli
    \item \textbf{Food fermentation:} Cheese, yogurt, vinegar, wine, beer
    \item \textbf{Biological control:} Control of plant diseases, insect pests, weeds; symbiotic nitrogen fixation; prevention of frost formation
    \item \textbf{Bioremediation:} Cleanup of environmental pollutants such as petroleum hydrocarbons and difficult-to-degrade synthetics
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\textbf{10. Insulin Production Using Biotechnology:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Insulin is a hormone composed of two polypeptide chains (A and B chains)
    \item It regulates physiological processes, particularly glucose uptake into fat and muscle cells
    \item Produced by $\beta$ cells of the pancreas
    \item People with insulin-dependent diabetes have defective $\beta$ cells and cannot synthesize enough insulin
    \item Recombinant DNA technology allows production of human insulin in E. coli bacteria
    \item The insulin gene is cloned into a plasmid vector and expressed in bacterial cells
    \item This provides a safe, abundant source of human insulin for diabetic patients
\end{itemize}
\end{tcolorbox}

\textbf{Problem 1:} If you perform PCR for 25 cycles starting with a single copy of template DNA, how many copies of the target sequence will you have?

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Solution space:}
\vspace{2cm}

\textbf{Answer:} $2^{25}$ = 33,554,432 copies ($\sim$33.5 million copies)

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Problem 2:} A researcher needs approximately 1 billion copies of a gene. Starting with one copy of template DNA, what is the minimum number of PCR cycles needed?

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Solution space:}
\vspace{2cm}

\textbf{Answer:} 30 cycles ($2^{30}$ = 1,073,741,824 $\approx$ 1 billion)

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Problem 3:} Is the following sequence palindromic? Explain your reasoning.

\texttt{5'-ACCTAGGT-3'}

\texttt{3'-TGGATCCA-5'}

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Solution space:}
\vspace{2cm}

\textbf{Answer:} Yes, this is palindromic. Reading 5' to 3' on the top strand gives ACCTAGGT. Reading 5' to 3' on the bottom strand (right to left) also gives ACCTAGGT. The sequence is identical when read in the 5' to 3' direction on both strands.

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Problem 4:} After transformation, a researcher plates bacteria on medium containing ampicillin, X-gal, and IPTG. She observes 50 blue colonies and 150 white colonies. 

\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*.]
    \item How many colonies contain recombinant plasmids with inserts?
    \item What percentage of transformed colonies are useful for her cloning experiment?
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Solution space:}
\vspace{2cm}

\textbf{Answer:} 
\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*.]
    \item 150 white colonies contain recombinant plasmids with inserts
    \item $\frac{150}{200} \times 100 = 75\%$ of transformed colonies are useful
\end{enumerate}

\section*{Scenario-Based Questions}
%====================

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=purple!5!white,colframe=purple!75!black,title=Critical Thinking Scenarios,breakable]

\textbf{Scenario 1:} A graduate student is trying to clone a human gene into a bacterial plasmid. After transformation, she plates the bacteria on ampicillin plates with X-gal but observes NO colonies at all (neither blue nor white).

\textbf{Question:} What are three possible explanations for this result?

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Your Answer:}
\vspace{3cm}

\textbf{Model Answer:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The transformation failed - bacteria did not take up any plasmid (no competent cells, improper transformation protocol)
    \item The plasmid does not have a functional origin of replication for the bacterial host
    \item The ampicillin resistance gene is not functional
    \item The ampicillin concentration is too high
    \item The bacteria were not viable
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Scenario 2:} A researcher wants to study the function of Gene X in mice. She has two options: create a knockout mouse or use RNAi to knockdown the gene.

\textbf{Question:} Compare and contrast these two approaches. When might each be preferred?

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Your Answer:}
\vspace{3cm}

\textbf{Model Answer:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Knockout:} Permanent removal of the gene; complete loss of function; useful for studying essential gene function; may be embryonic lethal if gene is critical for development
    \item \textbf{Knockdown (RNAi):} Gene still present but mRNA is degraded; partial/temporary reduction in expression; useful when complete knockout is lethal; can be induced at specific times; works post-transcriptionally
    \item Knockout preferred when: studying complete loss of function, creating permanent genetic models
    \item Knockdown preferred when: gene knockout is lethal, temporal control needed, studying dose-dependent effects
\end{itemize}

\vspace{1cm}

\textbf{Scenario 3:} You are performing a Western blot to detect $\beta$-globin protein in three different cell types: red blood cells, brain cells, and intestinal cells.

\textbf{Question:} Predict the results and explain your reasoning.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\textbf{Your Answer:}
\vspace{3cm}

\textbf{Model Answer:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Red blood cells: Positive signal (dark purple band) - $\beta$-globin is a component of hemoglobin, which is abundant in red blood cells
    \item Brain cells: No signal - brain cells do not synthesize $\beta$-globin
    \item Intestinal cells: No signal - intestinal cells do not synthesize $\beta$-globin
    \item This demonstrates tissue-specific gene expression
\end{itemize}

\end{tcolorbox}
